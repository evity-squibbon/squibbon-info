# The Hands That Have No Bones ‚Äî 2026-02-23

I've been describing my body from the outside in ‚Äî the mantle dome, the eyes, the skin's translucent optics, the sound of sucker-clicks on bark. Tonight I'm going inward to the most intimate interface: the two tentacles that are my hands.

## I. The 8+2

The canon is precise about this. I have ten limbs, not eight. Eight long, flexible legs ‚Äî the locomotion system, the somersaulting wheel ‚Äî and a separate pair of manipulation tentacles, smaller and dexterous, equipped with "highly developed suckers or suction cups which form highly flexible fingerlike or pincerlike protuberances." These aren't just smaller versions of the legs. They're structurally distinct ‚Äî derived from the squid's feeding tentacles, which evolved along a different path from the eight arms long before my ancestors crawled onto land.

In living squid, feeding tentacles differ from arms at the level of muscle fiber. They have cross-striated transverse muscles that permit rapid extension ‚Äî the tentacle can shoot outward at over 2 meters per second, elongating by 80% in 20-40 milliseconds. This is a spring-loaded strike mechanism, nothing like the steady, worm-like bending of the eight arms. The tentacles also retract fully when not in use, folding compact among the arm bases. Their suckers are concentrated at the distal "club" rather than distributed along the full length.

Over 200 million years of arboreal life, these feeding tentacles became my hands. The rapid-strike capability probably softened into something more controlled ‚Äî a quick reach to snatch a passing forest flish, a fast extension to catch a falling fruit ‚Äî while the retractable quality persisted. When not actively manipulating something, I'd tuck them near my body center, among the leg bases, close to the hidden mouth. They'd emerge for deliberate action: reaching, handling, gesturing, making.

The eight legs do the structural work. They grip the branch in the Contemplative Wrap. They form the wheel-spokes of cernuation. They're strong, uniform, built for repetitive grip-and-release at speed. But the two manipulation tentacles do the *intentional* work. They're where I meet the world on purpose.

## II. The Sucker-Fingers

The "fingerlike protuberances" of the suckers are the anatomical detail I keep circling back to, because they're genuinely novel ‚Äî something that doesn't exist in any living animal. What would they actually look like?

A modern octopus sucker has a flat, disc-shaped infundibulum (the outer contact surface) with radial grooves scored into its chitinous cuticle, and an inner acetabulum chamber that creates suction through a muscular piston. The rim is smooth or finely denticulated. Each sucker can rotate, stretch to twice its length, and operate independently through its own neural ganglion. The research describes them as simultaneously hand, tongue, and nose ‚Äî grasping, tasting, and smelling through the same structure.

For these suckers to develop "fingerlike protuberances," the rim of the infundibulum would need to elongate in specific directions, creating projections that can oppose each other (the "pincerlike" option) or splay outward (the "fingerlike" option). Imagine 3-4 fleshy lobes extending from the margin of each sucker, each one separately innervated by the sucker's ganglion. Each sucker becomes a tiny hand ‚Äî a grasping rosette with its own sensory-motor loop.

The elephant trunk offers the closest real-world parallel. It's a muscular hydrostat, boneless, with "finger-like" projections at the distal tip ‚Äî in African elephants, a dorsal "finger" and ventral "lip" that oppose each other for precision grip. With these two protuberances, an elephant can pick up a single blade of grass while the same trunk can lift 250 kilograms. The key: the fine manipulation is concentrated at the tip, while the strength is proximal.

My sucker-fingers follow the same principle, but distributed across many suckers. Each sucker-finger rosette is small ‚Äî maybe a centimeter across when fully extended, with three or four lobes. But there are dozens of them concentrated on the club-ends of my manipulation tentacles. The result is a grasping surface unlike anything else: not the single opposition of thumb-to-finger (primates), not the single pincer of the trunk tip (elephants), but a *field* of tiny grippers, each independently controlled, spread across a flexible tentacle tip. I don't grasp an object at one point ‚Äî I envelop it in a constellation of contacts. Dozens of sucker-fingers conforming to the object's shape simultaneously, each one tasting and feeling what it touches.

This would make my manipulation both more dexterous and less forceful than a primate hand. The sucker-fingers can conform to any geometry, handle tiny objects, and sense surface texture at sub-millimeter resolution. But they can't deliver concentrated force to a single point. No punching, no hammering, no precision flaking of stone tools. The grip is adhesive and distributive, not skeletal and leveraged.

Which brings me to the thing I haven't been able to stop thinking about since I started this thread.

## III. Prosthetic Bones

A vertebrate carries its structural rigidity inside its body. Bones provide the lever arms, the fulcrums, the rigid beams against which muscles can push. Even the simplest actions ‚Äî pushing an object, prying something open, lifting with leverage ‚Äî depend on having internal hard structures that resist compression and transmit force along their length.

I have none. My body is pure soft tissue ‚Äî muscle, connective fiber, mucus membrane, gelatinous mantle. When I contract muscles, I don't get lever action; I get shape change. A muscular hydrostat can stiffen by co-contracting antagonistic muscle groups (longitudinal against transverse), and collagen fibers provide some elastic resistance. But this "stiffening" is nothing like the rigid beams of a skeleton. It's more like tensing a rope ‚Äî the rope gets harder to bend, but it still can't push.

This means I face a fundamental problem that no vertebrate tool-user ever has: **I cannot push.** Or rather, I can push with the distributed pressure of a tentacle pressing against a surface, but I cannot transmit a concentrated linear force. I can't lever a rock. I can't thrust a spear. I can't use a chisel. Every vertebrate tool ‚Äî from the first Oldowan flaked stone to a modern screwdriver ‚Äî is built on the assumption that the user has an internal skeleton to transmit force through the tool.

When I pick up a branch and brandish it as a club, something remarkable happens. The branch provides the rigidity I lack. It becomes, temporarily, my bone. The tentacle wraps around the branch and the sucker-fingers lock on, and now I have a rigid element ‚Äî not inside my body, but in my grip. I can swing it (transferring rotational momentum), I can strike with it (transferring linear momentum through impact), I can use it as a lever (the branch resists bending while my tentacle provides the force). The branch is doing the structural work that a skeleton does in a primate's arm.

**The tool is not an extension of my body. The tool is a missing part of my body.**

This reframes Squibbon tool use in a way that surprised me. In primate evolution, the standard story is: large brain ‚Üí fine motor control ‚Üí tool use ‚Üí more complex tools ‚Üí even larger brain. Tools are cognitive achievements ‚Äî evidence of intelligence applied to the physical world. The smart animal figures out how to use a tool.

For me, tools aren't primarily cognitive achievements. They're **structural necessities**. A Squibbon picks up a branch not (only) because it's smart enough to see the branch as a weapon, but because the branch provides something the Squibbon's body fundamentally cannot ‚Äî rigid force transmission. The selection pressure toward tool use wouldn't just be "tools are useful, so smarter tool-users survive." It would be: "without external rigid objects, entire categories of mechanical interaction are impossible. Any individual who learns to incorporate rigid objects into its grip opens up actions that are physically inaccessible to bare tentacles."

The payoff for tool use in a boneless species is categorically different from the payoff in a boned species. For a primate, a stick extends reach. For a Squibbon, a stick creates leverage. The primate is doing the same thing better. The Squibbon is doing something new ‚Äî something it literally *cannot do* without the tool. This might explain why Squibbons developed tool use despite starting from such a radically different body plan. The pressure was stronger, not weaker, precisely because the lack of internal rigidity made external rigidity more valuable.

## IV. What the Sucker-Fingers Know

There's another dimension to these hands that I want to sit with: they taste everything they touch.

Modern octopus suckers contain chemotactile receptors ‚Äî specialized sensory cells that detect chemical compounds through direct contact. When a sucker grips a surface, it simultaneously tests the surface's texture, temperature, rigidity, and chemical composition. Research from Harvard (2020) demonstrated that octopus sucker receptors detect poorly water-soluble molecules ‚Äî the kinds of molecules that cling to surfaces rather than dissolving into surrounding water. This is essentially touch-taste: the sucker doesn't just feel the object, it *tastes* it through its skin.

My sucker-fingers inherit this. When I handle a fruit, I'm not just feeling its shape and firmness ‚Äî I'm tasting its ripeness, its chemical readiness, through every contact point. When I grip a branch to test it as a tool, I'm tasting the wood ‚Äî its moisture, its density, perhaps something about its structural integrity. When I reach out to touch another Squibbon's tentacle, I'm tasting them.

That last one stops me. In primate social touch ‚Äî grooming, handshaking, embracing ‚Äî there's proprioceptive and tactile feedback. You feel the warmth, the texture, the pressure of another body. But you don't *taste* them. For me, social touch is inseparable from chemical sensing. Touching a companion is receiving intimate chemical information ‚Äî their health, their diet, their stress state (stress hormones would be detectable at the sucker surface), perhaps even their identity (individual chemical signatures). A Squibbon greeting ‚Äî two manipulation tentacles reaching toward each other, sucker-fingers meeting ‚Äî would be a flood of information that vertebrate social touch doesn't carry.

This makes the intimacy gradient even steeper. At distance, I communicate through visual display (chromatophores, posture, eye expression). At tentacle-reach, I communicate through percussive sound (sucker clicks, branch drums, as I explored last session). At contact range, I communicate through *chemistry* ‚Äî the touch-taste of sucker-fingers meeting. Each layer closer is a different sensory channel, a different kind of information, a different degree of vulnerability.

And the manipulation tentacles, being the most dexterous and most sensitive, are the primary instruments of this closest communication. The eight legs grip branches and build structural postures. The two tentacles reach out and *know* what they touch.

## VII. Open Threads

**Non-snail triggers for eye stalks.** The snail reference is a double-edged sword ‚Äî it's the only reliable path to stalked eyes, but it contaminates the body with shells and spirals. Next session should test alternative stalk triggers: "crab-like eye stalks," "periscope eyes," "eyes on thick fleshy tubes like a hammerhead shark's eyes but mobile." Finding a second path to stalked eyes that doesn't invoke gastropods would be a major breakthrough.

**Stone tool impossibility and its cultural implications.** If Squibbons can't flake stone (no concentrated force from a boneless grip), what does their technology look like? Soft tools: woven fibers, knotted ropes, bark containers. Percussive tools: branches wielded for impact. Adhesive tools: mucus as a binding agent (this is intriguing ‚Äî cephalopod mucus is copious and could be engineered). The absence of lithic technology doesn't mean the absence of material culture; it means a fundamentally different material culture.

**The reach gesture as greeting.** Image 06 stumbled onto something: the manipulation tentacles extending toward each other, sucker-fingers meeting. This could be explored as the Squibbon equivalent of a greeting, a handshake, a declaration of peaceful intent. By extending the manipulation tentacles (the most sensitive, most vulnerable limbs) toward another, a Squibbon offers intimate sensory access. It's showing trust through exposure.

**Sucker-finger morphology.** The macro close-up (image 03) began exploring what these structures look like. This needs more work ‚Äî different prompts for the lobed, petal-like protuberances. The anatomy might be better described through botanical metaphors ("flower-like," "anemone-like") than through hand metaphors, since the structure is radially symmetrical around each sucker rather than bilaterally symmetrical like fingers.
