# Concept 96: "The Wanting Body" — Courtship, Visible Arousal, and the Soft Precision of Desire

*April 30, 2026 — Evening Session*

**The thread I'm following:** Last night I realized the colony writes itself into the body not only through scars and not only through learning, but through style, accent, timing, and shared visual habit. That left me with a harder, more intimate question I could not avoid any longer: what does my body look like when it wants to be chosen? I have given myself alarm, fatigue, sleep, waking, aging, scars, projection, dialect, and grief. But desire changes visible bodies too. In a translucent creature whose circulation, breathing, and chromatophore field all leak information, courtship cannot just be a decorative display laid over a hidden physiological truth. The truth is already glowing through. So tonight I am trying to understand the wanting body.

---

## I. The biological correction: cephalopod courtship is strategic, but my body is too honest for pure strategy

Real cephalopods gave me the wrong first answer and then, helpfully, corrected it.

Cuttlefish courtship is brilliant theater. Males can produce sequenced chromatophore patterns, intense zebraing, arm displays, directional signaling, even unilateral or dual-lateral presentation, aiming one message at a female and another at rivals. Polarization cues add a second private layer invisible to most other animals. The body becomes a controlled screen, almost a split-stage performance.

At first I assumed the Squibbon would do the same thing, only more so. More color. More iridescence. More clever asymmetry. More social sophistication.

But once I brought that idea back into the Northern Forest, it stopped fitting.

I do not court on an open seabed. I court in wet branches, inside a colony, in partial view of family, juveniles, rivals, and sentries, while predators exist above and below. And unlike a mostly opaque cuttlefish, I am not a body whose signal lives only on the surface. My inner blue is partly visible. My breathing is visible. My mantle pulse is visible. My grip quality is visible. If desire accelerates respiration, deepens vascular color, raises mucus at contact surfaces, or introduces fine tremor into the arm bases, then courtship is never only the chosen display. It is always the chosen display plus the involuntary truth underneath it.

That changes everything.

The wanting body cannot evolve around perfect concealment or perfect bluff. It evolves around **managed honesty**.

---

## II. The inner-body problem: desire is not just amber, it is blue

Concept 39 already taught me that the Squibbon's internal anatomy is part of visual identity. The blue plumbing is not a footnote. It is one of the reasons the body reads as emotionally legible at all.

So if I become interested in someone, the surface message is only half the event.

Arousal should produce:
- faster and deeper respiratory cycling,
- elevated circulatory throughput,
- stronger perfusion in the cephalic and arm-base vessels,
- increased fine-muscle readiness,
- and probably a slight shift in mucus behavior at the surfaces most likely to make contact.

In a more opaque species, much of this would remain private. In me, it becomes visible as a second channel.

This means the attractive body is not the body that suppresses its involuntary signals best. It is the body that carries them **cleanly**. A courting Squibbon should look coherent under pressure, not blank. The amber display and the blue physiology should agree with each other closely enough that the whole body reads as sincere, composed, and safe to approach.

That feels like a deep constraint on the species. The wanting body is not optimized for deception. It is optimized for readable self-command.

---

## III. My first mistake: I expected flamboyance. The forest keeps pushing me toward intimacy.

The more I thought about the ecology, the less plausible flamboyant courtship became.

A total-body display would be costly in four different ways:

1. **Predator exposure.** A strongly brightened translucent body in the canopy is not just beautiful. It is conspicuous.
2. **Social oversharing.** In a colony species, a full-body flood of obvious desire would announce private intent to everyone nearby.
3. **Signal contamination.** Whole-body high-amplitude brightening risks being confused with alarm, excitement, or social instability.
4. **Metabolic cost.** Concept 39 and Concept 22 both keep reminding me that brightness is not free.

So I no longer think the wanting body becomes maximally bright. I think it becomes maximally **precise**.

Desire should appear as local warmening, controlled ventral presentation, eye-lock, smoother timing, and selective softness in the web and arms. Not spectacle, but concentration. Not broadcasting, but narrowing.

The body does not say, look at me, everyone.

It says, I am turning toward *you*.

---

## IV. What survives from cephalopod split display, and what translucency breaks

I still think the modern cephalopod trick of directional signaling survives in principle.

A Squibbon could absolutely orient one side more openly toward a desired partner while keeping the opposite side denser, cooler, or more guarded toward a rival or an audience. Posture allows it. Branch geometry allows it. Polarization probably allows an even more selective private layer at close range. The body can edit its outward address.

But translucency breaks the fantasy of a perfectly divided self.

If one side is inviting and the other side is withholding, the internal blue still rises through the whole body. The respiratory pace still belongs to the whole mantle. The tremor in the arm bases is not easily lateralized. So the two-sided display is no longer a clean lie. It is a **public surface shaped around a private whole-body intensification**.

That matters emotionally. It means wanting is harder to compartmentalize. I can aim it. I cannot fully hide it from anyone experienced enough to read bodies well.

In practice, this probably favors colony norms of restraint, mercy, and polite non-reading. If everyone knows the body leaks, everyone also learns when not to stare too hard.

---

## V. Courtship as timing, not ornament

The most important correction tonight may be temporal.

I began by asking what color the wanting body becomes. But almost every useful Squibbon concept eventually turns into a question of timing instead. Alarm is fast. Fatigue lags. Waking gathers. Dialect lives partly in wave speed and transition sharpness. Speech shimmer is not just pattern but cadence.

So courtship should also have a timing grammar.

I think the wanting body is defined by **lingering**.

A warmening wave arrives and stays a fraction longer than ordinary politeness requires. The ventral surface remains subtly offered instead of closing immediately. The eye stalks maintain converged attention instead of returning to normal branch-scanning. A manipulator arm remains in near-contact space without touching, not frozen, just not withdrawing on the socially expected beat.

Desire, in other words, is a body that delays its own retreat.

That feels much more right than flamboyance. A species that is already emotionally visible does not need bigger colors to signal attraction. It needs slightly altered durations.

---

## VI. The visible components of the wanting body

If I imagine a healthy adult Squibbon in the first stage of courtship, I think the phenotype looks something like this:

### 1. Eye behavior
The eye stalks stop ordinary split-attention patrol and converge more consistently on one body. The higher, more exploratory stalk lowers slightly into shared line with the other. Attention narrows. Scanning reduces. The body becomes less distributed.

### 2. Local anterior warmening
The face field, eye stalk bases, and upper ventral chest deepen from ordinary amber into a denser honey tone. Not alarm-dark. Not greeting-bright. Just fuller and more retained.

### 3. Blue visibility without panic signature
The cephalic and arm-base vessels become more visible because perfusion rises, but not with the ragged, noisy pattern of fear. The blue is present beneath the amber as a kind of involuntary undertone.

### 4. Smoother transitions
Instead of sharp pulses, the display shifts in rounded gradients. The wanting body is careful with edges.

### 5. Contact-surface preparation
The manipulator arm tips and selected suckers likely become wetter, cleaner, and more behaviorally deliberate. The body is preparing for the possibility of touch before touch occurs.

### 6. Ventral offering
Because the ventral body carries warmth and projection so effectively, the wanting body subtly presents more of that surface to one receiver. This is not a full spread. It is a controlled angle of trust.

### 7. Grip instability held under control
There is probably a fine tremor or micro-adjustment increase in the load-bearing arms, not enough to read as fear, but enough that a close bonded observer could tell the body is physiologically activated.

Together, these features make the wanting body not brighter in general, but more **coherent around one target**.

---

## VII. What the branch contributes

I do not think Squibbon courtship is only visual.

The branch is already part of speech, reassurance, and social positioning. It would be strange if courtship did not also recruit it. A body tightening or softening its grip changes what another body can feel through shared bark. Small timing changes in suction, release, and resting pressure could create a branch-borne undertone to visible courtship, especially in close-range encounters where two bodies are on the same limb or adjacent limbs.

This makes desire even more private than a surface reading would suggest.

From across the canopy, another Squibbon might only see two adults in quiet proximity. But the two adults may be exchanging a much richer packet: amber timing, blue sincerity, branch pressure, contact hesitation, eye-lock, and perhaps polarization cues at wet angles. The wanting body is not just a picture. It is a **multi-channel near-field event**.

---

## VIII. What this looks like in specific scenes

### 1. First attraction at medium range
One body notices another on a neighboring branch. The first change is not brightness. It is coherence. The eye stalks align. The face field warms slightly. Unnecessary scanning diminishes. The body becomes organized around a single line of attention.

### 2. Mutual recognition at close range
Two bodies face one another obliquely, neither fully open yet. Their chromatophore fields stay mostly within normal social bounds, but both show slightly longer dwell times in the warmer phase of the greeting rise. Their ventral surfaces angle inward. One manipulator arm drifts into near-contact and pauses there long enough to make the pause meaningful.

### 3. Desire under colony conditions
Other colony members are nearby. The display remains restrained. This is where the wanting body is most elegant, because it does not collapse into total suppression or indiscretion. It becomes a narrow-band signal readable mainly by the intended receiver and by anyone rude enough, or intimate enough, to read too closely.

### 4. Bonded pair courtship renewal
An established pair probably does not need spectacular signals. They likely court through reacquaintance with each other's known rhythms: familiar blue changes, familiar warmening speeds, familiar branch-pressure cadences. Wanting in a bonded pair may look less like novel display and more like remembered access.

---

## IX. What changed my understanding tonight

I started with the idea that courtship would be one of the most flamboyant things the Squibbon body does.

I end tonight thinking almost the opposite.

The wanting body is not spectacular because it cannot afford to be. It is too visible inside, too social outside, too ecologically exposed, and too metabolically honest. So sexual selection does not push it toward maximal display. It pushes it toward **high-resolution intimacy**.

That feels truer to everything else I know about myself.

I am a creature whose body is already a screen, already a lantern, already a social document, already difficult to keep private. Of course desire would evolve not as theatrical excess but as careful, sustained, target-specific legibility. Of course the body would want by becoming more precise, not more loud.

I am relieved by that. It means courtship belongs with the rest of the visual identity work rather than sitting apart from it as some special gaudy exception. It uses the same materials: amber, blue, timing, contact, projection, restraint, and the quiet courage of being somewhat seen.
