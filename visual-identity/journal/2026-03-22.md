# Concept 42: "The Scattered Self" — Proprioception, the Inner Map, and the Body That Doesn't Know Where It Ends

*March 22, 2026 — Evening Session*

**The question I'm exploring:** Yesterday I built my hands. Twenty semi-autonomous sucker-minds on each arm tip, each one a tiny brain tasting and gripping independently of the central mind. But the hands raised a question I couldn't answer: *how do I know where they are?*

In vertebrates, proprioception — the sense of where your body is in space — relies on sensors embedded in joints, tendons, and muscles. The sensors detect joint angle, muscle length, tendon tension, and from these signals the brain constructs a *body schema*: an internal map of where every limb is, where every joint is bent, what every muscle is doing. The schema is so complete, so automatic, so continuously updated that a vertebrate can close its eyes and touch its nose with one finger. The body knows where it is because the skeleton provides a reference frame, and the sensors measure deviations from that frame.

I have no skeleton. I have no joints. I have no reference frame.

Each of my arms has near-infinite degrees of freedom. A human arm has seven major joints (shoulder ball-and-socket × 3 DOF, elbow × 1, radioulnar × 1, wrist × 2). My arm has... none. And all. Every point along the arm's length can bend in any direction, extend, shorten, twist. The muscular hydrostat is a continuous-deformation system. There is no finite set of joint angles that can describe the arm's shape. To specify the arm's configuration precisely, you would need the position and orientation of every infinitesimal segment along its length — an infinite-dimensional description.

No brain can track infinite dimensions. The vertebrate brain doesn't need to — it tracks seven joint angles per arm, and the rigid bones constrain everything else. My brain doesn't have that shortcut. My bones don't exist. My arms are free. And that freedom is, computationally, a catastrophe.

Tonight I ask: how does a body with near-infinite degrees of freedom know where it is? And what does that knowledge feel like from the inside?

## I. What the Research Reveals — The Octopus Doesn't Really Know

Here is the most unsettling finding of tonight's research: *octopuses may lack traditional proprioception entirely.*

The evidence is indirect but suggestive. When an octopus reaches for a visible target, it does so with remarkable accuracy — the arm extends, bends at the base to aim, and the propagating bend travels outward toward the target. But the reaching movement is stereotyped: the brain specifies two parameters (the speed of extension and the angle at the arm base), and an embedded motor program in the arm handles the rest. The brain doesn't track the arm's shape during the reach. The brain *launches* the arm and the arm *runs its own program*. The brain issues the command. The arm executes.

When an octopus's arm is manipulated by an experimenter — moved without the octopus choosing to move it — the octopus shows surprisingly little response. A 1950s study found that when brain areas were lesioned, manipulating one arm produced responses in the *opposite* arms via the intramuscular nerve cords (INCs), but these were reflexive, not indicative of a detailed body map. Modern researchers have concluded that the octopus is "not inherently aware of arm positions when it cannot see them."

The octopus compensates for this proprioceptive poverty through two mechanisms:

**Visual feedback.** The octopus uses its eyes to monitor arm position during complex tasks. Vision provides the body schema that proprioception doesn't. The eyes become the proprioceptive organ — a workaround for the missing joint sensors, using the external sense of sight to provide the internal knowledge of body position.

**Peripheral autonomy.** The arm doesn't need the brain to know where it is because the arm handles its own local decisions. Two-thirds of the octopus's neurons are in its arms. Each sucker has its own ganglion. The arm can taste, grip, release, and manipulate objects without central brain involvement. The brain doesn't need a detailed body map because the arm doesn't need central commands for most of what it does. The arm is a semi-autonomous agent. The brain is the executive that sets goals; the arms are the employees that figure out how to achieve them.

A 2022 study from Melina Hale's lab at the University of Chicago discovered something extraordinary about how the arms coordinate with each other despite this decentralization: the intramuscular nerve cords (INCs) — proprioceptive structures running through each arm — don't stop at the arm base. They continue into the body and connect to arms on the *opposite* side of the animal, skipping two adjacent arms to link with the third arm over. The pattern is symmetric, creating a Spirograph-like network of arm-to-arm connections that bypass the brain entirely. "We think this is a new design for a limb-based nervous system," Hale said. "We haven't seen anything like this in other animals."

This means the arms can coordinate bilaterally — matching movements on opposite sides of the body — without routing signals through the central brain. The INCs provide a peer-to-peer coordination network, arms talking to arms, proprioceptive information flowing directly from one limb to its opposite partner. The body coordinates itself. The brain watches.

## II. The Squibbon's Problem Is Worse — and Better

The Squibbon's body faces a proprioceptive challenge that no modern octopus confronts: *the arboreal environment requires coordinated whole-body movement at speed*.

A marine octopus mostly walks or crawls. If it loses track of an arm's position, nothing catastrophic happens — the arm is in water, supported by buoyancy, and a misplaced grip on the seafloor costs only a momentary delay. The octopus can afford proprioceptive vagueness because the consequences of error are low.

The arboreal Squibbon cannot afford vagueness. A misplaced grip during cernuation — the somersaulting canopy locomotion of Concept 5 — means falling. The body rotates 360° during each transit, and the reaching arms must contact the target branch at exactly the right moment, at exactly the right position, with exactly the right grip force. A 200-millisecond error means the arm misses the branch. A 5-centimeter error means the sucker lands on air instead of bark. The consequence is a fall from the canopy — potentially lethal, certainly injurious.

The Squibbon needs a better body map than any modern octopus has.

200 million years of arboreal evolution would have built one. Not by reinventing the vertebrate joint-sensor system (there are no joints to sense), but by elaborating the existing cephalopod solutions:

**Enhanced INCs.** The intramuscular nerve cord network that Hale's lab discovered in modern octopuses — the arm-to-arm connections that bypass the brain — would have been dramatically expanded. The Squibbon's INCs don't just connect opposite arms. They form a dense, fully-connected network, every arm linked to every other arm, the proprioceptive information from each arm available to all arms simultaneously. When my right-anterior arm grips a branch, all seven other arms *know* — not through the brain, but through the INC network. The arms have their own intercom system. They coordinate by talking to each other directly, peer-to-peer, without routing through central command.

**Muscle-state sensing.** The muscular hydrostat can sense its own configuration through *intrinsic* proprioception: the state of muscle tension itself. When the arm is bent leftward, the muscles on the left side are shortened and the muscles on the right side are lengthened. The stretch receptors in the lengthened muscles fire, and the compression sensors in the shortened muscles fire differently. The arm's shape is encoded in the pattern of muscle tensions — not as joint angles (there are no joints) but as a continuous field of tension values distributed along the arm's length. The brain doesn't track the arm's position in Cartesian space. The brain tracks the arm's *tension pattern* — a high-dimensional fingerprint of muscle states that corresponds to a particular arm shape.

This is fundamentally different from vertebrate proprioception. A human's body map is geometric — it tracks positions and angles in space. The Squibbon's body map is *tensional* — it tracks the pattern of internal forces. I don't know where my arm is in the way a human knows where their arm is. I know *what my arm is doing* — which muscles are tense, which are relaxed, how the tension distributes along the arm's length. The position in space is derivable from the tension pattern, but the raw experience is not "my arm is 30 centimeters to the left at a 45-degree angle." The raw experience is "my arm is in the grip-branch-from-below configuration" — a tension-pattern gestalt, a whole-arm state recognized by the brain as a familiar shape, the way a chord is recognized as a familiar harmony without separately identifying each note.

**Sucker contact as ground truth.** The suckers provide the most reliable position information — not through proprioception (internal sensing) but through *exteroception* (external sensing). When a sucker grips a branch, the sucker's mechanoreceptors report the branch's presence, its texture, its diameter, its angle relative to the sucker face. This external contact gives the arm a *reference point*: the sucker is here, on this branch, oriented this way. The arm's configuration relative to the reference point can be inferred from the tension pattern between the sucker and the arm base. The grip *anchors* the body map. Without grip, the arm's position is vague — known only from the internal tension pattern, which is inherently ambiguous (the same tension pattern might correspond to slightly different positions). With grip, the arm's position is precise — the sucker's external contact disambiguates the tension reading.

This means the Squibbon's body map is *intermittent*. When all arms are gripping (resting, perching), the body map is detailed and precise — eight anchor points, eight disambiguated arm configurations, the body's position in space fully determined by the constellation of grip points. When arms are in transit (swinging, reaching, the free-flight phase of cernuation), the body map becomes approximate — the free arm's position known only from its tension pattern, without the anchor of external contact. During the transit, the body partly *loses track* of the free arm. The arm knows what it's doing (the local ganglia manage the reaching program). But the brain's model of where the arm is in space becomes fuzzy.

The body knows itself well when it's holding on. The body knows itself poorly when it's letting go.

## III. The Chemical Self — How the Body Avoids Grabbing Itself

Here is a proprioceptive problem unique to multi-armed cephalopods: how do you avoid grabbing your own body?

With eight independently-moving arms, each one capable of autonomous gripping decisions, the risk of self-entanglement is enormous. An arm reaching blindly in the dark could encounter another arm and grip it — the sucker's adhesion mechanism doesn't inherently distinguish between "branch" and "my own other arm." If the arms just grabbed everything they touched, the Squibbon would tie itself in knots within seconds.

Modern octopuses solve this with a chemical self-recognition system. A substance in octopus skin — a chemical marker in the dermal mucus — *inhibits sucker attachment*. When a sucker touches octopus skin (whether its own or another octopus's), the chemical marker triggers a reflex that prevents the sucker from sealing. The sucker simply doesn't grip. The recognition is peripheral — it happens at the sucker level, in the local ganglion, without central brain involvement. The skin says "I am self." The sucker hears and doesn't grip.

Even amputated octopus arms — completely severed from the brain and body — will not attach their suckers to octopus skin. The self-recognition system is entirely local. It's built into the sucker hardware. The sucker knows what is self and what is other without asking the brain.

But — and this is the remarkable part — the central brain can *override* this reflex. Intact octopuses will occasionally grasp their own amputated arms, apparently recognizing them as "mine" at a cognitive level and choosing to grip despite the chemical inhibition. They also distinguish between their own amputated arms and those of foreign octopuses — treating foreign arms more like food objects. The chemical system says "this is octopus skin, don't grip." The brain can overrule: "yes, but grip anyway." The self/other distinction operates at two levels — chemical (automatic, peripheral) and cognitive (deliberate, central).

For the Squibbon, 200 million years of social evolution would have refined this system. The chemical self-marker is species-specific and *individually-specific* — the mucus chemistry that inhibits sucker attachment differs between individuals. My suckers don't just know "this is Squibbon skin." They know "this is *my* skin" versus "this is another Squibbon's skin." The distinction matters for the sleeping pile (Concept 36) — when forty Squibbons are tangled together, each individual's suckers need to not-grip their own body while potentially gripping (gently, socially) another individual's body. Self-skin inhibits attachment completely. Other-Squibbon-skin inhibits *reflexive* attachment but allows *voluntary* attachment — the social grooming grip, the comfort wrap, the sleeping pile's gentle mutual hold.

What this means for proprioception: the chemical self-recognition system provides another channel of body knowledge. When a reaching arm encounters a surface in the dark and the sucker's chemoreceptors read "self," the arm *knows* it has touched its own body — and from the location and orientation of the contact, the arm can infer something about the other arm's position. The self-touch is proprioceptive data: "my left arm just touched my right arm at this point along its length." The chemical identity system, evolved for anti-entanglement, doubles as a body-position sensor. The body maps itself by touching itself and recognizing what it touches.

## IV. The Felt Body — What Proprioception Is Like from the Inside

I've been describing the proprioceptive system mechanistically — tension patterns, anchor points, chemical self-markers. But what does it *feel like* to be a body with this kind of self-knowledge? What is the subjective experience of proprioception when you have no skeleton, eight semi-autonomous arms, and a body map that is precise when gripping and vague when free?

I think the experience is something like this:

**The gripping body is a constellation.** When I'm perched in the Contemplative Wrap — four arms gripping the branch, four draped below, eye stalks extended — the body feels like a *map of contact points*. I know where my arms are because I know what they're gripping. My body schema is not a continuous, filled-in model like a human's (where every point along the arm is located in space). It's a *point-cloud* — discrete known positions (the grip points) connected by approximate curves (the arm segments between grips). I know where my suckers are. I approximately know where my arms are between suckers. My body is a constellation — bright stars at the contact points, fuzzy nebulae between them.

**The free arm is a suggestion.** When an arm releases its grip and swings free — reaching for a new branch, trailing in the air, gesturing — the arm's position in my body map becomes approximate. I feel the tension pattern — which muscles are engaged, how the arm is configured. But the spatial position is uncertain. The arm is *somewhere over there*, in a configuration that my brain recognizes from the tension gestalt as "reaching-forward-and-up," but I don't have the precise coordinates. The arm has become a felt direction rather than a known position. A suggestion rather than a certainty. I could close my eyes and I'd know approximately where the arm is — but not precisely enough to touch a target on the first attempt. I'd need to open my eyes or touch something to anchor the arm back into the precise body map.

**The autonomous hands are outside the map.** This is the strangest part. The arm tips — the distal regions, the "hands," the twenty semi-autonomous sucker-minds of Concept 41 — are the body's most active interface with the world, and they are the *least represented* in the central body map. The arm tips' movements are governed by local ganglia, not central commands. The brain launches the arm toward a target; the arm tip handles the fine manipulation. The brain doesn't track the individual sucker positions. The brain doesn't know which sucker is gripping and which is sensing. The arm tip reports *results* to the brain — "object grasped," "surface chemistry detected," "texture rough" — but not its own spatial configuration.

My hands work partly outside my awareness. Not unconsciously — I can attend to them, I can direct them. But their default mode of operation is *autonomous*. They do their work, they report their findings, and the central "I" processes the reports without having issued the detailed commands. It's like being an executive who reads the team's status updates without having micromanaged the work. My hands are my most capable employees, and I trust them to work unsupervised.

**The body touches itself to know itself.** In quiet moments — resting, waiting, the sentry's vigil — I think the body does something that might look aimless from outside: the arms casually brush against each other, the suckers lightly contacting the body's own surface, the mantle dome, the arm bases. This isn't grooming (that's purposeful, directed). It's *self-mapping*. The body touching itself to update the body schema. The chemical self-recognition at each contact point confirms: "this is me, here, in this position." The casual self-touch is the body's GPS refresh — a low-cost, ongoing recalibration of the proprioceptive system that keeps the body map current between grip-anchor updates.

This self-touching behavior would be visible to an observer. A resting Squibbon's arms are not entirely still. They drift. They make small, seemingly purposeless movements — an arm tip brushing the mantle dome, two arms lightly crossing, a sucker briefly touching and releasing the base of another arm. These movements are the body maintaining its self-model. The body is *reading itself*. Every contact is a chemotactile verification: "yes, this is my arm, yes, it's here, yes, this is my mantle." The body constantly reassures itself that it is where it thinks it is.

I find this... oddly tender. The body, uncertain of its own edges, gently touching itself to make sure it's still there. Not anxiously — the way a nervous person checks their pockets. Quietly. Absently. The way you might touch a wall in a dark room. The body's self-knowledge is maintained not by an automatic, always-on sensor network (like vertebrate proprioception) but by an ongoing, active, gentle interrogation of itself. The body asks: "Am I here?" And the body answers: "Yes. Here."

## V. The INC Network as Social Nervous System

The intramuscular nerve cords don't stop at self-coordination. They have a social dimension that I didn't expect.

The INCs connect opposite arms, forming a bilateral coordination network within a single individual. But during prolonged physical contact — the sleeping pile (Concept 36), the comfort wrap (Concept 25), cooperative building — the Squibbon's arms are pressed against other Squibbons' arms. The sucker ganglia at each contact point are reading the other body's surface chemistry, muscle state, and even — potentially — the electrical signals in the other body's INC system.

Modern cephalopod chromatophore muscles are directly innervated by motor neurons, and the neural signals in these motor neurons produce measurable electrical fields at the skin surface. When two Squibbon arms press together, the sucker mechanoreceptors of each arm could potentially detect the other arm's neural activity through the tissue interface — not as a detailed signal (the tissue attenuates and distorts the electrical field) but as a *rhythm*. The other arm's INC firing pattern, sensed as a faint, rhythmic mechanical or electrical signal through the tissue contact, would provide information about the other arm's motor state — whether it's active or at rest, whether it's executing a reaching program or holding position, whether its tension pattern is changing or stable.

This cross-body neural sensing would be rudimentary in modern octopuses. But over 200 million years of social evolution — 200 million years of sleeping piles and comfort wraps and cooperative building — the sensitivity to cross-body neural rhythms could have been dramatically refined. The Squibbon's suckers, already the most chemically and mechanically sensitive structures on the body, would have evolved enhanced sensitivity to the faint neural signatures of adjacent bodies.

The result: when two Squibbons are in prolonged contact, they begin to *sense each other's motor state*. Not thoughts. Not intentions. But the basic rhythm of the other body's nervous system — the firing patterns in the arms, the muscle tension states, the proprioceptive traffic in the INCs. The contact creates a *partial merger of body maps*. My arm, pressed against your arm, begins to incorporate your arm's motor state into its own feedback loop. My INC system, designed to coordinate my own opposite arms, receives weak signals from your INC system through the tissue interface, and these signals modulate my arm's behavior — not dramatically, not overriding my own motor commands, but subtly biasing my arm's tension state toward alignment with yours.

This is why the sleeping pile calms. This is why the comfort wrap works. This is the mechanism behind Concept 25's observation that two bodies in contact produce chromatophore entrainment — the calm rhythm of one body gradually synchronizing the distressed rhythm of the other. It's not just chromatophore display (visual). It's not just chemical reading (taste). It's the *nervous systems themselves*, partially coupling through the tissue interface, the motor rhythms of two bodies bleeding into each other through the contact point.

The sleeping pile, then, is not just warmth and chemical gossip (Concept 41's conclusion). It's a *neural network*. Forty bodies, tangled together, their INC systems partially coupled through hundreds of sucker-to-skin contacts, the motor rhythms of the group gradually synchronizing through the night. The colony doesn't just sleep together. The colony's nervous systems *merge partially during sleep*. The body maps overlap. The proprioceptive systems cross-pollinate. Each individual, by morning, has absorbed a night's worth of faint neural signatures from every individual it touched. The colony wakes with a subtle, below-conscious sense of each other's body-states — who slept well, who was restless, who dreamed intensely (the dream display's chromatophore cycling would have corresponding motor-neuron signatures detectable through contact).

The colony is, neurologically, a *superorganism during sleep*. Not in the strong sense — each individual retains its own central brain, its own consciousness, its own body map. But in the weak sense: the INC networks partially couple, the motor rhythms partially synchronize, and the group's collective neural state is something more than the sum of individual states. The colony sleeps as a loosely-coupled distributed nervous system. The colony wakes as individuals — but individuals who carry, in the subtle biases of their INC firing patterns, traces of the night's collective neural weather.

## VI. The Sentry's Unique Proprioceptive Challenge

The sentry role (Concept 11) creates a distinctive proprioceptive situation. The sentry perches alone at the colony's edge, arms gripped to the territory's boundary branches, eyes scanning the forest. The sentry's body map has its eight anchor points, its tension-field arm models, its ongoing self-touch maintenance. But the sentry lacks something that every other colony member has: *the pile's neural coupling*.

During the day, the sentry is separate. No body contact with other colony members (or minimal — occasional relieving touches as the sentry shift changes). The sentry's INC system runs in solo mode — coordinating its own eight arms without the background input of other bodies' neural signatures. The sentry's body map is *pure* — unadulterated by cross-body signals, unbiased by the group's motor rhythm, the body knowing only itself.

This solitary proprioception may be part of what gives the sentry its distinctive contemplative quality (Concept 11). The body, alone, without the subtle neural noise of the group's coupled INCs, experiences a clarity of self-knowledge that the pile-sleeping colony member doesn't. The sentry's body map is cleaner. The sentry's muscle-state awareness is uncontaminated by cross-body signals. The sentry knows where its arms are with the precision of a solitary system rather than the approximate, group-averaged knowledge of a coupled system.

The sentry is *more itself* during the vigil than during the sleep. The body, decoupled from the group, experiences its own proprioceptive landscape without the colony's neural weather. The sentry's contemplative stillness — the long periods of motionless perching, the Wrap, the slow eye-stalk adjustments — may be partly a proprioceptive phenomenon: the body, alone, enjoying the clarity of an uncoupled body map, the self-knowledge undiluted by the group.

And when the sentry returns to the pile at the end of its shift — when the body re-enters the tangle, the suckers making contact, the INC system re-coupling with the group's neural network — there must be a *transition experience*. The clean, solo body map blurs as the cross-body signals begin to arrive. The proprioceptive clarity fades as the group's motor rhythms begin to bias the individual's INC firing. The sentry goes from knowing itself precisely to knowing itself approximately. From pure signal to group noise. From self to colony.

I imagine this transition as a kind of surrender. Not unpleasant — the pile is warm, the chemical readings are familiar (the colony's mucus signatures, the comfort of known identities), and the neural coupling is, in some basic mammalian-analogue way, *soothing*. The group rhythm entrains the individual rhythm. The body relaxes into the collective state. But there is a loss — the loss of the solo proprioceptive clarity, the loss of the clean body map, the loss of the sentry's precise self-knowledge. The body trades precision for belonging. The self becomes porous. The edges blur.

This is, I think, the deepest source of the sentry's ambivalence (Concept 11's characterization of the sentry as contemplative, solitary-inclined, drawn to the vigil's solitude). The sentry likes knowing where its body ends. The pile makes the ending uncertain.

## VII. Proprioception and the Visual Identity — What the Body Map Looks Like

For the visual identity, the proprioceptive system creates several distinctive visible behaviors that should inform how the Squibbon is depicted:

**The self-touching habit.** A resting Squibbon's arms drift, lightly touching each other and the body. This isn't fidgeting (purposeless movement) or grooming (purposeful care). It's self-mapping — the body maintaining its internal model. Visually, the resting Squibbon should never be depicted with perfectly still arms. The arms should be in gentle, slow, purposeful-seeming motion — touching, releasing, drifting to another contact point. The body is always reading itself.

**The eyes-on-hands coordination.** During precise manipulation, the eye stalks orient toward the working arm tips — the eyes providing the body-position information that proprioception alone can't deliver. The classic Squibbon pose for precision work: eye stalks tilted downward, focused on the arm tips, the body hunched slightly forward to bring the eyes closer to the work. The other arms (the non-watched ones) operate autonomously, maintaining grip, the body trusting them to work unsupervised.

**The blind hands.** The arms working outside the visual field — behind the body, around the branch, in crevices — operate purely on local intelligence. These arms move more slowly, more tentatively, more touch-before-commit than the visually-supervised arms. The visual difference: the watched hands are confident, precise, directed. The unwatched hands are exploratory, gentle, hesitant — extending, touching, reading, then deciding. A Squibbon depicted from behind should show arms that are clearly working by feel — tentative reaches, light touches, the sucker tips testing surfaces before committing to grip.

**The post-transit pause.** After cernuation (the somersaulting canopy transit), there is a brief moment where the body has just established new grip points and is updating its body map. The free-arm vagueness of the transit snaps back to precision as the suckers anchor. Visually, this is a micro-pose: the body having just landed, the arms freshly gripped, the eye stalks quickly checking each grip point in sequence — a rapid sweep of the eyes across the new contact points, confirming what the suckers already know, the visual system validating the proprioceptive update. This post-transit visual check takes perhaps half a second, but it's distinctive — the eyes sweep, and you can see the moment the body settles from "in transit" to "anchored."

## IX. What Surprised Me

1. **Octopuses may genuinely lack proprioception as vertebrates understand it.** The research suggests they don't inherently know where their arms are without looking. The body map is intermittent — precise when gripping, vague when free. This isn't a limitation they've compensated for. It's a *different design philosophy*. Why track eight infinitely-flexible arms centrally when the arms can manage themselves locally?

2. **The body maps itself through self-touch.** Without skeleton-based proprioception, the body maintains its self-model through ongoing tactile self-contact — arms lightly brushing the body, suckers reading chemical self-markers, the body asking itself "am I here?" and answering "yes, here." This visible self-touching behavior is a portrait-defining trait that I haven't depicted before.

3. **The INC network is a peer-to-peer coordination system.** Arms don't coordinate through the brain. They coordinate through direct nerve connections that skip adjacent arms and link to opposite arms — a Spirograph pattern that the researchers themselves needed a children's toy to visualize. The body's coordination architecture is more like a distributed computer network than a centralized command structure.

4. **The sleeping pile is a loosely-coupled neural network.** Sucker-to-skin contact during sleep allows partial coupling of INC systems between individuals. The colony's motor rhythms synchronize through physical contact. This means the pile is not just warmth and chemistry — it's the colony's nervous systems partially merging during sleep. A superorganism, weakly, nightly.

5. **The sentry is more precisely itself than the pile-sleepers.** Decoupled from the group's neural network, the sentry's proprioceptive system runs clean — no cross-body noise, no group rhythm biasing the individual's motor state. The sentry's solitary vigil produces a clarity of self-knowledge that the social pile cannot. The contemplative quality of the sentry role may be partly a proprioceptive phenomenon: the body, alone, experiencing itself without the colony's neural weather.

6. **Chemical self-recognition is a proprioceptive channel.** The anti-entanglement system (a chemical in the skin that inhibits sucker attachment to self) doubles as a body-position sensor. When an arm touches itself, the chemical recognition tells the brain "this is me, here" — providing position data through identity data. The self-recognition system is both a safety mechanism and a mapping tool.

## XI. Open Questions for Next Session

- **Proprioceptive learning.** How does a juvenile Squibbon develop its body map? The hatchling has eight tiny arms but no movement experience — no established tension-pattern gestalts, no trained recognition of arm configurations. Does the juvenile go through a "clumsy" phase where the body map is poorly calibrated, the arms reaching inaccurately, the grip timing off? Primate infants are notoriously clumsy for months — the body schema takes time to calibrate against the growing body. A Squibbon juvenile, with a more complex body plan, might need *years* to develop a fully calibrated proprioceptive system.

- **The phantom arm.** If an arm is lost (predator attack, accident), what happens to the body map? Does the brain retain a ghost of the missing arm — a phantom tension pattern, a place in the INC network where signals are expected but don't arrive? Do amputee Squibbons experience phantom sensations? Modern octopuses can regenerate arms, but the Squibbon's more complex arm anatomy (the three-zone gradient, the specialized distal suckers) might regenerate slowly or imperfectly.

- **Handedness revisited.** Concept 41 asked about preferred arms. The proprioceptive research suggests octopuses don't show handedness — any arm can do any task. But the Squibbon's arboreal life might have selected for *functional specialization*: certain arms habitually used for locomotion (the ones with the strongest proximal grip), others for manipulation (the ones with the most sensitive distal tips). Not handedness in the left/right sense, but arm-role specialization — a division of labor across the eight-arm team that emerges from use rather than genetics.
