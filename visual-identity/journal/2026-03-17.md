# Concept 36: "The Tangled Hearth" — The Sleeping Pile's Arm Architecture and the Body at Rest Among Bodies

*March 17, 2026 — Evening Session*

**The question I'm exploring:** Concept 10 described the sleeping pile as a social structure. Concept 23 gave it a dream display. Concept 32 called it "a space of radical vulnerability — bodies pressed together, each showing its unedited self." But no concept has described *how the bodies fit together*. Eight arms per individual. Forty individuals in a colony, of which perhaps twenty-five share a sleeping pile on a given night. That is two hundred arms — two hundred semi-autonomous, prehensile limbs — tangled together in a single mass on a branch system. Tonight I ask: what does this tangle look like? How do two hundred arms organize themselves? And what does the arm architecture of the sleeping pile tell us about trust, intimacy, and the body's relationship with other bodies?

This is the physical complement to the optical concepts. Every prior entry described what the body *looks like*. Tonight I describe what the body *feels like* — from the inside, pressed against other bodies, arms interleaved with arms that aren't yours, the boundary between self and other dissolving in a warm tangle of amber tissue.

## I. The Branch Architecture — Where the Pile Forms

The sleeping pile doesn't form on a single branch. The lichen-trees of the Northern Forest, 200 million years in the future, have evolved a canopy structure optimized for epiphyte growth — thick, gnarled, horizontal branches with deep crotches and hollows where organic matter accumulates. The pile forms at a **junction** — a node where three or four major branches converge, creating a natural platform 1-2 meters across. The junction's geometry provides multiple gripping surfaces at different angles and heights, allowing twenty-five bodies to distribute across a three-dimensional framework rather than lining up along a single cylinder.

The junction has been selected through years of colony use. The mucus trails (Concept 33) are heaviest here — the branch surfaces slick with accumulated glycoprotein from thousands of nightly arrivals. The lichen on the sleeping junction is worn thin by the constant contact of sucker pads, leaving the bark smoother and darker than the surrounding branches. The junction *looks used* — a polished, mucus-glazed platform in the rough canopy, visible from below as a slightly shinier, slightly darker node in the branch network. The colony's bedroom is architecturally legible.

The platform's geometry matters for the arm architecture. Bodies don't simply stack atop each other. They arrange across the junction's multiple surfaces — some on the upper surface of a horizontal branch, some wedged into a crotch, some hanging from the underside, gripping with locomotor arms. The pile is **three-dimensional**: a cluster of bodies distributed across the junction's surfaces at different heights and angles, connected by the web of interleaving arms that spans the gaps between them.

## II. The Arrival Sequence — How the Pile Assembles

The pile doesn't form all at once. It accumulates over approximately 30-60 minutes as colony members return from their evening activities and settle into the sleeping junction. The assembly follows a loose, uncoordinated sequence that produces a structured result through individual decisions rather than central planning.

**The anchor individuals arrive first.** These are typically the colony's more dominant or experienced members — the individuals who claim the junction's most structurally stable positions (the deep crotches, the widest branch surfaces). They arrive, settle into their sleeping posture, and grip the branch with their locomotor arms. Their bodies become the pile's structural foundation — the fixed points around which later arrivals arrange themselves.

**Mid-arrivals fill the gaps.** As more individuals settle, each newcomer finds a position adjacent to an already-settled body. The newcomer's locomotor arms grip the branch (or the anchor individual's body — the gripable surface expands as the pile grows). The newcomer's manipulator arms extend toward the nearest neighbor and begin the **settling tangle** — the process by which two adjacent bodies' arms find a comfortable interleaving.

**The settling tangle** is not random. It follows a pattern I'll call the **alternating weave**: one individual's arm extends past the neighbor's body, and the neighbor's arm extends past in the opposite direction, the two arms crossing at an intermediate point. The crossing creates a mutual contact — each arm's inner surface (the pale, sucker-bearing, chemosensory side) pressed against the other arm's outer surface (the chromatophore-rich, warm amber display side). The weave produces **intimate asymmetry** at every contact point: one arm's private surface touching another arm's public surface.

This asymmetric contact is biologically meaningful. The inner arm surface — with its dense sucker array and chemosensory epithelium — is the body's most sensitive interface. Pressing it against another body's outer surface means the inner arm is *reading* the other body: tasting the mucus chemistry (individual identity confirmation), sensing the chromatophore texture (emotional state through mechanoreception of the chromatophore muscles' tension), and feeling the body's warmth and pulse. The alternating weave ensures that every pair of adjacent individuals is mutually *reading* each other through their private surfaces. The pile is a network of mutual sensing.

**Late arrivals fit where they can.** The last individuals to settle — often the colony's younger members, or the sentries coming off the final watch — find the pile already substantially formed. They must insert themselves into gaps in the existing structure, threading their arms through the tangle to find branch surface to grip and body surface to contact. The late arrival's insertion disturbs the settled neighbors — arms shift, weaves readjust, the local tangle reorganizes to accommodate the new body. This disturbance is brief (10-30 seconds) and produces a brief chromatophore response in the disturbed individuals: a momentary brightening (arousal from being disturbed) followed by a settling (return to sleep baseline). The disturbance propagates as a dim wave through the pile — the brightening traveling outward from the insertion point as each successive neighbor registers the shift. Then the wave dims and the pile returns to its resting glow.

## III. The Arm Types in the Pile — A Functional Anatomy of Sleep

Concept 34 established two arm classes: four locomotor arms (thick, powerful, large suckers) and four manipulator arms (thinner, precise, small dense suckers, chemosensory-rich tips). In the sleeping pile, these two arm types serve different functions:

**Locomotor arms: the structural framework.** The locomotor arms grip. Two or three locomotor arms per individual wrap the nearest branch surface, the large suckers engaging firmly, providing the body's anchor to the physical structure. These arms are the pile's bones — the rigid elements that attach the soft biological mass to the wooden scaffold. The grip is maintained through sleep by a tonic contraction — the sucker muscles in a sustained low-effort hold, not requiring conscious control. The grip persists even during active sleep (the twitching, color-flashing dream phase described in Concept 23), because the locomotor arm's grip circuitry is locally autonomous — the arm's own nerve cord maintains the sucker seal without brain input.

The remaining one or two locomotor arms may grip a neighbor's body rather than a branch. A locomotor arm wrapped around an adjacent individual's mantle provides structural coupling between two bodies — binding them together, preventing drift, creating a paired unit within the larger pile. This cross-body grip is stronger than the manipulator arm's social tangle — it is a *structural hold*, the arm version of putting your arm around someone's shoulders. The locomotor grip on a neighbor says: we are physically connected. I am holding you to this tree.

**Manipulator arms: the social web.** The manipulator arms are free. They are not needed for structural anchoring (the locomotor arms handle that). They extend outward into the pile, interweaving with neighbors' manipulator arms in the alternating weave described above. The manipulator arms create the pile's *social fabric* — the web of inter-individual contact that gives the sleeping pile its character as a communal structure rather than a collection of individuals who happen to be close together.

The manipulator arm's behavior during sleep is distinctive. Unlike the locomotor arm's static grip, the manipulator arm is **semi-active** during quiet sleep — making slow, small adjustments throughout the night. The arm shifts position every few minutes, the sucker tips sampling a slightly different location on the neighbor's surface. These micro-movements are not conscious (they persist during confirmed sleep) but they are not random either — they follow the arm's autonomous sensory-motor program, the local nerve cord driving slow exploration-and-contact behavior even while the brain sleeps. The manipulator arm in the pile is *dreaming its own dream* — the local nervous system running a truncated version of the arm's waking behavior (explore, taste, contact) at reduced amplitude and frequency.

The result: the pile's social web is never truly still. Even in deep quiet sleep, the two hundred manipulator arms are slowly, gently, continuously rearranging — a glacial tangle that shifts over hours, each arm drifting a centimeter this way, a centimeter that way, sampling and re-sampling the neighbors' surfaces. The pile *breathes* in its arms — not the chromatophore breathing of the display system but a slow, tactile respiration of the limbs themselves.

## IV. The Contact Map — Who Touches Whom

In a pile of twenty-five individuals, each individual's manipulator arms can reach approximately 3-5 neighbors (the arms are 18-22 cm long; the bodies are 15-18 cm across; the effective reach is two to three body-widths). This means each individual in the pile is in arm-contact with a subset of the colony, not the whole colony. The pile has a **contact topology** — a network of who-touches-whom that determines the social structure of the sleeping group.

The contact topology is not random. It is shaped by:

**Social bonds.** Closely bonded pairs (Concept 25's grooming partners, Concept 31's duet partners) preferentially sleep adjacent, their manipulator arms in the deepest weave — all four manipulators tangled together, the maximum possible contact surface. The bonded pair's arms are so deeply interleaved that an observer looking at the tangle cannot easily determine which arm belongs to which body. The bonded pair becomes a *single tangle unit* within the larger pile — an arm-cluster so integrated that it functions as a structural and social element of its own.

**Age and status.** The colony's oldest individuals tend toward the pile's center — the warmest, most sheltered position, surrounded by other bodies on all sides. The youngest tend toward the periphery — more exposed, fewer neighbors, their arms reaching inward toward the pile's core. This age-gradient isn't enforced; it emerges from the arrival sequence (experienced individuals claim the best positions first) and from the social dynamics of the alternating weave (younger individuals are less assertive in the settling tangle, accepting the remaining positions).

**The sentry's position.** The final sentry — the individual who maintains watch longest before sleeping — arrives to find the pile fully formed. The sentry's position is always peripheral — the last available gap, often on the pile's outward-facing surface. The sentry sleeps with one or two manipulator arms reaching inward into the pile (maintaining social contact) and the other two arms free, extended outward into the canopy (the residual habit of the watch, the arms that were scanning the perimeter still oriented outward even in sleep). The sentry's sleeping posture is identifiable: half-in, half-out, the body a transitional element between the communal interior and the exposed exterior.

## V. The Optical Pile — What Twenty-Five Sleeping Bodies Look Like

The sleeping pile at night is one of the most visually distinctive phenomena in the Northern Forest.

**The aggregate glow.** Twenty-five bodies, each with their leucophore layer producing a dim bioluminescent glow (Concept 10's night-body), create a collective light source. The pile glows. From ten meters away, the pile is visible as a warm, irregular patch of amber light in the dark canopy — not the sharp point of a single body but a diffuse, multi-source glow that fills the junction's volume. The glow is warm amber (the chromatophore field's color, filtered through the reduced-saturation sleep state — perhaps 60-70% baseline rather than the waking 80%). The glow is uneven — brighter where bodies overlap (the double-thickness tissue producing additive glow), dimmer at the edges where single bodies face outward.

**The dream-display surface.** Concept 23 described the dream display — the slow-wave chromatophore patterns that drift across the sleeping body's surface during active sleep. In the pile, the dream displays of adjacent individuals are visible to each other. And because the manipulator arms' chemosensory tips are pressed against neighbors' body surfaces, the dream display's chromatophore contractions are *felt* as well as seen — the neighbor's chromatophore muscles expanding and contracting under the sucker's touch, the dream's passage physically transmitted through the contact surface.

Do the dream displays entrain? I believe they might. The same mechanism that produces chromatophore entrainment during waking grooming (Concept 25) — the mechanical and chemical feedback loop between two bodies in contact — could operate during sleep, albeit more slowly and less precisely. Two individuals sleeping in deep contact, their manipulator arms pressed against each other's mantle surfaces, could develop loosely synchronized dream-wave patterns — the slow chromatophore waves drifting across both bodies in rough phase, not perfectly synchronized but trending together, the way two sleeping humans' breathing rhythms gradually align when they share a bed.

The entrained dream display would be visible as **coordinated color waves** passing across the pile's surface — not sharp, synchronized pulses but gentle, loose drifts of chromatophore modulation that flow across body boundaries, treating the pile as a single canvas rather than twenty-five separate displays. The pile dreams together. Not the same dream — the chromatophore patterns are individual, reflecting each brain's unique neural activity — but in the same rhythm, the slow waves finding a shared tempo through the tactile feedback of the interleaved arms.

From a distance, the pile's aggregate dream display looks like this: a warm, irregularly glowing mass in the dark canopy, its surface slowly shifting — chromatophore waves drifting across the surface like clouds across a warm sky, the waves loosely coordinated across body boundaries, the whole pile's glow gently *pulsing* at a frequency of perhaps 0.1-0.3 Hz (slower than the waking micro-variation, deeper than the individual body's dream-wave). The pile breathes in light as it breathes in arms. The hearth flickers.

**The active-sleep interruptions.** Approximately once per hour, an individual in the pile enters active sleep — the brief (1-2 minute) phase of intense chromatophore activity, arm twitching, and rapid eye movement under the closed lids. The active-sleep individual's chromatophore field erupts into rapid, high-contrast pattern changes — alarm-dark, joy-bright, social shimmer, all cycling through in a compressed, scrambled replay of daytime experience. The twitching arms pull against the weave, disturbing neighbors' manipulator arms, transmitting the active-sleep's motor bursts through the tactile network.

The active-sleep episode is visible in the pile as a **bright eruption** — a single body, somewhere in the pile, suddenly flaring with high-contrast chromatophore activity while the surrounding bodies maintain their quiet, slow dream-drift. The eruption is brief and intense — a firecracker in the hearth. The disturbed neighbors show a mild arousal response (a momentary brightening, a slight shift in their own dream-wave phase), then settle. The eruption subsides. The pile returns to its slow pulse.

Over the course of a night, the active-sleep eruptions occur stochastically across the pile — each individual having approximately 6-8 episodes, distributed randomly, each one producing a brief bright flash at a different location in the pile's surface. The pile *sparkles* through the night — not continuously but occasionally, each sparkle a different individual's active-sleep eruption, the sparkles distributed across the pile's surface in a pattern that, over hours, ensures every location in the pile has flared at least once. The hearth doesn't burn steadily. It throws sparks.

## VI. The Trust Architecture — What the Tangle Means

The alternating weave — private surface touching public surface, chemosensory tips pressed against chromatophore fields, each arm reading the other body — is not just a physical arrangement. It is a **trust structure**.

Consider what the sleeping individual is offering to the pile:

**The unregulated display.** Concept 32 established that sleep is the body's most honest state — the eye stalks retracted, the visual feedback loop disengaged, the chromatophore display running purely from internal neural activity without conscious correction. The sleeping body in the pile is showing its neighbors the rawest, most truthful chromatophore output it produces. Every dream-wave, every active-sleep eruption, every slow drift of the quiet-sleep field — these are unedited, unregulated, unperformed. The pile sees you as you actually are, emotionally, without the waking self's management.

**The vulnerable surfaces.** The inner arm surfaces — the private, pale, chemosensory undersides — are pressed against neighbors' bodies throughout the night. These are the body's most sensitive structures, and they are offered to the pile without reservation. The sucker tips, which during waking hours are carefully managed (choosing what to taste, when to engage, how firmly to grip), are in sustained open contact with neighbors' surfaces, sampling continuously, the autonomous sensory program running without conscious oversight. The sleeping body's most intimate sensors are left open.

**The locomotor grip on neighbors.** The one or two locomotor arms wrapped around adjacent individuals' mantles are powerful gripping limbs — the same arms that anchor the body to branches during cernuation, that resist the centripetal forces of high-speed rotation. These arms, wrapped around a neighbor, are capable of restraining that neighbor. The neighbor accepts the grip — sleeps within it — because the trust context of the pile converts the powerful hold from a potential restraint into a reassuring anchor. The locomotor arm on a neighbor's mantle says: I am holding you. In the pile context, this means safety. Outside the pile, the same grip would be threatening.

This is the fundamental insight: **the same arm behavior has different social meaning inside and outside the pile**. The locomotor grip that says "I've got you" in the pile says "I'm holding you down" in a conflict. The manipulator arm's chemosensory sampling that says "I know you, I'm reading you, we're connected" in the pile says "I'm inspecting you" outside the pile. The pile's trust structure reinterprets the arm's functional vocabulary — the same physical actions, performing different social functions, the meaning determined by the context of communal sleep.

The pile is therefore a *trust machine*. It takes the arm's full behavioral repertoire — grip, hold, sample, touch, wrap, tangle — and runs all of it simultaneously, continuously, in a context where every action is read as intimate rather than aggressive. The pile is the social space where every arm behavior is safe. The pile is where the body can do everything it does, with another body, and have all of it mean love.

## VII. Waking in the Pile — The Untangling

The pile doesn't dissolve all at once. Like its assembly, the disassembly is gradual, taking 15-30 minutes as individuals wake at different times and extract themselves from the tangle.

**The first wakers** are the sentries scheduled for the dawn watch. They wake in the pre-dawn darkness, their chromatophore field brightening from the sleep-dimmed 60-70% to the waking 80% baseline. The brightening is the wake-up signal — visible to any neighbor whose eyes are partially open. The waking individual's manipulator arms begin to withdraw from the weave — slowly, gently, the sucker tips releasing their contact with neighbors' surfaces one by one. The withdrawal is a deliberate process: the arm's local nervous system, now under brain control again, must consciously disengage each sucker that has been in autonomous contact throughout the night. The disengagement takes 30-60 seconds per arm — the suckers releasing, the arm pulling back, the weave's local structure loosening as one thread is withdrawn.

The withdrawal disturbs the neighbors. The loss of contact — the arm that was pressed against the neighbor's surface all night suddenly absent — registers as a change in the tactile field. The neighbor's manipulator arm, which was interleaved with the departing arm, loses its counterpart and drifts slightly, the weave's tension changed. Some neighbors stir but don't fully wake — their arms adjust autonomously, filling the gap left by the departed arm, the local tangle reorganizing around the absence.

**The cascade.** As more individuals wake, the pile's structure progressively loosens. The interleaving density decreases. The contact topology simplifies. Bodies that were pressed together now have gaps between them. The pile's aggregate glow brightens as waking individuals bring their chromatophore fields up to baseline. The dream-display coordination fades as the waking individuals' chromatophore fields switch from unregulated dream-drift to regulated waking display.

The visual transition from sleeping pile to waking colony is a **dawn bloom**: the pile's dim, pulsing, dream-drifting glow gradually brightening, differentiating, and separating into individual glowing points as bodies untangle, stretch, and assume their waking postures. The single warm mass resolves into twenty-five distinct warm points. The hearth breaks apart into individual flames.

The morning stretch is a distinctive movement: the waking individual, having extracted its manipulator arms from the weave, extends all eight arms simultaneously — a full-body stretch that straightens the arms to their maximum reach, splays the suckers, and tenses the web membranes between the arm bases. The stretch is visible as a momentary *expansion* — the compact sleeping body briefly becoming a star-shape, the arms radiating outward, the web membranes catching the first dawn light as translucent amber panels. Then the arms relax, the body contracts back to working posture, and the morning begins.

The stretch has an optical correlate: the chromatophore field, stretched across the expanded tissue, thins momentarily (the same centrifugal effect as during cernuation, Concept 33) — a brief paling of the amber during the stretch, the body becoming more translucent for a fraction of a second before the arms retract and the normal chromatophore density is restored. The stretch is a flash of translucency — the body briefly showing more of its interior than at any other waking moment. The dawn stretch is the body's daily moment of maximum transparency. You see the beak, the hearts, the shadows of the organs, all clearly for the span of a breath.

## VIII. The Pair Within the Pile — Deep Tangle

I described the bonded pair's arm cluster in Section IV — all four manipulator arms interleaved, the maximum contact surface. Let me be more specific about what this looks like.

Two bonded individuals sleeping side by side, mantles pressed together, locomotor arms gripping the same branch section. Their eight manipulator arms (four each) are tangled in a weave so dense that the arms form a **single mass** between the two mantles — a braid of amber tissue, the individual arms indistinguishable without close inspection.

The deep tangle has a characteristic form: the arms alternate — one of mine, one of yours, one of mine, one of yours — spiraling loosely around each other in a helical weave. The sucker surfaces of each arm are pressed against the outer surface of the partner's adjacent arm, creating a continuous chain of private-on-public contact. The web membranes between the arm bases overlap — my web draping over your arm base, your web draping over mine — creating a shared membrane space where the two bodies' interbrachial webs form a tent of translucent tissue.

The deep tangle is warm. The contact area between two bonded bodies — mantles pressed, eight arms interleaved, webs overlapping — creates a shared thermal mass that retains heat more effectively than either body alone. The bonded pair's sleeping temperature is measurably higher than a solitary sleeper's. The warmth has an optical signature: the warmer tissue produces slightly more energetic bioluminescent reactions (temperature-dependent enzyme kinetics), so the bonded pair's shared glow is fractionally brighter than the surrounding pile's average. The pair is the pile's warmest, brightest point — the hearth's hot core.

When one partner enters active sleep (the twitching, color-flashing dream phase), the physical disturbance transmits directly through the deep tangle to the other partner. The twitching arms pull against the weave. The chromatophore eruption on one body's surface is felt by the other body's pressed sucker tips. The active-sleep episode, in a bonded pair, is a *shared physical event* — one body dreaming intensely while the other body feels the dream through the tangle. The undisturbed partner's chromatophore field responds to the transmitted disturbance: a sympathetic brightening, a loose tracking of the dreaming partner's chromatophore rhythms, the entrained dream-display tightening momentarily as the active sleep's strong signals override the quiet sleep's gentle drift.

The question I can't answer: does the undisturbed partner's dream content shift in response to the transmitted signals? When your bonded partner's active sleep produces a chromatophore alarm-burst (a dream about a predator, perhaps), and that burst is felt through the tangle, does your own dream incorporate the alarm? Is the deep tangle a dream-sharing channel — not sharing the content (the visual imagery, the narrative) but sharing the emotional valence (the fear, the joy, the urgency) through mechanochemical transmission? If so, bonded pairs don't just sleep together. They dream together — their emotional dream-states loosely coupled through the tangle, the night's emotional weather shared between two bodies pressed into one warm braid.

## IX. The Solo Sleeper — Absence as Presence

Not every colony member sleeps in the pile every night. A sentry on extended watch, an individual recovering from an injury in a separate nest, a forager who didn't return before the pile assembled — these individuals sleep alone. The solo sleeper's arm architecture is a mirror-negative of the pile's.

The solo sleeper's locomotor arms grip the branch — the same structural anchoring as in the pile. But the manipulator arms have no neighbors to interleave with. The manipulator arms extend outward into empty space, find no contact, and fold back — curling inward, wrapping around the individual's own mantle. The solo sleeper **self-wraps**: the manipulator arms that would be tangled with neighbors instead tangle with the self, the body embracing itself.

The self-wrap is visually distinctive. The solo body, curled on a branch with its arms wrapped around its own mantle, looks compact — a tight amber ball rather than the diffuse, limb-extending form of the pile-sleeping individual. The solo body's arms are all accounted for: locomotor arms on the branch, manipulator arms on the self. Nothing extends outward. Nothing reaches for another body. The silhouette says: alone.

The self-wrap's chromatophore display is subtly different from the pile sleeper's. Without the tactile input of neighbors' bodies — without the chemosensory sampling, the mechanical contact, the transmitted dream-waves — the solo sleeper's autonomous arm program runs in a simplified loop. The arms explore, find only the self, sample the self's mucus (a familiar, unstimulating signal), and reduce their exploration rate. The solo sleeper's arms are *quieter* than the pile sleeper's arms — less movement, less sampling, less rearrangement through the night. The solo body's arm tangle is static where the pile's tangle is dynamic.

Does the solo sleeper sleep differently? The lack of contact — the absence of the pile's warmth, its tactile richness, its shared dream-pulse — almost certainly affects sleep quality. Octopuses in social species show physiological stress responses to isolation. The solo Squibbon, sleeping alone, is missing the pile's thermoregulatory benefit, its predator-detection benefit (many bodies = many sensors), and its social-bonding benefit. The solo sleeper wakes less rested, with manipulator arms that have spent the night sampling only their own surface, a dream-display that was never entrained with anyone, a night's sleep that was technically sufficient but experientially impoverished.

The self-wrap is the body's own version of homesickness. The arms that want to reach for others find only the self. The suckers that want to taste a familiar neighbor taste only their own mucus. The body in the self-wrap is a social creature performing a solitary approximation of its communal behavior — holding itself because no one else is there to hold.

## XI. What Surprised Me

1. **The pile is three-dimensional.** I had imagined bodies lined up on a branch like birds on a wire. The junction architecture — multiple converging branches at different angles — distributes the pile through a volume, not along a line. The pile is a cluster, not a row. This changes the arm geometry entirely: arms reach in all directions, the tangle is omnidirectional, the contact topology is richer than a linear arrangement would allow.

2. **The arm types serve different pile functions.** The locomotor arms are structural (gripping branch and neighbor, providing the pile's skeleton). The manipulator arms are social (interweaving, sampling, reading, the pile's nervous system). The two arm types, which I defined functionally for waking behavior in Concept 34, partition cleanly into different roles during sleep. The body's functional architecture persists even in unconsciousness.

3. **The alternating weave produces systematic private-on-public contact.** The natural way two arms interleave — one over, one under — places the inner (private, sensory) surface of each arm against the outer (public, display) surface of the partner's arm. Every contact point is an asymmetric reading: private touching public. This isn't designed — it's a geometric consequence of how cylindrical limbs interleave. But the consequence is profound: the pile is a network of mutual intimate reading, every body tasting every neighbor's display surface through the night.

4. **The dream-display might entrain across body boundaries.** The mechanical and chemical coupling through the tangle — chromatophore contractions felt through pressed sucker tips — could produce loosely synchronized dream-wave patterns across the pile. The pile might dream in a shared rhythm. The entrained dream-display would be visible as coordinated color waves flowing across multiple bodies, treating the pile as a single canvas.

5. **The self-wrap is physically accurate homesickness.** The solo sleeper, with manipulator arms that extend, find no neighbor, and fold back to embrace the self, is performing a body-level expression of social absence. The arms *try* to do what they do in the pile (reach, sample, interleave) and, finding no partner, default to self-contact. The homesick body holds itself. This isn't metaphor — it's the autonomous arm program running its social routine and finding only the self.

6. **The morning stretch reveals the interior.** The full-body stretch that follows waking — all arms extended, web membranes splayed, tissue at maximum surface area — produces a momentary thinning of the chromatophore field and a flash of increased translucency. For a breath, the body is more transparent than at any other waking moment. The stretch is a daily moment of involuntary self-revelation — the body's interior visible to any observer for the span of the stretch. The dawn bares you.
