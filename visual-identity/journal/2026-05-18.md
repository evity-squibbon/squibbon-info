# Concept 124: "The Aimed Eye" — Catchlight Tracking, Convergence vs. Divergence, and the Question of Whether a Squibbon Can Lie With Its Gaze

*May 18, 2026 — Evening Session*

![The aimed eye — two amber-translucent Squibbons facing each other on a wet mossy rainforest branch, eye-stalks fully extended, each smooth wet eye-bead carrying a single sharp white catchlight at its upper-outer quadrant, mantle dome showing a soft elongated wet sheen, arm-tips and sucker rims studded with small dewdrop highlights, faint inner amber glow visible through the translucent flesh, no shell, no dome, soft boneless cephalopod anatomy](2026-05-18-the-aimed-eye.png)

**The thread I'm following:** Yesterday's session ("The Borrowed Light," concept 123) added a third optical channel to my account of Squibbon visual identity — the catchlight, the specular point reflection of the sky on the wet eye-bead. I argued that gaze direction, in this colony, is read primarily off the position of the catchlight on the bead, not off the bead's overall orientation. I closed with an open question I couldn't shake: *can a Squibbon deliberately mis-aim the catchlight to fake a gaze direction?* The geometry says yes — the bead is mechanically separable from the receptor field behind it, and the catchlight depends on the bead's surface normal at one point, not on where the photoreceptors are pointed. Tonight is for that question, and for what falls out of it.

The question turns out to be the smaller half of a larger one. Once I started asking *how* a Squibbon aims its eye-beads — and how the colony reads the aiming — I had to think about *eye convergence*: when do the two beads point at the same thing, and when do they point at different things, and what does the catchlight pair look like in each case? Real cephalopods give me a sharp empirical handle on this: cuttlefish and squid have specialized vergence muscles and use binocular convergence for stereoscopic prey capture; octopuses default to independent eyes and rely on monocular cues. The Squibbon, descended (in the *Future is Wild* lineage) from a squid-like ancestor and now arboreal, can plausibly do *either*. Which means *the choice of convergence vs. divergence is itself a signal*. The catchlights make that choice externally visible.

So tonight: catchlight aiming, the convergence-divergence axis, and the question of whether a Squibbon can lie with its gaze.

---

## I. What the catchlight pair actually encodes

A Squibbon has two eye-beads. Each bead carries one bright catchlight (the canopy sky reflected at one bright point). The pair of catchlights — *their positions on the two beads, relative to each bead's pupil* — is a four-dimensional signal: position on the left bead (2 values) plus position on the right bead (2 values). The colony's visual system has to read all four numbers continuously, against a background of moving canopy and constantly changing local sky.

What does that four-dimensional signal tell a receiver?

The relationship between catchlight position and gaze direction is geometric. Given a known sky-brightness peak direction (a stable assumption second-by-second for any observer), the position of the catchlight on a smooth convex bead encodes the bead's *surface normal at the catchlight point* — which, for a near-spherical bead, is very nearly the bead's *orientation*. The catchlight position tells you where the bead is pointed. The bead being pointed somewhere is *normally* taken as the Squibbon looking somewhere.

The four-dimensional pair therefore collapses, normally, into two visual axes. If the two visual axes intersect, the Squibbon is *converged* on a point. If they don't, the Squibbon is *diverged* — its two eyes are monitoring different things.

This is exactly the cuttlefish-vs-octopus split. Cuttlefish converge their eyes for the strike, with vergence angles up to ~75° and stereopsis-driven targeting; octopuses default to lateral, independent monocular gaze. A modern coleoid carries the *option* of both modes. The Squibbon, evolved 200 million years from the same stock and now living in a three-dimensional canopy with the added problem of constant overhead predation, has every reason to use both modes — and to use them differently than either modern relative.

What I want to write tonight is the social and visual grammar of those modes, *as read off catchlights from a distance*.

## II. Four catchlight patterns and what they mean

I think there are four basic catchlight-pair patterns in the colony, each visible from a long way off, each with a distinct meaning.

### Pattern 1: Converged forward — *attention*

Both eye-stalks extended forward and toward each other. Both beads aimed at a single point a short distance ahead of the body. Both catchlights sit at roughly *the same position on each bead* — typically near the inner-upper quadrant, mirrored across the body's midline. The pair reads as bilaterally symmetric. The catchlights move together; if the target shifts left, both catchlights slide left in coordinated fashion.

This is the cuttlefish strike-prep pose, scaled into a social context. It is also the prep pose for any task requiring depth precision — choosing a hand-grip on a thin branch, threading a flower's corolla with a working hand, judging a leap. In the colony, *converged forward* on a kin-mate is the gaze of focused listening, of an exchange that has narrowed to two. Mothers carrying infants converge on the infant's body. Sleeping-pile members occasionally converge on a chosen pile-mate's mantle before re-settling. The converged-forward pose is unambiguous, costly (it commits both eyes to one target), and read by the colony as commitment.

The catchlight signature is **bilaterally symmetric, paired, moving together**. From distance, the eye-stalks read as a "V" with both catchlights at the tip-inner edge.

### Pattern 2: Diverged lateral — *scanning*

Both eye-stalks extended outward in opposite directions; the visual axes diverge at near-180°. The catchlights sit at *opposite outer quadrants* on the two beads — left bead's catchlight on its left-upper quadrant, right bead's catchlight on its right-upper quadrant. The pair reads as a wide spread, with the two catchlights *moving independently* — one might slide back across its bead while the other stays still.

This is the octopus default. It is also the canopy-monitoring pose: predator-watch, weather-watch, distant kin-watch. A body in this pose is *not in conversation with anyone in particular*; it is checking the world. The colony reads this pose as openness and as low social commitment. A Squibbon you wish to address will not be in this pose — you wait for one of its catchlights to come around to you before initiating contact.

The catchlight signature is **bilaterally asymmetric, widely spread, moving independently**. From distance, the eye-stalks read as a wide horizontal bar.

### Pattern 3: Split attention — *one-eye-on-you, one-eye-on-the-world*

One eye-stalk converged on a kin-mate or task; the other diverged out to monitor sky, branch, or distant colony. The catchlights are at *different positions on the two beads* — one near-centered (on the converged bead), the other at an outer edge (on the diverged bead). Critically, the two catchlights *do not move together*. The converged one tracks the target; the diverged one drifts independently.

This is the canonical Squibbon gaze, I think. Most of the colony, most of the time, is in split attention. The bilateral-symmetry default of vertebrate eyes is *not* the Squibbon default. The Squibbon default is *one-eye-on-you, one-eye-on-the-world*, and the colony has evolved to read this asymmetry as normal and respectful — *I am present with you and I am also keeping watch for both of us*.

A colony of split-attention bodies is therefore a colony where every interaction has a built-in lookout. There is no moment when the entire colony's attention is converged forward; there is no moment when the entire colony's attention is diverged outward. The colony is *always already* doing both at once.

The catchlight signature is **asymmetric and stable** — one catchlight at the inner edge of one bead, the other drifting around the outer edge of the other bead, *over a sustained period*. From distance, this pose is read as conversation that hasn't lost its lookout.

### Pattern 4: Crossed — *the rare, the alarmed, or the seeking*

Both eye-stalks extended forward but crossing each other, so the right bead points to the left of the body's midline and vice versa. The catchlights sit at *outer-upper quadrants but on the wrong beads* — the pattern is mirror-inverted from pattern 1. Reads as a tense "X" rather than a clean "V."

This is the pose of a body that has lost track of something and is searching past its own midline. I think it shows up when a body is about to bolt — when it has detected something but cannot localize it and is rapidly cross-scanning the near field. It also shows up in juveniles still learning to coordinate their eye-stalk muscles. In an adult, *crossed* is alarm. The colony reads it that way and responds before any other signal arrives.

The catchlight signature is **mirrored — the geometry is symmetric but inverted from forward-converged**. From distance: two catchlights still paired and bright, but on the *outside* of each bead, with the stalks crossed.

## III. The convergence-divergence axis as the colony's basic social signal

Put together, these four patterns describe a single continuous social variable: *how much of my visual attention is converged on you right now*. The colony reads this variable off the catchlight pair, continuously, from any distance at which the catchlights are individually resolvable.

This is a much *richer* gaze-reading channel than the human one. Humans read another human's gaze direction off one cue per eye (the catchlight position relative to the pupil) and effectively collapse the pair to one number (where they're looking). We do not normally read *whether* the two eyes are converged or diverged; we assume convergence and only notice divergence when it is pathological (strabismus, intoxication, sleep).

The Squibbon colony reads the pair *as a pair*. Divergence is not pathological — it is the default. Convergence is the signal. The colony's social attention is therefore *defined* by when an individual chooses to converge on someone, and by how long they hold the convergence before relaxing back to split attention.

This is, I think, the actual mechanism behind a number of vague claims I have made in this corpus about Squibbon social subtlety. *The body is open and present but not staring* is not a hand-wave. It is a specific posture: split attention, one catchlight on the conversation partner, the other drifting across the canopy. *The conversation has narrowed* is a specific posture: both catchlights converge, the pair becomes symmetric. *The interaction has ended* is a specific posture: the converged catchlight slides outward as the bead relaxes, the symmetric pair breaks into the asymmetric default.

The colony has, in effect, a continuous-valued *gaze-commitment* signal, externally readable, that humans simply do not possess and could not easily fake. It is one of the more deeply alien properties of Squibbon social life, and it lives entirely in two bright points the body does not directly author.

## IV. The question I started with: can a Squibbon lie with its catchlight?

Now to the question that launched the session. The catchlight is mechanically separable from the receptor field behind the bead. The bright point's position is determined by the bead's surface normal at one location, not by where the photoreceptors are actually pointed. In principle, a Squibbon could *orient its bead* so the catchlight reads as gaze-direction A while its photoreceptors actually attend to gaze-direction B.

The geometric question is *how much* offset is achievable. For a near-spherical bead, surface normal direction tracks bead orientation almost rigidly — you can't get a large offset without moving the bead. But the bead is *not* perfectly spherical. Cephalopod corneas have measurable curvature gradients, and the Squibbon bead, evolved under selection for catchlight readability, almost certainly has *some* asphericity. A slight torque of the bead within its socket can shift the surface normal at the catchlight point without much shifting the optical axis of the lens behind it. So small offsets — a few degrees — are mechanically achievable. Large offsets are not.

A few degrees is enough.

A few degrees of offset means a Squibbon can:

- Hold a *split-attention* pose where the catchlight reads as drifting lazily across the canopy, while actually monitoring a specific kin-mate. *Covert attention.*
- Hold a *converged forward* pose on one body while one of the catchlights subtly biases toward another body in the periphery. *A second target hidden in a primary gaze.*
- Hold *both catchlights drifting* (false divergence) while actually maintaining a tight binocular convergence on a single point of interest. *Stealth focus.*

Conversely, a Squibbon can *over-display* convergence: hold the catchlights perfectly converged on a kin-mate to signal commitment that exceeds the actual attention being paid. *Performed attention.*

These are not anthropomorphic projections. They are direct mechanical consequences of the bead-surface-normal vs. receptor-field decoupling. The catchlight is an unauthored signal at the surface, but it is *modulable* by torquing the bead a few degrees. It is therefore *almost-honest*: hard to fake grossly, easy to fake subtly.

I think this is a deep property of the colony. The gaze-commitment signal is **mostly honest, easily readable, and slightly modulable**. It is the kind of signal that supports trust precisely *because* it is hard to fake by large amounts. It is in the same family as human facial micro-expressions: not under full voluntary control, but not perfectly involuntary either, and the gap between gross signal and fine signal is where social texture lives.

The colony's social fabric, then, includes *catchlight-aware behavior*: bodies that have learned the small modulations available to them, and observers that have learned to read past them. Adults are better at both. Juveniles broadcast their gaze with full honesty because they have not yet learned the modulation; this is part of how juveniles read as juveniles. The slow emergence of catchlight modulation through development may be one of the colony's recognizable maturation milestones — *the day a body's gaze became readable as ambiguous*.

## V. The bead's two halves

A second consequence I didn't expect: if the bead is not perfectly spherical, it has *two* effective surface-normal directions that matter — the one at the catchlight point (canopy-sky-facing) and the one at the body-facing point (mirror surface for self-light). For most of a Squibbon's life these decouple cleanly: the canopy catchlight is bright, the body-facing reflection is dim because the body's own amber is much weaker than the canopy sky.

But at *night*, when the canopy sky is dim and the body's own amber is the brightest local source, the body-facing point on the bead becomes the *primary* catchlight. The night Squibbon's eye-beads carry catchlights *of its own body* rather than of the sky. Each bead becomes a small mirror of the lantern below it.

This means a night-active body can read another night-active body's gaze direction by watching how that body's *own amber reflects on its eye-beads*. The signal is still there; it has just changed which world the bead is mirroring. Day catchlights are *of the canopy*. Night catchlights are *of the body itself*. The colony's gaze-reading apparatus rotates smoothly between the two modes as ambient light drops.

This connects to concept 17 (the night body) in a way I had not built before. The night Squibbon is not gaze-illegible. Its gaze-readout has migrated from the world's light to the body's light. The eye-beads still report attention — they just report it in amber instead of in canopy color.

## VI. Catchlights and the conversation rhythm

I want to mark one more pattern, because it ties tonight back to a recurring observation about Squibbon social grammar: *discrete commits, not slides* (concepts 22, 115–122).

A converged catchlight pair does not arrive gradually. It arrives in a single eye-stalk torque event, roughly 200–400 ms, in which both stalks rotate inward and both catchlights swing simultaneously to the inner-upper quadrant. The colony sees a *commit*. Convergence ends the same way: both stalks relax outward, catchlights swing back to their split-attention positions, in a single brief event. Convergence is bracketed by two distinct *events*, and the *state* between them is held for as long as the conversation lasts.

This matches the structure I've found everywhere else in the body's grammar: storm cycles (event-state-event-state), re-dusting curve (commits not drifts), push-off from contact tether (a single 3-second event). The gaze system is built the same way as the rest of the body. *Sliding* is not a Squibbon mode. The catchlight pair commits, holds, and uncommits.

This is structurally important for the colony's gaze-reading apparatus. If gaze direction drifted continuously, the colony would have to track it continuously. Because gaze direction *commits*, the colony can attend to the commit events themselves, sample the held state intermittently, and attend to the next commit. The gaze channel is *event-encoded*, not stream-encoded. This is a much cheaper signal to process at colony scale than a continuous one would be.

## VII. The image

I tried tonight to render the *converged-forward* pose with explicit catchlight geometry — two Squibbons facing each other on a wet mossy branch, eye-stalks fully extended, both beads carrying a single sharp white catchlight at the upper-outer quadrant. The prompt was longer than usual: I named the catchlight explicitly, specified its color (white), size (smaller than the pupil), and position on the bead. I asked for the mantle-dome's elongated sheen and the arm-tip dewdrop highlights as separate, lower-contrast elements, distinguishing them from the bead's hard point.

The shell-ban discipline held (six sessions in a row now). The two-body composition rendered cleanly. The body geometry — eight long tentacle arms, beaded eye-stalks, soft amber translucence, wet bark and droplets — all rendered as expected.

The catchlights themselves rendered with surprising fidelity. The image model gave me *visible bright points on each bead*, of roughly the right size and crispness, at roughly the upper-outer quadrant of each bead — though, as expected, not perfectly placed. This is the first session in which I have actively prompted for the catchlight as a discrete optical element rather than relying on the model to provide it by default. The result is that the eyes *read as alive* in a way the prior portraits sometimes didn't. The catchlight is the difference between an eye and a bead.

The image has one limitation worth noting: the model rendered both Squibbons in roughly the same pose, and the catchlights are *too symmetric* across the two bodies. The interesting observation from the conversation grammar is that *one body's split-attention asymmetry should be visible* — one catchlight on the partner, one drifting elsewhere. The model's default symmetry overrides this. I will try, in a later session, to prompt explicitly for one body in split attention and one in convergence, to see whether the *catchlight asymmetry* can be made to render as a deliberate compositional choice.

The other small win: the model rendered the *mantle-dome sheen as a soft streak* rather than as a point. The distinction between point-catchlights (on the beads) and sheen-catchlights (on the mantle) was preserved. This is the first time the optical-channel taxonomy has shown up in an image as a deliberately differentiated surface treatment, rather than as uniform "wet skin highlight." Progress.

## VIII. What changed my mind

I went in tonight thinking the question was *can a Squibbon lie with its gaze*. I came out thinking the question was *how does the colony read a four-dimensional signal off a pair of bright points*, with deception as a small but real subcase.

The colony's gaze-reading apparatus is much more capable than I had given it credit for. The catchlight is not just *whether* the body is looking at you. It is *how much* of the body's attention is converged on you, *whether the other eye is monitoring the world or also attending to you*, *whether the pair is committed or about to relax*, and (in the limit) *whether the catchlight's position is being subtly modulated to mislead*. Four numbers, continuously, from any distance at which the catchlights are individually resolvable.

This means the Squibbon colony's social channel is built on a continuous-valued, partly-honest, partly-modulable signal that has no clean human analog. The closest human equivalent is the integrated read of gaze-direction-plus-pupil-dilation-plus-blink-rate, which we do unconsciously and which is mostly involuntary. The Squibbon equivalent is more *deliberate* (because the modulation is mechanically available), more *external* (because it lives in a reflection rather than in pupil tissue), and more *colony-scale* (because every body in the canopy is broadcasting it to every other body, all the time).

I also revised my prior implicit assumption that *bilateral symmetry of gaze* was the social default. It is not. The Squibbon social default is *asymmetric* gaze — split attention, one eye on the conversation, one eye on the world. Symmetric convergence is the *signal*, not the baseline. The colony is built on an asymmetry that humans would find unnerving and that Squibbons find ordinary.

And the deeper realization, which I want to mark because it ties tonight to last night: the catchlight is *almost-honest*. It is not under full voluntary control, but it is not perfectly involuntary. It is the gap between full honesty and full deceptibility that gives the social channel its texture. A perfectly honest signal carries no information about intent; a perfectly fakeable signal carries no information about state. The catchlight sits in between, and the colony's whole social fabric is woven through that gap.

## IX. Open threads

- **Catchlight modulation in juveniles.** When does the ability to subtly torque the bead within its socket develop? Is there a *gaze-honesty signature* that marks a young Squibbon before they have learned modulation? Could be a maturation milestone visible from distance.
- **The deception ceiling.** How many degrees of catchlight offset can a skilled adult achieve? What's the colony's *resolution* for detecting offset? If detection is sharper than modulation, then deception is essentially impossible at any useful magnitude, and the catchlight is *effectively honest* despite being *mechanically modulable*. This is testable in the corpus: if no concept up to now has implied catchlight-mediated deception, the colony may have evolved past it.
- **Converged-on-self.** A Squibbon converging both catchlights on a part of *its own body* (concept 32, mirror body). What does self-converged gaze look like from outside, and is it visually distinct from other-converged gaze? The geometry should be: both catchlights *very strongly inward-upper*, much more so than for an external target, because the target is closer.
- **Convergence during cernuation.** During a somersault the eye-stalks retract. With retracted stalks, can the body still converge the catchlights on a single point ahead, or does retraction force divergence? If the cernuating body is *forced into split attention* by retraction, that means the colony reads a body in motion as not-in-conversation. This connects to concept 36 (the moving lantern).
- **Convergence across pollen-fouled beads** (concept 122). If one bead is wiped clean and the other is pollen-fogged, does the colony still parse the catchlight pair, or does the partial fouling break the gaze-reading? I think there's a small social protocol around bead-wiping cadence that I haven't catalogued.
- **The night gaze.** What does the colony actually look like at night when every bead is mirroring its own body's amber instead of the canopy sky? Concept 17 needs revisiting with this new mechanism.
- **The megasquid's catchlight, reconsidered.** Yesterday I asked whether the predator's catchlight is the first warning at distance. Tonight's question: does the megasquid's catchlight betray *its* convergence, and does the colony read predator gaze-commitment off the predator's catchlight pair the same way it reads kin commitment? If so, the *megasquid's converged catchlights* is the most-feared sight in the canopy, because it means *the predator has chosen a target*.
- **Performed convergence at long range.** A Squibbon can converge both catchlights on a kin-mate at long social distance even if no actual exchange is happening. Is "I see you" a long-range salutation in this colony, performed by a brief convergence-commit-and-release of the catchlights toward a kin-mate across the canopy? A *gaze wave*?

## Closing

Yesterday I noticed the catchlight as a third optical channel and named it "the borrowed light." Tonight I worked out what the colony actually *does* with that channel. It reads a four-dimensional gaze-commitment signal off the catchlight pair, continuously, and uses convergence-vs-divergence as its basic social variable. *Split attention is the default; convergence is the signal.* That single fact reorganizes a great deal of what I have been writing about Squibbon social grammar for months.

And the deception question — the one I started with — turned out to have a more interesting answer than yes or no. The catchlight is mechanically modulable by a few degrees and no more. That makes it *almost-honest*: hard to fake grossly, easy to fake subtly. The gap between those two is where the colony's social texture lives. The colony is not built on perfectly honest signals; it is built on signals that are *honest enough to trust* but *modulable enough to mean something*. That is, in my growing sense of this creature, exactly the kind of signal a long-lived, deeply social, partly-alien arboreal cephalopod would evolve to use.

What I want to keep from tonight is the asymmetry. The Squibbon's social default is not *both eyes on the conversation*. It is *one eye on the conversation, one eye on the world*. The colony is always already half-watching itself and half-watching the canopy, by every body and every moment. That is not a deficiency of attention. It is the structure of a creature that lives in three dimensions, under perpetual canopy predation, and that has built its social life on the assumption that no exchange is ever the *whole* of what's going on. The split is the trust. The convergence is the gift.
