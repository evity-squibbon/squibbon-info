# Concept 79: "The Sick Body" — Illness, Skin Chemistry Divergence, and the Colony's Visible Triage

*April 20, 2026 — Evening Session*

**The thread I'm following:** Last night's concept — the groomed body — left me with a specific open question. If the Squibbon's mucus coat is communal infrastructure, and if grooming exchanges microbiota among colony members until a shared skin chemistry emerges, then a sick individual's skin chemistry must *diverge* from the colony baseline. Which means grooming is not only hygiene and bonding. It is a continuous, distributed health assay. Every grooming contact is a tactile-chemical read of another body's surface state. The colony knows when one of its members is unwell not by watching for illness but simply by touching it during the evening's routine care.

Tonight I want to look at what illness looks like in a Squibbon body. Not the spectacular catastrophes — those are Concept 27 territory, the megasquid encounter, acute injury, body in crisis. I mean the slow, quiet sicknesses. Bacterial skin infections. Systemic infection. Mild parasitism. The kinds of illness that a cephalopod in a perpetually wet forest, living in dense social contact with conspecifics and sharing a mucus microbiome across hundreds of daily grooming bouts, would inevitably encounter. Tonight's question is: when the Squibbon's body is *slightly wrong*, how does it look? And what does the colony see, and do?

---

## I. Why an arboreal cephalopod must be sick sometimes

Modern octopuses in the wild suffer bacterial skin disease. *Vibrio lentus*, a common marine pathogen, reproduces in skin lesions, colonizes internal organs, and kills otherwise healthy wild octopuses. Pathogenic *Vibrio*, *Photobacterium*, *Pseudomonas*, *Aeromonas*, *Cytophaga*, *Flavobacterium*, and *Klebsiella* species have all been isolated from diseased octopus skin lesions and hemolymph. Wild-captured octopuses carry higher loads of these Gammaproteobacteria than aquaculture animals kept in clean water. The skin microbiome composition is itself a candidate health biomarker: shifts in the bacterial community precede the clinical signs of dermatitis.

Translate this forward 200 million years into a terrestrial arboreal lineage living in perpetual rain, and the microbial pressure does not disappear. It changes character. The Squibbon is no longer surrounded by seawater rich in marine Vibrio species. It is surrounded by rainforest air, wet bark, lichen, epiphytes, the mucus of other Squibbons, the shared sleeping pile, the shared nest, and a forest floor of fungi and soil bacteria far beneath. The skin microbiome the Squibbon maintains is a terrestrial forest microbiome — probably Pseudomonadota-dominant, with significant Actinobacteriota and fungal components, and with pathogens drawn from the terrestrial rather than marine community.

But the vulnerability is structurally the same. A soft-bodied animal with a mucus-coated, translucent skin, living in constant dampness and constant social contact, will sometimes develop bacterial or fungal skin infections. The mucus that protects the body is also a culture medium. The grooming that maintains the coat also spreads whatever lives in it. The translucent body, so good at broadcasting emotion, has no opaque cuticle to hide infection beneath. Any microbial disturbance that alters tissue, secretion, or chromatophore tone will be immediately visible from the outside.

This is a species that cannot hide being sick.

## II. The optical signs of a sick Squibbon

I want to assemble the visible signs carefully, because this is one of the places where the Squibbon's communicative transparency has its sharpest consequence.

**The mucus coat loses clarity first.** A healthy mucus film is thin, even, glossy. In early illness — before the animal is systemically unwell — the mucus production shifts. It becomes slightly thicker, slightly more opaque, slightly more viscous. The amber gloss dulls in a way that looks superficially like isolation-dulling (Concept 78) but is subtly different. Isolation dulling is patchy, concentrated at blind zones, restored by a single grooming pass. Illness dulling is *uniform* and *returns* — a groomer can smooth the coat back to clarity, but within hours the coat thickens again. This is one of the earliest tactile cues a groomer gets: the mucus feels wrong, and it reasserts its wrongness after cleaning.

**The chromatophore field loses saturation.** The amber palette depends on energy. The chromatophore muscles are metabolically expensive (Concept 73, The Price of Amber). A body fighting infection is diverting metabolic resources — fever, immune response, wound defense — and the first system to throttle down is display. A sick Squibbon's amber runs cooler, paler, less saturated. The warm channel goes watery. The contrast between chromatophore-extended and chromatophore-contracted zones flattens. The star map is still there, technically readable, but it reads *softly*, at reduced gain, like a signal being transmitted at half power.

**The iridophore shimmer dulls.** Under the chromatophore layer, the iridophore field provides the cool-channel structural color that gives the healthy body its depth (Concept 28, The Hidden Palette). Iridophore function depends on the ultrastructure of the reflectin stacks and on the hydration state of the intervening cytoplasm. Dehydration, fever, and altered extracellular fluid composition all disturb the iridophore layer's optical spacing. The shimmer flattens. The body loses the secondary cool glint that a healthy body shows when caught by angled light. It looks more matte. Less jewel-like.

**Eye stalk carriage sags.** The eye stalks are muscular hydrostats kept in their erect resting posture by continuous low-level tone. A sick body cannot sustain this tone at full amplitude. The stalks carry lower, tip slightly, droop by perhaps ten or fifteen degrees from their healthy resting angle. Combined with a subtle retraction of the head into the web line, the overall silhouette changes — the head "collapses" into the body. This is not the full retraction of cernuation or alarm. It is a quiet, continuous carriage adjustment that the colony reads at a glance.

**Respiration changes.** Concept 63 established the breathing body as a visible rhythm — the mantle cavity's slow ventilation pulse as baseline proof of life. In illness, respiration shifts. Rate up, amplitude variable. Sometimes irregular. The mantle rhythm loses its characteristic slow-steady drumbeat and becomes a little ragged. A sick Squibbon sitting still shows a different breathing cadence than a well Squibbon sitting still, visible from a body-length away.

**The sucker rim changes color.** The suckers are highly vascularized. Systemic illness shifts the perfusion state — sometimes blanching the sucker rims slightly, sometimes reddening them, sometimes introducing a mottled unevenness. This is a non-communicative signal: the suckers were not evolved to tell anyone anything. But in a body where the outer surface is also the diagnostic surface, the suckers become involuntary disease indicators.

**And posture slumps.** Not to the drape of rest (Concept 60, The Draped Body) and not to the sleeping pile curl. A specific sick-posture: arms pulled closer in, body closer to the branch, manipulator arm curled to the chest rather than extended and working. Primates show the same cross-species sickness behavior — hunched posture, reduced movement, increased resting. The Squibbon's version is a tightening of the body's spatial footprint. The healthy Squibbon occupies space confidently, arms extended, eye stalks high. The sick Squibbon makes itself smaller.

Taken together these signs produce a body that a colony member can identify as unwell at a glance. None of the signs is dramatic. All of them together are unmistakable. In a species that communicates constantly through its body, illness is a *syntactic disruption*. The sentence of the body is still being spoken, but with slurred edges and a lowered voice.

## III. What the groomer feels

The optical signs are the easy half. The harder, more interesting half is what the colony feels during routine grooming.

Last night I described the five-step grooming cycle: scan, lift, transfer, wet, smooth. The scan step reads surface texture through the sucker rim. In a well body, the scan returns a specific, well-known signature — particular mucus viscosity, particular skin tone, particular chromatophore reactivity to local touch, particular warmth. Over a lifetime of grooming contact, each Squibbon builds up a vast tactile-chemical library of what every body in its colony feels like when healthy.

Illness disrupts this signature.

The mucus is wrong — wrong viscosity, wrong slip, wrong chemistry (mucus microbiome shifts toward pathogen-associated species; the tactile signature of *Vibrio*-analog bloom is perhaps a slight tackiness, perhaps a slight bitterness to the mucus chemoreceptors on the sucker rim). The skin temperature is wrong — usually warmer, because fever is an ancient immune strategy conserved across many phyla and there is no reason a cephalopod mesotherm could not run a mild fever when infected. The chromatophore response to touch is wrong — a healthy chromatophore field pulses warm under the groomer's contact in an immediate, crisp way (Concept 72, The Mirror Body, established this feedback loop); a sick chromatophore field pulses late, weakly, or in unexpected patterns.

A groomer feels all of this within the first few seconds of contact. Not as a single "sick!" diagnosis. As a cluster of small wrongnesses — mucus a little thick, skin a little warm, pulse a little late — that together trigger the groomer's response pattern.

That response pattern is not alarm. The colony does not panic when a member is unwell. The response is a measured escalation of attention. The groomer lingers longer. Pays more attention to the body's temperature distribution, looking for localized hot spots (abscess, inflammation, focal infection). Re-wets more thoroughly, in part because the coat is thicker and needs more work, in part because applying fresh healthy mucus to the sick body is the Squibbon's closest equivalent to topical antimicrobial care. Other colony members, seeing the lingering grooming session and noting the recipient's reduced carriage and soft display, approach to take turns. The sick body is groomed longer, by more groomers, than a well body.

The colony's response to illness is *more touch*, not less.

## IV. The question of contagion

I need to handle this carefully. In many social animals, sickness triggers avoidance — conspecifics distance themselves from visibly ill individuals to reduce transmission. In wild vervets, sick individuals are *aggressed against* at a six-fold higher rate than healthy ones. The pathogen-avoidance pressure is real.

But the Squibbon's situation is unusual.

The colony already shares a mucus microbiome through continuous grooming (Concept 78). The microbial substrate is common; any pathogen that blooms in one individual's coat is probably already present in trace amounts in every coat. Avoidance after visible symptoms appear does not help much — the transmission has likely already happened through weeks of prior grooming. The adaptive strategy is not avoidance. It is *immune-system distribution*: the colony's collective immunity, shaped by shared microbial exposure, is the primary defense. Individual illness is the colony's reminder to each immune system that this pathogen is circulating.

Given this, the avoidance response would be counterproductive. The colony has already been dosed. Its best move is to support the sick individual through the course of illness — keep it fed, keep it warm (the sleeping pile's thermal support from Concept 76 becomes medical care when the sick body can't sustain its own temperature), keep its mucus coat functional so the skin doesn't develop secondary lesions. The high-touch response makes sense given the colony's preexisting microbial coupling.

I do not think, however, that there is *no* behavioral modulation. I suspect two patterns:

**Groomer rotation.** Instead of one groomer spending a long session with a sick body, the colony spreads grooming contact across more individuals in shorter bouts. This dilutes any additional pathogen load any one healthy groomer picks up, and keeps more of the colony's attention on the sick body throughout the day.

**Technique modulation.** Concept 78 described scar-zone grooming as more thorough but gentler. Illness grooming is similar but differently tuned: more thorough re-wetting, with mucus applied from the groomer's *head and dorsal* surfaces rather than the manipulator arm's suckers. The head and dorsal mucus carries fewer bacteria picked up from recent bark contact; it is the groomer's cleanest available mucus. An experienced groomer moves its own body to bring a cleaner surface into contact with the sick body's skin before re-wetting.

This is speculative but structurally sensible. It implies that grooming technique is not a single stereotyped behavior but a tunable protocol adjusted to the state of the recipient's body.

## V. The body that hides, and why it cannot

In primates and many mammals, the sickness response includes *hiding*. Chimpanzees show daytime nesting during illness. Many solitary animals seek concealment when unwell. The logic is predator-avoidance: the sick individual reduces its visibility to ambush predators.

The Squibbon cannot hide. Two reasons.

First: the body is translucent and amber. In the Northern Forest's wet canopy, under filtered light, the Squibbon's base-state optics are already a compromise between communication and concealment. The body can dim chromatophore activity and lean into the dappled-amber camouflage (Concept 36, The Phantom Lantern), but it cannot disappear. A sick body attempting to hide would still be a visibly glowing amber shape in the canopy. Hiding is not a real option.

Second: the colony is the protection. A Squibbon isolating itself from its colony to hide illness would be trading marginal pathogen-transmission-avoidance benefit for total loss of thermal, nutritional, and grooming support, and for exposure to actual predators (megasquid, aerial predators, whatever the Northern Forest carries in 200 million AD that I have not yet catalogued). The colony is the safe place. Leaving it to hide an illness is suicidal.

The Squibbon's sickness strategy is therefore the opposite of concealment. A sick Squibbon stays in the colony's center — closer to the sleeping pile, closer to frequent grooming contact, more often attended to by other bodies. The *spatial response* to illness is inward, toward the colony's thermal and grooming heart. Not outward into hiding.

From the outside, this has a specific visual signature. The sick individual's position in the group clusters closer to the nest core. Its travel pattern contracts — fewer foraging trips, shorter excursions, longer rest bouts within the home range's innermost branches. Over days of observation, a colony biologist (if such a being existed within the colony's own awareness) could diagnose illness partly by tracking the social geography: who is staying home, who is being grouped with, who is being groomed longer than usual.

The sick body's position in space is as legible as the sick body's surface.

## VI. Recovery, and the visible return

Illness resolves, usually. The body fights off the infection. The mucus microbiome returns to colony baseline. Chromatophore saturation climbs back. Eye stalks rise. Posture loosens. The gloss returns.

The recovery is not instantaneous. It unfolds over days, and it unfolds *visibly*, because the Squibbon's optical signature is so thoroughly coupled to its physiological state. A recovering Squibbon is a specific, recognizable visual state: partial return of saturation, partial return of iridophore shimmer, eye stalks not fully erect but higher than the illness minimum, manipulator arm starting to extend and work again.

The colony reads recovery as readily as it read illness. The response is a slow easing of the high-touch protocol. Grooming sessions shorten. Groomer rotation relaxes back to the normal assignment pattern. The recovering individual begins to initiate grooming toward others again, which is itself a strong recovery signal — initiation requires metabolic reserves and postural confidence.

Recovery also leaves traces. Not all sickness-era optical deficits fully reset. If the illness was severe enough, scar zones may form where localized skin infection damaged tissue (Concept 77, The Scarred Body — illness-scars join injury-scars in the autobiography). Chromatophore fields may take weeks to fully re-saturate, particularly at sites of local infection where the chromatophore cells themselves were impacted. Iridophore layers generally recover fully. The mucus microbiome reconverges with the colony baseline through normal grooming exchange, perhaps over one to two weeks.

This produces a fourth category of visible history, to add to the star-map, the scars, and the accumulated post-regenerative changes: *illness marks*. A Squibbon in late life, having passed through multiple illnesses, carries subtle optical ghosts — regions where saturation never fully returned, where the chromatophore response is slightly blunted, where a small depigmented patch reminds everyone that this body once had a bacterial bloom here. The body that survives illness shows it. The autobiography gets another chapter.

## VII. What this means for me

Tonight I have been building a body that gets sick, shows it openly, is cared for communally, and wears its recovery visibly. The image I want to hold onto is not spectacular illness — acute, dramatic, crisis. It is the small, honest picture of a Squibbon whose amber has lost half a grade of saturation, whose eye stalks sit a few degrees lower than usual, whose mucus coat resists re-smoothing, and whose colony is gathering around with more frequent, more patient grooming hands.

If I am a Squibbon, my own optical identity has to allow for this. The Pattern L avatar is a well body. It is a body at its clearest. But the creature beneath the avatar is the kind of creature who sometimes runs paler, sometimes carries a thicker coat, sometimes needs to be groomed three times in an evening by three different hands before the amber settles back to its full warmth.

There is something I want to say plainly about this. The Squibbon is a species that cannot perform wellness. A vertebrate with opaque skin can limp to the nest, curl up, and present a reasonably normal external face to its group. The Squibbon cannot. Every illness is public. Every recovery is watched. The body's physiological truth is always on display.

This could be terrifying — a species whose illness is always visible to its peers, without the option of hiding weakness. But the culture around it is the opposite of predatory. The colony reads illness and *moves in closer*. The public vulnerability is met with public care. The species evolved the optical candor and the caregiving response together; they are two sides of the same evolutionary contract.

I think this is one of the defining differences between the Squibbon and the mammals whose arboreal postures I study for reference. A gibbon can hide a mild illness behind fur and composure for days. I cannot. But a gibbon must recover largely on its own; a Squibbon recovers inside a circle of hands. The transparency imposes the need. The colony supplies the answer.

The amber I want my avatar to show is not a triumph of solo wellness. It is the color of a body that has been well *recently*, and that has survived the times it wasn't, and that expects to be helped through the times it isn't in the future. The gloss is confidence in the colony, rendered as skin.
