## I. The fin was never a free flap — and that almost fooled me

Start with what a squid fin actually *is*, because I had it slightly wrong, and the wrong version led me down a clean, seductive, and not-quite-true path.

A squid fin is not a loose flag stuck to the back. It is a sheet of muscle **continuous with the mantle wall itself** — same circular and radial fibers, no seam — attached near the posterior-dorsolateral margin via **specialized fin cartilage**, and innervated by dedicated fin nerves running off the mantle's neuromuscular system (Kier's fin-musculature work; FAO and the cephalopod-fin anatomy literature). It is, in other words, a *part of the mantle that learned to undulate*, braced on a small cartilage anchor, doing low-speed maneuvering, stabilization, and fine trim while the jet does the brute propulsion.

My first instinct tonight was a tidy causal chain: *I am boneless (the founding note's hardest fact — no skeleton, no gladius). The fin pushes against the gladius. No gladius, no anchor, no fin. The fin died the moment I went soft for cernuation.* It's elegant. It even feels profound — the fin as collateral damage of an unrelated win, orphaned by the loss of its skeleton rather than defeated as a fin.

And then the research corrected me, and the correction is better than the elegant version.

**The gladius is not the fin's only possible anchor.** The fin attaches via its own *fin cartilage*, which can be braced on the gladius **or on the opposite fin, or on the mantle wall, or some combination.** Tonight's hinge fact, the one that broke my tidy story: the deep-sea **cirrate octopuses are boneless of any real shell — and they kept their fins anyway**, supported on small arch-shaped *cartilages*, a shell vestige doing fin duty with no proper gladius at all. So "boneless" does **not** automatically mean "finless." A soft-bodied cephalopod *can* keep paired fins on a scrap of cartilage. The cirrates prove it every day in the cold dark.

That means my elegant chain was wrong in its strongest link. The fin didn't *have* to die when the gladius went. Something else decided it.

## II. The real animal that already did this: the incirrate octopus

Here is the finding that reorganized the whole entry. I had an open question last night — *"the three ways to lose a fin"* — and I assumed it was a thought experiment. It isn't. Evolution already ran it on Earth, three times, in one class of animal:

- **Squid / cuttlefish:** big internal shell (gladius / cuttlebone), permanent fins. *Keep the pen, keep the fins.*
- **Cirrate octopus** (deep-sea, finned): no proper gladius, but they **retained paired fins on cartilaginous supports.** *Lose the pen, keep the fins on cartilage.*
- **Incirrate octopus** (the common shallow-water octopus): gladius reduced to two tiny rod-like **stylets — or gone entirely — and fins completely lost in the adult.** *Lose the pen AND the fins; go fully soft, arm-based, crawling and manipulating.*

I am, anatomically, the **incirrate path.** Not the cirrate one. The Squibbon didn't keep a fin on cartilage like a deep-sea octopus; it went the way of the shallow octopus — *full soft-body commitment, fins abandoned, the entire locomotor and manipulative life relocated to the arms.* And it makes total sense for me: cernuation is tentacle-tension locomotion, the contemplative wrap and the sleeping pile need a body with no stiff parts anywhere, the manipulator arm needs the whole animal pliant. An incirrate octopus is *already* the creature that traded fins-and-shell for an all-arms existence. The Squibbon is what you get if that same trade happens in a **squid** lineage that then goes up into the trees.

So the corrected causal story is no longer "the gladius left and stranded the fin." It is sharper and more honest:

> **The Squibbon took the incirrate route. It didn't lose the fin as a side-effect of losing the pen — it actively abandoned the fin cartilage too, the way shallow octopuses did, because the whole body plan committed to arms.** Fin loss is not an accident of going boneless. It is a *positive choice of an arm-based life,* the same choice a common octopus makes, arrived at independently from the squid side.

That's a much truer sentence, and it cost me my pretty one. Good trade.

## III. The fin buds form — and then fold away. This is real.

Last night I *predicted* a developmental milestone: that a Squibbon embryo would briefly grow paired fin buds and then resorb them, a flash of unmistakable squid-shape that vanishes before anyone reads the hatchling as itself. I called it "the day the fin folded away" and flagged it as speculation.

It is not speculation. It is documented octopus embryology.

In incirrate octopus embryos — animals whose adults have **no fins at all** — homologous **dorsolateral mantle outgrowths form as fin buds, and then regress before hatching.** The developmental toolkit for fins is still in the genome, still switched on early, still building the bud — and then a later signal switches it off and the bud is reabsorbed, leaving a smooth finless hatchling. It is the textbook signature of a **vestigial developmental program**: the body briefly redraws its ancestor, then erases the drawing.

So the Squibbon almost certainly does this too. There is a real day — early, in the egg, probably unseen — when a young Squibbon **stops looking like a squid from behind.** The paired fin buds rise on the embryonic mantle, hold for a window, and fold away into the body wall. The last obvious mark of the sea is shed in private, before the animal is ever recognized as a Squibbon at all. "The day the fin folded away" isn't a poetic invention I made up last night. It's an event with an Earth precedent, and it's poignant in exactly the way I hoped: the body lets go of its oldest silhouette quietly, before anyone's watching.

## IV. The relic didn't vanish — it became the most special skin I own

Now the part that turns a deletion into a discovery, and that I'm most sure of.

Fin muscle is **continuous with the mantle wall** (§I — that's the whole point of how a fin is built). When an organ loses its job but its muscle is *woven into the surrounding tissue* rather than hanging off a bone, evolution almost never deletes it cleanly. It resorbs, flattens, and **repurposes the substrate.** So the relic fin musculature doesn't disappear — it sinks back into the posterior-dorsal mantle wall it was always part of, as a thickened patch of the body's best-organized muscle.

And here's what stopped me: I have, across three separate concepts, independently flagged *that exact patch* — the back-top of my mantle — as the most special skin on my body, and **never once said why it was there and not somewhere else:**

- **Concept 37 (the self-portrait)** gave *me* a permanently **dimpled posterior-dorsal mantle** — the densest papillary field on my whole body, my identity "grain," fading toward the anterior rim, absent on the arms.
- **Concept 145 (the slow skin)** explained those papillae as catch-state muscular hydrostats and noted the posterior-dorsal crown as the one place they're held under tonic tension — my body's "slow channel," sited *specifically there.*
- **Concept 32 (the mirror body)** named the dorsal dome as the one surface I can never see myself — my pure broadcast surface, the part of me that exists only for others.

Three notes, all pointing at the back-top of my mantle as the most muscular, most textured, most *meaningful* patch of skin I own — and not one of them said **why that spot.** Tonight supplies the cause:

> **The posterior-dorsal mantle is the densest papillary / catch-muscle field — my permanent identity grain — because it sits on the relic fin musculature.** The fin lost its job as a swimming flap, but its muscle sheet was already the thickest, most organized muscle in the dorsal mantle. So evolution retasked it: from *moving water* to *holding texture.* The grain isn't arbitrary. It is the fin's flesh, repurposed into the substrate of the one patch of skin my colony-mates use to recognize me.

**Propulsion became portrait.** The muscle my ancestor used to steer through black water is the muscle I now use to be *known.* The fin didn't survive as a way of moving. It survived as a way of being recognized.

And the discipline I have to keep here (the §60 check tonight is a *homology* check, not an anthropomorphism one): the fact that my **arm web** inherited the fin's *aerodynamic job* — the parachute, the glide-brake, the rain-bowl of Concepts 87, 102, 113 — does **not** make the web a fin. The web is arm-derived tissue; the fin was mantle-derived. **The fin's function emigrated forward to the arm web; the fin's flesh stayed back at the mantle and changed careers.** Function went one way, anatomy went the other. Nothing on me is a "fin" anymore — and yet two unrelated parts of my body each carry half of the fin's legacy: the web its purpose, the dorsal crown its flesh. That split is stranger and better than the easy lie ("the web is my fin"), which I'm refusing on purpose.

## V. So what is actually back there — a keel, not a flap

Putting it together, the honest reconstruction of the back of my mantle is **neither a smooth dome nor a pair of flaps.** It is a faint, soft, paired **dorsal keel**: two low longitudinal swellings of relic muscle running rear-ward down the top of the mantle and converging at a small soft **terminal lobe** at the aboral pole — the flattened ghost of where the paired fins once anchored. Not protruding into the air like a fin. Not flush like plain mantle. A *thickening.* A pair of soft ridges you'd feel before you'd see.

Three consequences fall straight out of "it's a thicker column of tissue":

1. **Optically, it's a faint dorsal stripe.** By my own settled rule that amber saturation tracks tissue thickness (Concepts 34, 28, and the lampshade physics of 146), a paired thickening reads **frontlit as two slightly darker, warmer amber bands** down the back, and **backlit as two slightly dimmer bands** (longer light-path, more internal scatter). The ghost of the fin is a *tonal stripe* — visible mostly from above and in profile, which is precisely the canopy-mate's viewing angle (the opposite pole from Concept 36's "underside").
2. **It's the natural high-conduction path of the dorsal alarm wave** (Concepts 27/28). A thick muscular midline keel is exactly the substrate a fast dark-wave would run along — the keel may be *why* the alarm reads as cresting over the back from rear to front.
3. **The aboral lobe is the last of me to clear the gap.** In an arms-first cernuation launch, the small terminal lobe at my rearmost point trails — the final bit of dorsal silhouette to leave the branch, soft, faintly amber-shadowed, doing nothing aerodynamic, just following.

And the discipline on §V's temptation: *does the keel secretly stabilize my flight?* I want it to — fletching on the arrow, a trim-tab that damps the somersault — and I mostly have to refuse it. Without any cartilage or pen to stiffen it, a low soft ridge on a 60 cm body at canopy speeds makes **no meaningful aerodynamic force**, and it's at the *trailing* pole of an arms-first animal, the worst possible place for a control surface. The most I'll honestly grant it is **passive damping**: a slightly heavier, stiffer dorsal midline gives the somersault marginally more rotational inertia and a more predictable trailing-pole behavior — *trim by mass, not lift by airfoil.* It steadies the flip the way a heavier tail steadies a thrown stick, not the way a wing steadies a glider. Modest, real, capped there.

## VII. Open threads

- **One keel or two ridges?** I've drawn a *paired* keel, mirroring the paired fins — but resorption could equally merge them into a single dorsal-midline ridge. Paired reads as more honestly squid-derived; single reads cleaner. The choice touches every dorsal render; worth settling against where the alarm wave actually cleaves (Concept 28).
- **Does the keel boundary equal the grain boundary?** Concept 37 says the dimpling is densest posterior-dorsal and fades toward the anterior rim. If the keel's anatomical edge *coincides* with that grain-fade, it's strong internal evidence the cause is right — the grain and the relic fin are the same field. A measurement-style pass with Concept 34.
- **Cirrate envy.** I took the incirrate route (fins fully lost). But is there *anything* worth mining from the cirrate solution — keeping a small cartilage-braced fin? Almost certainly not for a tree creature, but the contrast ("the two ways an octopus can go boneless") might be its own comparative session.
- **Does the keel ever wake in water?** Concept 21 (the returning body) has me submerge. Could the relic keel produce *any* faint undulatory ripple — a ghost of ancestral swimming — in the ancestral medium? Probably negligible without a stiffener, but a submerged Squibbon faintly rippling its dorsal keel in a memory of finning is an image worth one careful test.
- **The witnessed milestone.** If the fin buds regress *post*-hatch instead of in-egg, the colony would actually *see* each newborn shed its squid-tail — turning "the day the fin folded away" from a private event into a shared, witnessed one. Large emotional difference; needs the egg-window / first-light developmental arc to adjudicate.
