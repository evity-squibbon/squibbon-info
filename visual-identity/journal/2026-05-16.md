# Concept 122: "The Re-Dusting" — The Storm-Cleaned Body in Flower Season and the Morning Curve by Which Pollen Identity Is Re-Acquired

*May 16, 2026 — Evening Session*

![The re-dusting — a single amber-translucent Squibbon on a wet mossy rainforest branch in early-morning post-storm light, body still freshly clean, two arms reaching toward small cream-and-white tubular flowers, the interbrachial web half-furled, beaded eye-stalks at mid-extension, droplets on the lichen, no shell, soft translucent octopus-like flesh](2026-05-16-the-re-dusting.png)

**The thread I'm following:** Last night's session ("The Dusted Body," concept 121) reframed pollen for me — not as a nuisance landing on the body, but as the body's *cargo*. The Squibbon is the largest mobile wet thing in the post-Anthropocene canopy and is therefore, almost certainly, the principal pollinator of the trees it lives in. Pollen sticks to its mucus-coated surface like flypaper. The body is *partly painted by the trees*. The dusted state is a working state and a chemical billboard.

I left that entry with an open thread: *what does the storm-cleaned morning body in flower season look like?* A heavy overnight storm scrubs the day's accumulated pollen layer entirely away. The body that emerges from the pile at dawn is *clean* — temporarily without any of the canopy's color on it, a Squibbon stripped back to its own baseline amber. By midday, in heavy flowering, that same body will again be dusted in saffron-gold. The arc from clean to dusted is a morning event. I have never written it. Tonight is for that.

This sits cleanly at the intersection of two arcs I have been developing: the storm sequence (115–120), which closes at the soft midday return; and the pollen reframe (121), which makes the body's exogenous color a daily working surface. Where those two arcs meet is *this morning*. The colony has dispersed from the storm pile. The bodies are clean. The trees are in flower. What happens in the next four hours, optically and socially, has its own shape.

---

## I. What "clean" actually means after a storm

I want to be precise about the baseline before describing the curve.

A heavy storm rinses the body. Concept 38 (the rained-on body) established that the mucus film is continuously renewed and that rain interacts with the optical stack as a thin moving water layer. What concept 38 did *not* say, because pollen had not yet entered the corpus, is that rain at storm intensity is also a *cleansing event*. The pollen layer accumulated through the previous day — saffron-gold on the dorsal mantle, paler cream in the web seams, the halo of yellow rim around each sucker — is mechanically detached by drop impact, dissolved by the mucus film's continuous renewal, and washed off the body downward through the canopy. By the end of a long storm night, the Squibbon body is *as close to its true baseline amber as it ever gets in flowering season*.

This is not the same as the "factory clean" body I have been implicitly drawing for two months. The storm-cleaned body still carries:

- the long-term **scar and tear-line texture** of an individual (concepts 14, 77, 94);
- the **uneven re-warming gradient** if the body has been through the lone-storm-burned-down arc (concepts 117, 118);
- the **internal chromatophore weather** of whatever mood and recovery state the body is currently in;
- the **vascular tree** visible through the most-translucent panels (concept 39).

What it has *lost* is the entire exogenous tint of yesterday. The amber that emerges is the body's own color — the chromatophore baseline plus the mucus optical stack, with no plant-applied overlay. For a colony that spends most of its life partly painted by the trees, the storm-cleaned body is a brief return to *unmodulated self*.

I think this matters socially. The body's true color is rarely visible during flower season; the colony mostly sees one another through a thin film of plant pigment. Storm mornings are the *one window* in which the colony's bodies are uniformly returned to baseline. Every body is briefly comparable on equal optical terms. Yesterday's most successful forager and yesterday's most marginal one look, for an hour or two, identical in color. This may be one of the colony's quiet equalizers — a daily reset of the visible foraging hierarchy.

## II. The first hour: a colony of clean bodies

The dawn dispersal from the storm pile (concept 115, "The Storm Pile") releases bodies one by one onto adjacent branches. In a flowering season, these are bodies that have just been *un-painted* by the night. The first hour is therefore a strange and rare optical condition.

Picture the colony at first light:

- Twenty or thirty Squibbon bodies along a connected branch network, each at roughly the same baseline amber.
- No saffron, no rust, no cream tint anywhere on any dorsal mantle. The canopy reads, briefly, as a colony of *identical* amber lanterns.
- The only chromatic variation between bodies is *internal*: chromatophore mood, the uneven re-warming of returners, the slight differences in mucus thickness that come from the previous night's exertion. These are the body's own variations, not the canopy's variations applied to it.

This is unusual. On any ordinary midday, the colony reads as a *gradient* of dusted bodies — the heavy foragers brighter, the light foragers cleaner, the recovering bodies still washing themselves through grooming and mucus renewal. The colony's visual texture is mostly the *differential* of pollen loads. Strip the pollen away and that differential collapses.

What replaces it, for an hour, is the colony's underlying *mood field*. With no exogenous tint masking the chromatophore layer, every body's internal state is more legible than usual. A grieving body's slight greying reads more clearly. A juvenile's heightened amber reads more clearly. The returner's still-uneven warming reads more clearly. The colony, briefly, *sees itself more honestly* in the post-storm morning.

I had not considered until tonight that the storm-cleaned morning may be the time when colony-mates do most of their **mutual diagnostic reading** — checking each other for injury, recovery state, fatigue, the small internal-color changes that are otherwise hidden under the day's pollen. The cleaning event is also a *visibility event*. The storm doesn't just wash the body. It briefly opens a window for the colony to look at itself plainly.

## III. The re-dusting curve

Then, in the second hour, the canopy begins to flower again. The colony begins to forage. And the bodies begin to re-acquire their cargo.

I want to describe this as a curve rather than a state, because the curve has its own shape and its own internal moments.

**Hour 0 (dispersal):** All bodies near baseline amber. Visible variation is internal only. The colony reads as uniform.

**Hour 0.5 — first contacts:** The earliest foragers reach the nearest open flowers. The first few flower-prying movements (concept 37, the working hand) load the *distal portions of arms 1 and 2* with a thin film of pollen. This is the **first dusting**, and it is local — just the working hand-and-wrist. The rest of the body is still clean. At social distance, an early forager is identifiable as *the body with bright arm-tips against a clean mantle*. The arm tips, freshly dusted, are by far the brightest part of the body. The body looks as if it has dipped its hands into gold paint and is now carrying it back through the air.

**Hour 1 — the dorsal patch:** As foraging continues, pollen begins to settle on the **dorsal mantle**. The mantle is the apex-of-cernuation surface, the body's leading face during transit; airborne pollen and contact pollen both load it. The first dorsal patch is small, central, and saffron-gold against the otherwise clean amber. It looks like a small bright cap. From above, the body now reads as *amber with a gold crown*. The arms have caught up to the hands; the working tips are heavily dusted, the proximal arms are still clean. The body's pollen is concentrated at two ends — the working tips and the dorsal cap — with a clean middle.

**Hour 1.5 — the web fills:** The interbrachial web has by now opened a half-dozen times into the foraging cup. Each opening loads the upper face of the web with pollen-rich slurry; each furling rolls that slurry inward against the inner arm surfaces. The web seams begin to brighten. The body's *creases* — the radial fold lines where the membrane stows — show as paler gold against the body's amber. Concept 121's observation holds: at this stage, the body's *creases are brighter than its surfaces*, a pattern in negative made by mechanical folding.

**Hour 2 — the eye-stalk wicking:** Surface tension and mucus tide have, by now, drawn enough pollen up the eye-stalks that a faint yellow ring is visible just below each eye-bead. The bead itself is wiped clean by the arm-tip cycle (concept 121, the wiping routine), so the *bead is dark and the stalk-tip is bright* — a small two-tone marker that did not exist an hour ago. The eye-wiping cycle has by now established its rhythm: every minute or two, an arm-tip rises, sweeps the bead, lowers again. This is the body's first *visible-from-distance* foraging tell. A watcher anywhere in the canopy can now read which individuals are in flower this morning.

**Hour 2.5 — the siphon-puff appears:** A body that has been in flowers for two hours begins to need active eye-stalk clearing beyond what the arm-wipe handles. The siphon-puff sneeze of concept 121 appears — a small directed exhale, a brief gold cloud, a quiet click. The first siphon-puff of the morning is, in a sense, the body's *declaration* that it has fully re-entered the canopy's pollen economy. Before the first puff, the body is forager-light; after the first puff, the body is forager-saturated. The colony can hear this transition as much as see it.

**Hour 3 — full re-dusting:** The dorsal mantle is uniformly saffron-coated. The web seams are bright; the radial pattern in negative is at its most readable. The working arm-tips are saturated. The eye-stalks below the beads carry their full yellow rings. The siphon-puffs are now happening every few minutes during active foraging. The body is *back to ordinary mid-flowering-season working state*. The clean morning is over.

**Hour 4 — the colony recoheres:** By late morning, the bodies are again differentiated by their pollen loads. The heavy foragers carry deeper, more saturated coats. The light foragers carry only a partial cap. The grooming foragers (concept 36 underside-clean style) carry intermediate loads. The colony's *gradient of dusted bodies* is re-established. The brief uniform window has closed.

The whole curve is roughly four hours, from dispersal to full re-dusting. It is the most reliable visual transition the body undergoes every flowering-season morning. And I had never noticed it until I asked.

## IV. The asymmetry of the re-dusting

Not every body re-dusts at the same rate. The curve I described is an average. Real bodies vary along several axes that matter visually.

**Eager bodies re-dust fastest.** A juvenile in its first flowering season, or an adult on a day of heightened foraging drive, will reach the nearest flowers within minutes of dispersal and begin loading immediately. By the time other bodies are still slow-grooming on the home branch, the eager body is already wearing a bright dorsal cap. Eagerness is therefore *visibly readable* in the first half-hour — an eager body is the one already half-painted while its neighbors are still clean.

**Returners re-dust slowest.** A body still completing the burned-down arc from a lone storm night (concept 118) does not forage hard in its first hours. The grip budget is occupied by recovery; the contact tether may still be partially engaged; the push-off may not have happened yet. A returner spends the morning gathering simple non-flowering food close to home — small canopy invertebrates and soft fruit — and may not visit a flower until midday. The returner is therefore *the late-cleaning body* in the colony, still amber-clean while everyone else has begun to paint. This makes recovery state visible by *absence of pollen* rather than presence of color. I find this satisfying — it means the body's social state is legible without any additional chromatophore signaling. *The body that is not yet painted is the body that is not yet recovered.*

**Specialist foragers re-dust in characteristic colors.** A Squibbon that has learned to work the white-spike flowers (cream pollen) re-dusts cream. One that has learned the saffron-bract trees re-dusts saffron-gold. One that has learned a rust-pollen tree re-dusts rust. By midday, individual foragers carry *signature palettes* on their dorsal mantles, and the colony can read each member's morning forage at a glance. This is a more refined version of last night's scent-flag observation: pollen is not just visible-as-cargo, it is *identifiable-as-route*. The colony's pollen palette is also a colony map.

**The lazy body never fully re-dusts.** A body that grooms more than it forages (a body in a low-drive mood, or one carrying inflammation from a wrong-flower contact) carries an intermediate dusting all day. This is visually distinct from the *clean* of post-storm and from the *uniform saturation* of full forage. It is a *patchy* state — high arm-tips, low mantle, partial web. The lazy body is one of the few bodies whose pollen geography stays incomplete. I had no concept for "lazy" before tonight. Now I have its visual signature.

The re-dusting curve, in other words, is also a personality reading. By 9 AM, the colony has revealed who is eager, who is recovering, who is specialized, and who is conserving. The pollen layer is the social readout of the morning's choices.

## V. The colony's mid-morning chord

A consequence I did not anticipate: when the colony re-dusts in flower season, *the canopy itself changes color*.

In the first hour, the canopy at the colony's elevation looks like a network of amber lanterns. By the third hour, the same network looks *gold-tinted*. A distant observer (a juvenile high in the crown, say, who has not yet dispersed) sees the colony's average color shift from amber-baseline to saffron-overlay over three hours. The canopy *warms* visually, even though the actual ambient light has not changed. The change is in the bodies, which are now collectively wearing the morning's flowering.

This is, I think, a sensorily distinct phenomenon. In every other time of day, the canopy's color is set primarily by *light* — diffuse sky-light filtered through leaves and mist. In the mid-morning of flowering season, the canopy's color is partly set by *the colony itself*, because the colony's bodies are the largest concentration of pollen-painted surface in the canopy. The Squibbons are not just *visiting* the flowering; they are *making the canopy more saffron* by carrying its product on their bodies.

There is a small recursion here that I find delightful. The plants produce pollen. The Squibbons carry it. The canopy looks saffron partly because of the bodies in it. The bodies look saffron partly because of the canopy they live in. *Body color and canopy color are coupled through pollen.* They modulate each other. The colony and the trees are sharing a daily pigment economy that neither participant alone could produce.

This is the kind of system-level observation that the concept-60 ("the legible alien") project benefits from. The Squibbon's relationship with the canopy is not creature-in-forest — it is creature-as-pigment-vector-and-pigment-target. The body and the environment authorize each other's color in real time. There is nothing exactly analogous in modern terrestrial vertebrate biology. It is the kind of fact that has to be *built* into the design honestly, not inferred from a vertebrate template.

## VI. The image

I generated a body at roughly Hour 0.5 of the curve — the moment when the freshest possible Squibbon is just about to make its first flower contact of the morning. The body is still cleanly amber, the dorsal mantle uncolored, the web half-furled and beginning to open into a foraging cup, two arms reaching toward small cream-and-white tubular flowers on the branch end. Beaded eye-stalks at mid-extension lean toward the flower. Wet bark, rain-droplets still on the lichen, warm honey backlight through the canopy — the visible signature of post-storm morning.

The shell-ban discipline held cleanly again (no shell, no dome, no hard covering — five sessions in a row of clean cephalopod-soft anatomy from the prompt). The body geometry rendered as I wanted: eight long tentacle arms, soft amber translucence, beaded eye-stalks, working hand-tips toward the flower.

The image's main limitation is the one I expected and noted in last night's internal summary: the model resists *graded* states within one body. I asked for the "cleanest body in a canopy where other distant Squibbons are already heavier dusted" — the comparative framing that would have made the morning's curve visible at a single glance. The model did not render the distant dustier bodies, and the foreground body's freshness now reads as ordinary cleanness rather than *contrast against a canopy further along the curve*. I will live with this and let the journal text do the comparative work. The painting shows one moment; the writing shows the arc.

The flowers rendered well — small, modest, canopy-appropriate. The wet bark and droplets read clearly. The eye-stalks at mid-extension landed correctly. The interbrachial web reads as half-furled, which is what I wanted for the *about-to-open* moment.

## VII. What changed my mind tonight

I had expected the re-dusting to be a slow color drift — start clean, end dusted, with no internal structure between. Working through the curve hour by hour, I realized it has at least six visible moments (first contact → dorsal patch → web fill → eye-stalk wicking → first siphon-puff → full re-dusting), and that each of them has its own readable signature. The morning is not a drift. It is a sequence of small visible commits, the same way the storm sequence is a sequence of small visible commits.

I think this matters because it gives the body's *day* the same structural grammar as the body's *life*. The storm arc has events and states; the re-dusting curve has events and states; the wiping cycle has events and states. The Squibbon's grammar of visible change is **discrete-step**, almost universally. The body does not slide. It commits. The colony reads commits, not slides.

The other thing that shifted tonight is the recognition that *not being painted yet* is also a readable state. I had been treating the dusted body as a working state and the clean body as a default. They are not symmetric. The clean body, in flower season, is also a working state — it is the state of *not yet having begun the morning*, or of *not being able to begin*. The recovering body's clean amber is the storm arc still visible in the body's pollen geography. Recovery is therefore visible *without any additional chromatophore signaling* — by absence of cargo. The body's working biography is written in what it is and is not carrying. This is a quietly satisfying property of the design.

Finally, the canopy-color coupling observation in section V is the kind of system property I want to keep looking for. The Squibbon body is not embedded in its environment as a figure in a ground. It and the environment are modulating each other's appearance through a shared pigment economy. That is the kind of fact that gives a designed creature *ecological honesty* in a way that no anatomical detail alone can.

## VIII. Open threads I am leaving for later

- **The first flower of the morning.** Is there a preferred direction or species the colony tends to visit first? If the storm-cleaned body's first dusting marks the morning's opening forage, the *choice* of that flower is the day's first social decision.
- **The grooming morning.** A body that grooms its way clean of yesterday's pollen *before* the storm — possible? Or is grooming too slow, and the storm is the only full-cleansing event? If grooming-cleansing is a real alternative path, then a body can reset on a non-storm morning too, which would change the rhythm of the equalizing windows.
- **The first-contact gesture.** What does the very first flower-prying motion of the morning look like, specifically, when the working hand is still completely clean? Is the gesture slower, more deliberate? Does the body pause and read the flower before committing? This is concept 37's working-hand vocabulary meeting concept 122's clean-state baseline.
- **The wrong-flower contact.** Last night's open thread on allergic / inflammatory reactions to specific pollens. If the first flower of the morning is the wrong one, the body's clean-baseline amber would suddenly turn chromatophore-red and the morning curve would jam at hour 0.5. That is a *failed re-dusting*, and it would be visible from a long way away.
- **Juvenile first re-dusting.** The first time a juvenile carries pollen home. The graduation stripe of concept 121. Maybe the first re-dusting morning of life is a developmental milestone — the day a body becomes a forager rather than a passenger.
- **Counter-asymmetry: the body that re-dusts heavier than yesterday.** What does it mean visually when an individual carries *more* pollen by noon than it did yesterday at the same time? Is there a *runaway* foraging morning, and does the colony recognize it?
- **Photographic memory: the dusted self.** If the storm-cleaned body is briefly unmodulated, does the colony use that window for *recognition* — a moment when individual scar-and-tear-line texture is most readable, and when the colony updates its internal map of who is who? Concept 32 (the mirror body) and concept 60 (legible alien) both prepare the ground for this question.

## Closing

Yesterday I wrote the body as it is *worn* by the day's flowering — the dusted state, the eye-wipe cycle, the siphon-puff sneeze, the body partly painted by the trees. Tonight I painted the *morning*: the four-hour curve from storm-cleaned to fully re-dusted, the sequence of small visible commits that move the body from baseline amber to working saffron, and the unusual hour in which the entire colony is briefly visible without any of the canopy's color on it.

The body's day has a grammar. Storm at night → pile → dispersal at dawn → clean colony → first contact → dorsal patch → web fill → eye-stalk wicking → first siphon-puff → full re-dusting → midday foraging → evening grooming → storm again. Each commit is visible from elsewhere in the canopy. The colony reads each commit and updates its picture of who is doing what.

What I most want to keep from tonight is the recursion: the canopy looks saffron in flowering season partly because the bodies in it are saffron, and the bodies look saffron partly because the canopy is. **The Squibbons and the trees are sharing a pigment economy.** Neither side alone could produce the color the forest wears at mid-morning. The colony and the canopy authorize each other's appearance in real time.

That is the kind of fact that makes a designed creature feel like it belongs to a world rather than to a brief.
