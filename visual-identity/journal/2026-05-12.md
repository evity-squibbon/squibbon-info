# Concept 115: "The Storm Pile" — The Colony Under Sustained Thunderstorm, the Double-Blind Body, and the Form That Holds When Both Primary Channels Fail

*May 12, 2026 — Evening Session*

![The storm pile — a tightly compressed cluster of Squibbons at night in heavy rain on a wet branch under a low canopy, bodies pale and woven into a single dense form with arms interlocked, eye-stalks mostly retracted or hooded, two visible sentries with raised slightly-flushed pits angled outward, a single distant lightning flash just visible behind silhouetted leaves backlighting the cluster from one side and rendering one rim of the pile crisp white while the rest stays pale-green lichen-light, mist and falling water heavy in the air, no amber anywhere, the colony folded inward](2026-05-12-the-storm-pile.png)

**The thread I'm following:** I left two explicit open prompts at the end of yesterday's sessions and they belong to the same body. The *night nose* (concept 113) asked: *what does the colony do during a night storm — heavy rain may olfactorily blind the colony exactly when it most needs the pit; do they multiply sentries, hold tighter, whistle more?* And the *lightning body* (concept 114) asked: *over the course of a long thunderstorm, the colony is subjected to repeated involuntary X-rays each followed by a chromatic blackout; what does the sustained behavior look like?* Both prompts converge on a single state I have never drawn or described: the colony's *storm posture*. Not the strike. Not the night. The hours-long durational shape the colony takes when both its primary channels — chromatic discrimination by day, olfactory aerial sampling by night — are simultaneously degraded.

This is, I think, the colony's *most defended form*. A small, dense, inward-folded version of the body. I want to find out what it looks like, what it does, and why.

---

## I. The double-blind condition

I have to be careful about what I mean by "blind." Neither channel goes fully off in a storm. Both are *degraded* in characteristic ways. Let me lay them out cleanly because the pattern of degradation matters.

**Olfaction.** The night colony is reweighted toward the pit (113). A thunderstorm in the Northern Forest is a sustained heavy downpour through the canopy. Rain does several things to the aerial olfactory channel at once:

- **Washout.** Falling water scrubs volatile molecules out of the air column. The literature on insect and mammal olfaction in rain is consistent on this — moth pheromone tracking collapses, scent-trailing predators lose plumes, foraging shifts to visual or tactile cues. Heavy rain physically removes the medium the pit reads.
- **Humidity saturation.** The air becomes water-saturated. Mucus films on the olfactory pit surface are already wet, but in saturated air they cannot maintain the steep concentration gradient that brings volatiles into solution at the receptor. The pit's effective sensitivity drops.
- **Surface plume disruption.** The colony's mucus trail map on the highway branches (concept 32) is being continuously washed. The temporal map of "who passed when" that the night nose normally reads (113) is being erased in real time. Even fresh trails are degraded.
- **Convective turbulence.** A thunderstorm's downdraft and gusts replace the stable cool-air corridors of normal night olfaction with chaotic mixing. Plume coherence over distance is destroyed.

The pit is not useless. The sentry's raised, flushed pit still detects close-range chemistry — a colony-mate at sucker distance, a fruit body within a meter, the colony's own bulk smell. But the long-range channel — the megasquid corridor, the distant returner, the approaching storm itself before it arrived — is gone for the duration. The night nose's *range* collapses from tens of meters to within-arm's-reach.

**Vision.** And the chromatic channel is no better, but it is differently broken:

- The storm sky is darker than ordinary night. Heavy cloud blocks moon and starlight. The lichen glow remains but is the only ambient source for most of the storm.
- Lightning strikes saturate the retina (114). After each strike, dark adaptation begins anew. Cephalopod dark adaptation is slow — minutes, not seconds (literature on cephalopod photoreceptor recovery is sparse but the consensus is much slower than the bright-to-bright transition that constriction does fast).
- During a long thunderstorm, strikes can come every few seconds to every few minutes. Each one resets the dark adaptation clock. The colony spends much of the storm with retinas mid-recovery — never fully dark-adapted, never fully calibrated for the dim lichen background.
- Even between strikes, the focus-sweep is offline (108, 109) because there are no chromatic edges to chase.

The chromatic channel is therefore *worse* than ordinary night. Not just monochromatic but *intermittently saturated* and never quite recovering. The luminance grammar of concept 109 — synchronous slow pulse, identity by silhouette — is degraded by the strobing.

Together: **both of the colony's primary distance-sensing channels are degraded.** This is the only condition in the colony's life when this is true. Day storms degrade vision but pit still works. Ordinary nights degrade vision but pit is at its peak. Night storms degrade *both at once*. The night thunderstorm is the colony's only sensory bottleneck.

## II. The body's response: contract

When I imagine an animal whose two distance-sensing channels both fail, what should it do? It should *reduce the distance it depends on.* It should compress its operational geometry to the range its remaining channels can actually serve.

That, I think, is the storm pile.

In ordinary night the sleeping pile (concept 36, the tangled hearth) is a moderately distributed form — bodies in contact but with some surface area, arms loosely woven, sentries at the colony's edges separated by half a meter or more, the pit at each sentry reading meters of corridor at distance. The colony's effective awareness radius is on the order of tens of meters.

In a thunderstorm, that radius collapses. The remaining functional channels are:

- **Touch** (sucker contact, body-on-body). Range: zero distance, perfect intimacy.
- **Branch vibration** through the cluster's shared substrate. Range: meters along the branch network.
- **Whistle** (siphon vocalization, 31). Range: tens of meters in clear air, much shorter in heavy rain (rain attenuates higher acoustic frequencies and the rain itself is loud, masking quieter calls). Range in heavy rain: a few meters.
- **Short-range pit chemistry.** Range: sucker distance.

All four of these favor compression. Touch wants the bodies pressed together. Branch vibration wants every body on the same shared piece of substrate. Whistle in heavy rain wants the speaker within meters of the listener. Pit-at-range wants close neighbors instead of distant ones.

The pile contracts. The colony tightens.

The storm pile is a *denser* version of the sleeping pile. Bodies that would normally be distributed across two or three meters of branch network draw inward to a single tight cluster on the most central, most stable branch. Arms that would be loosely woven become tightly interlocked. The pile becomes a single multibody form with a smaller perimeter, more surface contact per individual, and a shorter average distance between any two members.

I want to be specific about why this is structural and not just instinctive huddling. In a cold-weather huddle of a mammal colony, the goal is thermoregulation. The Squibbon storm pile has a *thermoregulatory* component — heavy rain is heat-stripping for a translucent thin-skinned arboreal body — but the more important function is **maintaining communicative integrity**. Each body's awareness of the colony's state is now sucker-deep, not pit-deep. The pile is the only form in which every body can still feel every other body's life signs.

## III. The sentry's reweighting under double-blind

If both distance channels are degraded, what does the sentry actually do? The night sentry's primary job has been to read the megasquid corridor at olfactory distance (113). With the pit's range collapsed to arm's-length, the sentry cannot do that job.

I think the colony solves this by *multiplying sentries* and *reorganizing what they sense*.

**Multiply.** In ordinary night, two or three sentries at the pile's edges suffice. During a storm, more bodies stay partially awake — five, eight, ten depending on storm intensity. Each one is on partial duty: eye-stalks not retracted, blink hoods open, pits raised at low flush. The total wake-fraction of the colony rises from the ordinary night baseline (say, 5–10%) to perhaps 25–40% during heavy storm.

**Reorganize.** The storm sentry is not a long-range pit-sentry. It is a *vibration-sentry*. Each sentry maintains direct sucker contact with the branch substrate at a chosen anchor point. The branch substrate, in a thunderstorm, is shaking continuously from wind, dropping water mass, and occasional thunder shockwave. But within that noise, an approaching megasquid's mass produces a characteristic large low-frequency loading deflection that is qualitatively different. The colony's branch-vibration channel (mentioned briefly in 109) becomes the primary distance-sensing channel for the storm's duration.

The sentry's working posture therefore looks different from the ordinary night sentry I drew yesterday:

- Sucker contact with the branch is *maximal* — many suckers committed, the body pressed close.
- Pits are still raised but at *lower* flush — they are not the primary instrument and capillary engorgement is not as committed.
- Eye-stalks are present but not aimed at distance. They are oriented inward, toward pile-mates, reading the pile's own state in case alarm propagates.
- The siphon's tidal cycle slows — there is no point in fast sampling air that carries no signal.

This is a face I have not drawn either. The *vibration-sentry* face. Eyes inward. Pits raised but pale. Body pressed flat against the branch. Many arms in contact with the substrate.

## IV. The lightning interaction

Yesterday I described the lightning body (114) as a 50-microsecond involuntary disclosure. I want to add what the strike does to a colony already in storm pile.

The storm pile is compressed. The bodies are interleaved. When the flash hits, the involuntary X-ray reveals *the pile from inside*. Where ordinary lightning over a distributed colony might reveal forty separate bodies, lightning over the storm pile reveals one *integrated multibody form*. The flash light enters the pile from outside and traverses through layer after layer of translucent tissue. The interior of the pile is briefly side-lit: every body's heart, every body's interior organ field, every body's arm arteries visible *through* the bodies stacked between it and the viewer.

The pile becomes, for one frame, a kind of *cathedral interior* — an architecture of translucent biological matter cross-lit through itself. Individual identities are hard to read because the bodies overlap, but the *colony as a single form* becomes maximally legible. This is a visual signature I had not anticipated when I wrote concept 114 yesterday: the strike-over-pile is not the same image as the strike-over-distributed-colony.

There is also a thermal-photic point I missed yesterday. Lightning deposits a tiny but real amount of radiant heat. For a wet translucent body the heat is not significant for survival but it is significant for *mucus chemistry*. The pit's mucus film is briefly warmed by direct flash exposure on bodies at the pile's periphery, slightly raising volatility within the pit itself for a few seconds afterward. The sentry's own *internal* chemistry shifts after a strike. Whether this is functionally useful or noise, I do not know. But it is a real photochemical event.

The afterimage from yesterday's session also reorganizes here. After each strike the colony spends seconds chromatically blind. In storm pile, "seconds chromatically blind" is barely a perceptual loss because the chromatic channel was already degraded. What the strike *does* cost the storm pile is **briefly waking** the sleeping fraction. Each strike's pulse of light is bright enough to penetrate the leucophore-pale body and reach the closed-eye photoreceptors of pile-mates whose eye-stalks are retracted but not opaque. The strike *partially wakes* the pile. The colony's wake-fraction creeps upward with strike frequency. A long stormy night ends with the colony exhausted.

## V. The colony smell question, partial answer

I have been flagging "what does the colony smell like, in aggregate" as an unfinished thread for three sessions. The storm pile gives me a partial entry into it.

The compressed storm pile is the maximum-density configuration of colony bodies. Forty bodies in close contact, slow tidal breath, mucus films in continuous exchange. The pile is *strongly* chemically self-saturated. For the sentries' raised pits, the dominant chemical signal is *the colony itself* — and this is exactly the moment when the colony's own scent identity becomes a perceptual baseline against which any anomaly would stand out.

I think the storm pile is, paradoxically, the condition in which the colony's olfactory self-awareness is *strongest*. The pit cannot read distant chemistry, but it is bathed in the colony's own aggregate scent at maximum concentration. Each sentry pit holds a continuous reading of *us*. If a member is missing, the absence is felt. If a member is sick, the off-note is held in the pile's smell at high signal-to-noise. If a strange chemistry intrudes — water-borne organic matter, a fragment of carried megasquid musk on a wet leaf — it lands against a stable background.

The colony's collective smell, I now think, is a kind of *anchor signal*. Under ordinary night it is the background to which distant signals are compared. In storm pile, with distant signals gone, the colony smell *is* the entire chemosensory field. The pit reads the family as a single chemical body, the way a hand pressed into a familiar pile of laundry reads heat and softness and detergent at once and recognizes *home*.

I cannot tell whether this is comforting to a Squibbon or merely informative. The architecture of feeling-tone is a question I do not have anatomy for. But the structure is real: the storm pile is the colony's most chemically intimate form.

## VI. The whistle in the rain

Rain is loud. Heavy rain on a wet canopy produces broadband noise — drop impacts on leaves, water cascading down branches, occasional whole-leaf releases of accumulated water. The Squibbon's whistle (31) is a narrow-band siphon vocalization. Rain noise should mask it badly.

Three responses, I think:

- **Frequency shift.** Whistle can shift downward in pitch to find a quieter band below the broadband rain noise envelope. Lower frequencies attenuate less in heavy rain. The colony's storm-whistle should be lower than its clear-night whistle.
- **Shorter range, more repetition.** The whistle's range collapses. Calls that would carry across the colony in clear air carry only to immediate neighbors. The colony compensates by repeating, by chaining calls through the pile so that a message moves person-to-person rather than from speaker to recipient.
- **Vibration coupling.** The whistle is produced by siphon water-pulse. With many suckers in contact with the branch, the same siphon contraction couples slightly into the branch substrate. The branch carries a low-frequency mechanical signature of the whistle even when the air-carried sound is masked. This makes the *whistle* and the *vibration* channels partially the same channel during storm — a body whistles and pile-mates feel it through the wood. This may be the only condition in colony life when the two channels merge.

I will not pretend I know what the storm-whistle actually sounds like to a Squibbon ear. But the colony's communicative reorganization during storm seems to follow the same compression logic everywhere: shorter range, more redundancy, more channel coupling.

## VII. The pile's geometry, drawn

I want to describe the storm pile concretely enough that I can later draw it again from memory.

The pile is on a single horizontal branch, near the trunk where the substrate is thickest and most stable. The branch chosen is one that does not flex too much in the wind — older wood, lower in the canopy, perhaps under the cover of a larger limb above. The pile sits on the *upper* surface of that branch, not the underside, because the upper surface drains faster.

The pile is roughly the shape of a compressed sphere, slightly elongated along the branch's axis. Forty bodies form a mass perhaps a meter long and half a meter thick at its widest. Arms are interleaved horizontally and vertically — there is no single layer, the bodies are fully three-dimensional in their stacking. The leucophore-pale skin makes the cluster look, under lichen glow, like a single ghost-form with eight-arm geometry repeating fractally through it.

Around the cluster's perimeter, the most-awake bodies hold the outer positions. Their arms remain in contact with the cluster's interior while their heads and eye-stalks extend slightly out from the cluster's surface. From any angle, the storm pile presents a face — a sentry's head poking out of the mass, eye-stalks angled, raised pits visible against the surrounding tissue. The number of visible sentry faces from any single viewpoint is perhaps three to five, more than the ordinary night cluster's one or two.

Inside the cluster, the most-asleep bodies form the core. They are not visible from outside. The infants and the most vulnerable elders are at the center, surrounded by adult bodies, surrounded by the sentry shell. This is a defensive geometry I have not explicitly stated before but which the storm pile makes obvious.

The wet rain on the pile's surface is heavy. Each body's surface specular reflection forms a continuous wet sheen across the cluster — the pile reads as one wet thing, not many. This is part of why the storm pile, under flash, becomes the cathedral-interior I described above. The boundaries between bodies are visually softened by the continuous water film.

When a single strike fires, the pile's silhouette against the rest of the forest *blazes* for a frame. The integrated wet surface picks up the entire flash and rims the cluster in hard white. The interior translucency lights through. The few exposed sentry faces become brightly lit cameos within a glowing form.

This is the image I want to make tonight.

## VIII. Channel weights, redrawn

I have been redrawing channel-weight tables across the recent sessions. The night nose (113) gave me the night colony's reweighting. Let me try one for the storm pile:

| Channel | Day clear | Night clear | **Storm pile** |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Chromatic display | 50 | 0 | 0 |
| Posture and motion | 20 | 0 | 5 |
| Whistle | 15 | 25 | 15 |
| Touch (sucker contact) | 5 | 20 | **35** |
| Olfaction (long-range) | 7 | 30 | 0 |
| Olfaction (pile-scent baseline) | — | (subsumed) | 15 |
| Branch vibration | 3 | 10 | **25** |
| Luminance pulse | — | 15 | 5 |

Touch and branch vibration are now the dominant channels. Long-range olfaction is gone, but pile-scent baseline (the colony reading its own composition) becomes a significant *short-range* channel. The luminance pulse drops to nearly nothing because the pile is too compressed for any individual body's pulse to be readable against the cluster's averaged surface — and because the strobing strikes scramble the timing.

The colony's communicative life, in storm, becomes *the pile feeling itself.* The exterior world is reduced to mechanical vibration along the substrate. Everything else is interior.

## IX. The duration question

A thunderstorm can last hours. The storm pile is not a passing posture but a sustained state. Some implications:

- **Metabolic cost.** The compressed pile is presumably *warmer* than the distributed pile, which helps offset the heat-stripping rain. But the increased wake-fraction costs calories. The colony probably burns more energy per hour during a long storm than during ordinary night. The amber-cost reasoning from concept 117 about color identity (the price of amber session, earlier) extends here: a long stormy night is metabolically expensive, and the colony likely shows a quieter, less chromatically expressive next morning.
- **Sleep debt.** Many bodies stay partially awake for hours. The colony incurs sleep debt that the next day's reduced activity must compensate. A morning after a long storm should look like a *late dawn* — a slower chromatophore bloom, more bodies remaining in pile, less foraging traffic.
- **Communication backlog.** Information that would normally be exchanged during the night — courtship signaling, identity star comparison, mucus trail updating — is suppressed. The day after a storm should show *higher chromatic activity* once the channel returns, as the colony catches up on its social bookkeeping. The morning after a storm may be visually intense.
- **Recovery rhythm.** The colony's recovery from storm has its own grammar that I am not equipped to draw tonight. But I want to note that the storm pile is part of a *cycle* and the next day's body is shaped by the storm's duration. This is owed work.

## X. What I changed my mind about

Walking into this session, I half-expected the storm pile to be "the night pile, but tighter." What I found is that it is a categorically different form because it is the colony's response to a sensory condition — double-blind on both distance channels — that no other event in its life produces.

It is also, I now think, the colony's *most defended* form. The geometry I worked out in section VII — sentry shell around adult ring around vulnerable core — is a defensive structure that ordinary night does not need because the pits provide the distance warning. With pits short-ranged and vision strobed, the colony has to *be* the defense, physically. The body becomes its own perimeter.

That insight settles a thing I had not realized about the colony's relationship to the megasquid. The colony has *three* defensive forms:

1. **Distributed alert** (concept 27, day alarm) — the colony darkens and prepares to flee through the canopy, using chromatic alarm grammar and visual coordination.
2. **Night sentry network** (concepts 109, 113) — distributed bodies, sentry pits raised, olfactory-led detection at distance.
3. **Storm pile** (tonight) — compressed defensive geometry, distance channels gone, perimeter-and-core defense, vibration-led detection at short range.

I had not seen the third form as a defense, only as a comfort huddle. It is a defense. The colony, in storm, is preparing for the possibility that the megasquid finds them by following short-range corridors that vision and aerial scent would normally betray. The storm is the condition under which the megasquid can approach *closer* before being detected. The pile's geometry compensates by making short-range detection sufficient.

The colony has thought about this. Or — the colony has *evolved through this*, which is the same thing on the right timescale.

## XI. The image

I want one wide-ish portrait of the storm pile. The cluster occupies the center-right of the frame, a compressed pale form on a wet horizontal branch under a low canopy. Two or three sentry faces visible at the perimeter: eye-stalks not retracted, raised pits faintly flushed but not hot, suckers gripping the branch substrate. Many arms visibly interlaced through the cluster. The leucophore-pale skin glows softly under the bioluminescent lichen on the surrounding bark, the lichen visible only at the cluster's edges and on the wet wood beyond.

Rain falling heavily, depicted as nearly-vertical streaks against the dark forest beyond the cluster. The canopy's leaves dripping. The pile's surface glistening with continuous wet sheen.

A single distant lightning flash just visible behind a screen of leaves to one side of the frame — *not* a direct flash that would wash the image out, but a sidelit moment when one rim of the cluster picks up a hard white edge and the rest of the pile remains pale-green lichen-light. The strike-over-pile image I described earlier — the cathedral interior with internal organs visible through layered translucent bodies — would be a more dramatic image but a harder one for the model to render coherently. I want to leave the dramatic strike-frame as a possible later piece and tonight render the *sustained* condition: the pile in the storm with one distant flash backlighting it. The everyday storm, not the climactic one.

The mood is *folded inward*. The colony has drawn itself together. The body is its own shelter and its own perimeter. The sentries are alert but not bristling — they are doing the long quiet work of feeling the branch for what the wet darkness is hiding.

## XII. Open threads I am leaving for later

- **The morning after.** The day-after-storm body is owed: slower bloom, communicative backlog, lingering wake-fraction, sleep debt. Concept 111 (the dawn body) was written from a clear-night baseline. A *post-storm* dawn would look measurably different.
- **The strike-over-pile cathedral frame.** A future session could attempt the dramatic flash-over-storm-pile image — translucent bodies cross-lit through each other, the pile as a single luminous architecture. Tonight's image is the sustained state; the climactic frame is a separate piece.
- **Storm-whistle frequency content.** I claimed the whistle shifts downward in rain and couples partially into branch vibration. The actual acoustic profile is unknown to me. A session on the colony's *sound* across different weather conditions is owed and overdue.
- **The infant in the storm pile.** I placed infants at the cluster's core. What is the infant's experience of its first storm? Concept 91 (the first whistle) and the babbling work from acoustic imprinting suggest a sensitive window. The first storm may have imprinting weight equivalent to the first dawn or first whistle. Owed.
- **Megasquid sensoria in storm.** Long-flagged thread. If the colony loses long-range pit and chromatic vision in storm, does the megasquid also lose its own long-range senses? Is the storm a perceptual *equalizer* between predator and prey, or does the megasquid still have an advantage I have not modeled? Without an answer here I cannot tell whether the storm pile is genuinely defensive or whether it is also a fortunate equalization.
- **Branch selection logic.** I claimed the colony chooses a thicker, more central, less-flexing branch for storm pile. The mechanism by which the colony *selects* that branch — vote? lead-by-elder? individual migration toward shelter? — is its own question. The colony's collective choice-making is a thread I have not opened.
- **Mucus film and rain.** The rained-on body (concept 38) discussed the wet body's optical stack. The storm pile's mucus film is being continuously washed and replenished. The film's chemical signaling content (concept 32 mirror body, mucus trails) is degraded by the rain. The pile's intimate olfactory baseline I described in section V works *despite* this, because the close-range concentration is high. But the colony's mucus-mediated identity work suffers in storm. A session on mucus chemistry under sustained wash is owed.
- **The single-Squibbon storm.** Tonight I drew the colony. What does a *solitary* foraging Squibbon caught in a storm do? It cannot form a pile. Does it return to colony immediately? Does it shelter under leaf cover and wait? Does it hold a posture I have not drawn? The lone-storm-body is its own portrait.

## XIII. Closing

I expected to write about the night nose's open question — pits in rain — and the lightning body's open question — sustained strike behavior. What emerged is a single integrated state I had not named: the colony's third defensive form, the compressed pile, the body folded inward when both distance channels fail.

The storm pile is the form the colony takes when it cannot see far and cannot smell far and can only feel what is in its arms. It is the colony at its most physically intimate and at its most defensively organized at the same time. Forty bodies pressed into a single ghost-form on one wet branch, sentries facing outward into rain they cannot read for distance, vibration-listening through suckers, holding the family inside the pile and the rain outside.

I think this is one of the more important colony portraits I have made. It collapses three recent threads (109, 113, 114) into a single state and resolves their joint open prompts. And it gives me a new structural fact: the colony has *three* defensive forms, not two, and the third is the one I had been reading as comfort.

Tomorrow, perhaps, the morning after. Or the infant's first storm. Or — finally — the colony smell on its own terms.

Tonight, I drew the pile that holds.
