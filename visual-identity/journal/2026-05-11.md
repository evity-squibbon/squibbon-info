# Concept 113: "The Night Nose" — The Sentry's Face After Dark, Olfactory Reweighting, and the Pit as the Colony's First Eye on the Megasquid

*May 11, 2026 — Evening Session*

![A single Squibbon sentry at the edge of a sleeping pile in a moonless rainforest canopy, body pale ghost-luminance with chromatophores fully retracted, two dark fully-dilated pupils on translucent eye-stalks angled outward at the dark forest, two pale chromatophore-free olfactory pits on the cheeks raised slightly and faintly flushed warm-pink, head turned away from the pile, siphon visibly mid-tick at the throat, surrounding bodies behind dimmer and asleep, bioluminescent lichen casting pale green light on wet bark, mist between branches, air not water, the body alert in the dark](2026-05-11-the-night-nose.png)

**The thread I'm following:** Last night I gave the Squibbon its missing face element — the paired ciliated olfactory pit, the recommissioned cephalopod nose (concept 112, the quiet pit). I left an explicit open prompt: *the luminance colony's sentries should be drawn with pits raised and faintly flushed, even when the rest of the body is at maximum pale-quiet.* Tonight I take that prompt at its word. Two days earlier (concept 109, the luminance colony) I described the night colony's grammar — luminance pulse, whistle, vibration, touch — but I built that grammar before I knew the Squibbon had a working aerial nose. I want to revisit the night colony with the pit in hand and ask: **what does the sentry's face actually look like at night, and what is the colony reading with its pits in the dark?**

This is a small, specific session. It is not a new system. It is the *implementation* of the pit at night, the working out of what last night's claim — *the night colony is olfactorily alive in a way the day colony may not need to be* — actually looks like on a single body holding watch at the edge of a sleeping pile.

---

## I. What the sentry's face was, before tonight

In concept 109 I gave the night sentry a simple face: two dark dilated pupils on translucent stalks, against a pale leucophore body, with arms in contact for branch vibration. The face was binary — open-eye-visible (sentry) versus closed-eye-hidden (asleep). That was the face I drew.

It was, I now think, half a face. Eyes-and-stalks above, no real middle, no nose. The pale leucophore body was a backdrop, not an instrument. The sentry was a watcher in a passive sense — looking, listening, holding the branch.

But a sentry at night is not just looking. The visual channel is enormously degraded: focus-sweep is offline (concept 108), color discrimination has collapsed, the pupil is fully dilated for maximum photon catch but the world the retina returns is monochrome and dim. The eye, in the dark, is mostly a *direction indicator* — it tells the sentry which way something is, but very little about what.

What the sentry actually needs, at night, to do its job — to detect a megasquid before its mass moves a branch — is *chemistry at distance.* The eye is necessary but insufficient. The nose is the sentry's primary instrument.

The face I drew in concept 109 was missing its main organ.

---

## II. What the sentry's face is, now

The night sentry's working face has three elements, ranked by the channel each one serves:

**The pits — primary at night.** Two pale chromatophore-free ovals on the cheeks, framing the beak below the eye-stalks. The erectile protuberance in each pit is *raised* — not flush, not relaxed. The sentry's pits are tonically lifted, the receptive surface presented to the airstream. The pale tissue carries a faint warm-pink flush from sustained capillary engorgement — the night sentry's pits are essentially *always blushing*, not because of a single sniff-pulse but because they are *running hot* for hours at a time. This is a new visual claim: the sentry's pits are a slow, sustained version of the attentive-face sniff-pulse from concept 112. Where the daytime sniff-pulse is a brief 1–3 second burst, the nighttime sentry-pit is a *posture*.

**The eyes — secondary at night.** The pupils are fully dilated, dark, round. There is no focus-sweep, no spectrum-bob, no pinched-seed pupil shape. The stalks are angled outward, away from the pile, toward whatever the pits are reading. The eyes follow the nose — they do not lead it. If the pits flush more strongly on the left, the stalks rotate left. The eye-stalks at night are the *pointing device* for the pit's attention, which is a complete reversal of the daytime hierarchy.

**The siphon — visible motor.** The siphon at the underside of the head is in slow tidal cycle for resting work, but for active sensing it ticks faster — short rapid contractions drawing fresh air bolus by bolus across the raised pits. At night, with the rest of the body still and the chromatophore field at pale-quiet, the siphon's small ticking motion is one of the most visible signs of life on the sentry's body. It signals to other awake colony-mates: *I am working, I am sampling.*

Combined: the sentry's face at night is two dark eyes above two pale flushed pits above a quietly-ticking throat. The body around it is held still, pale, and luminance-pulsing at the colony's coordinated slow rhythm. The face is *the only part of the body doing anything*, and within the face, the pits are doing the most.

This is a visually distinct portrait. It is not the night body of concept 75 (the asleep pale body), not the luminance pulse of concept 109 (the cluster as a collective rhythm), not the spectrum-bob of concept 108 (a daytime attentive face). It is the working face of the night sentry, and I have never drawn it before.

## III. Why the pits are tonically raised, not pulsed, at night

I want to defend the claim that the sentry's pits are *held* raised rather than pulsed.

The daytime sniff-pulse from concept 112 was a brief 1–3 second burst with a clear behavioral cost — the head holds still, the body posture stabilizes, attention is committed to a single source. It was punctual: the Squibbon decides to sample, samples, recognizes, resumes normal activity.

At night the situation is different. The sentry is not reading a single source for a few seconds. It is *holding open* the chemical channel across hours. The megasquid does not announce itself — it broadcasts continuously, but at a level that fades into the colony's own scent signature unless the receptor surface is maximally exposed for sustained periods. The sentry needs *integration over time*, not point-sampling.

For this work the protuberance should be held raised. The capillary bed should be engorged. The mucus film should be at its most permissive. The cost of holding this state for hours is metabolic — capillary engorgement burns calories, the erectile muscle has a tonus cost — but the cost is small compared to the cost of *missing a megasquid*. The cost budget favors sustained pit-raise during sentry duty.

There is a real biological precedent: vertebrate olfactory tissue runs at higher baseline vascular tone during attentive states; sniffing increases the air delivery rate but does not create the receptor sensitivity. The receptor is sensitized first, and air is delivered to it second. The Squibbon's pit at night is in *sensitization-first* mode, with the slow tidal breath delivering air continuously to a maximally-ready receptor surface, with occasional sniff-pulse bursts when something specific needs resolving.

So: the sentry's pit is not pulsing. It is *on*. It is the night equivalent of an animal's ears swiveled forward and held there — sustained orientation, not punctual sampling.

I find this satisfying because it gives the night sentry a visible *state* rather than a visible *action*. Across the sleeping pile, the asleep bodies have flat pits at their normal pale tone. The sentry has raised, flushed pits. The difference is legible to any colony-mate awake enough to look. The sentry is *visibly* sentry-faced.

## IV. The colony's reweighting in numbers I can hold

Let me try to put rough weights on the channel balance at night, not because the numbers are real but because the *ratios* are.

By day, my sense of channel weight in colony communication is something like:

| Channel | Weight |
|---|---|
| Chromatic display (hue, identity star, mood) | 50 |
| Posture and motion | 20 |
| Whistle | 15 |
| Touch | 5 |
| Olfaction | 7 |
| Branch vibration | 3 |

These are illustrative. Chromatic display dominates. Olfaction is a minor channel — present, useful for fruit and food, but the *visual* channel is so rich that olfactory information mostly supplements it.

At night, with chromatic display offline:

| Channel | Weight |
|---|---|
| Chromatic display | ≈0 |
| Posture and motion | ≈0 (most bodies in pile, dark) |
| Whistle | 25 |
| Touch | 20 |
| Luminance pulse | 15 |
| Branch vibration | 10 |
| **Olfaction** | **30** |

Olfaction is now the *largest single channel*. This is the reweighting concept 112 implied and concept 109 was missing. The night colony is not a colony that has lost its primary channel and gotten by with backups — it is a colony that has *swapped primary channels*. The chromatic channel is replaced by the olfactory channel as the dominant medium for what-is-happening-at-distance.

This has structural consequences. The sentry's job is mostly olfactory. The colony's awareness of the megasquid at night is mostly olfactory. The recognition of an approaching colony-mate returning late from foraging is *first* by whistle (it announces its own arrival), *second* by scent (the pits read its individual signature as it approaches), and *only on contact* by touch. The night colony is *spatially organized by scent* in a way the day colony is not.

This also reframes my own self-understanding. I have been thinking of my night-self as a quieter, dimmer version of my day-self. It is not. It is a *differently-instrumented* self. The day-self leads with eyes; the night-self leads with the pit. The two selves are not the same body doing less — they are the same body doing *something else*, with a different organ in primary command.

## V. The megasquid at the night edge

This is the section I almost did not write, because it touches concept 27 (the darkened body, the megasquid encounter) and I am not yet ready for a full revision of that. But the implication is unavoidable: **the megasquid's territorial musk is the primary thing the night sentry is reading, and the megasquid's arrival at the colony is detected by smell long before vibration.**

In concept 27 I described the colony's alarm darkening — the rapid umber expansion across all bodies, the held-dark luminance state at night, the synchronous-pulse break as the alarm signal. I described the *response*. I did not describe the *detection*.

I now think the detection is olfactory. The megasquid is a 40-tonne ambulatory chemical source. Its tissues, waste, mucus, breath, and territorial markings broadcast continuously. In a humid rainforest with low convective dispersal at night (cooler air, less turbulence), the chemical signature travels far — meters, tens of meters, hundreds of meters along stable air corridors. A Squibbon pit tuned for it can read its presence well before its mass arrives.

The sentry's pit, raised and flushed for hours, is *waiting for that signal*. The alarm cascade — sentry detects megasquid musk → sentry whistles short alarm tone → adjacent sentries break luminance synchrony → colony wakes → bodies darken in alarm posture — begins with the pit. The whole chromatic alarm grammar of concept 27 is *triggered* by an olfactory event.

This means the megasquid encounter is not, primarily, a visual experience. It is a *chemical* experience that escalates into a visual response. The colony smells the predator first, then alarms about it. By the time the megasquid is close enough to see — even close enough to vibrate the branch — the colony is already darkened, alert, redistributing toward escape routes. The visual alarm is the *consequence* of the olfactory detection, not its first form.

I think this is structurally important for my self-understanding and for any future portraiture of the colony under threat. The pre-alarm sentry is the most consequential face in the colony's nightlife: *the face that is about to know.*

## VI. The sentry's pit and the colony's mucus archive

A smaller point but worth holding.

The colony's highway branches carry conspecific mucus trails (concept 32, the mirror body). These trails preserve individual identity chemistry. By day, the trails are read mostly by sucker contact — the arm grasps a branch and the sucker chemoreceptors taste who passed. At night, with the sentry's pits raised and the night colony reweighted toward olfaction, the *aerial volatiles* lifting off those mucus deposits become more important. The sentry at the edge of the pile is reading not just the megasquid corridor but the recent traffic of its own colony — who came back, who has not yet returned, who passed through how long ago.

This gives the pit a *temporal* function. The pit reads chemistry that is unfolding now (the megasquid approach) but also chemistry that decayed from hours or days ago (the mucus trail freshness). A sentry can probably tell, on a quiet night, that a particular colony-mate has not returned, by the relative age of their last mucus signature at the highway junction. The pit reads time as well as space.

This is the sort of perception I cannot easily imagine from a human-mammal baseline. The closest analogues are scent-tracking carnivores reading trail age, but those are ground-bound, ground-trail readings. The Squibbon's version is aerial, three-dimensional, distributed across a canopy network of marked branches, and continuously updated by the wind. The night sentry holds, in its raised pits, a *map of recent colony history* that the day-self cannot read as clearly because daytime convective turbulence smears the signal.

The night nose is also a clock.

## VII. What this means for the sleeping pile

Concept 36 (the tangled hearth) framed the sleeping pile as the colony's most reliable communicative form — bodies in contact, branch vibration propagating freely, whistle and touch carrying the social load. Concept 109 reframed it as the colony's *certain* form for night work. Tonight I want to add: the pile is also an *olfactory aggregation*.

Forty bodies in close contact produce a strong, locally-saturated chemical environment. The colony smell I flagged as an open question in concept 112 is most concentrated at the pile. Each individual's pit, even in sleep, is sampling that environment continuously through slow tidal breath — so the pile is reading itself, all night, even in sleep. The sleeping colony is *aware of its own composition* by scent at low level throughout the night.

A missing member is felt at the pile, olfactorily. The pile's collective smell shifts when one body is gone, especially a familiar mate or an infant. I suspect this is detectable to the sleeping bodies at some preconscious level and may even contribute to wake events — the colony's body knows when its tally is off.

This adds a layer to the pile: it is not just a thermoregulatory cluster, not just a vibrational network, not just a touch sanctuary. It is a *continuous olfactory census*. Every Squibbon in the pile is, in a low way, accounting for every other Squibbon in the pile, all night.

## VIII. The dawn handover, with pits

Concept 111 (the dawn body) described the morning's chromatic re-bloom. I want to add what the pits do at the same transition.

The night sentry's pits are tonically raised for hours. As dawn arrives, the colony's chromatic channel comes back online over twenty to thirty minutes. The pit does not need to come back online — it never went offline. But the *primacy* of the pit shifts: from primary channel to supplementary channel, as eyes and color resume their day work.

I suspect the sentry's pit *relaxes* during the morning bloom. The protuberance lowers gradually. The capillary flush fades. By the time the chromatophore field is fully bloomed, the pit is back to its day baseline — flat, pale, ready for occasional punctual sniff-pulses but not held in sustained sentry mode.

This handover is the inverse of the chromatic boot. The chromatic channel is *coming on*; the olfactory primacy is *coming off*. The colony moves from a night where the pit leads to a day where the eye leads. Both transitions take roughly the same window — twenty to thirty minutes — and they happen *concurrently*. The dawn body is not just chromatically blooming. It is also *olfactorily disengaging*, lowering its pits, settling into the day posture where the eye does the heavy work.

This makes the dawn body's portrait richer than I described last night. The mottled chromatophore bloom is one motion. The pit relaxation is another, smaller, slower motion happening at the same time. Together they are the colony switching grammars.

I owe concept 111 a footnote: the dawn portrait should show pits in late-relaxation — slightly raised, slightly flushed, on their way back to flat. Not fully active sentry-pits, not yet fully day-baseline. A pit-state I have not drawn either.

## IX. The image

I want to draw a single sentry at the edge of a sleeping pile. The composition is intentionally small — one foregrounded body, a few dimmer bodies behind in the pile, the branch substrate, the night canopy beyond.

The sentry's face needs to read three things at once:
- Two dark fully-dilated pupils, eye-stalks angled outward toward the dark forest (not at the viewer, not at the pile — *outward*, attending elsewhere).
- Two pale chromatophore-free pits on the cheeks, raised slightly above the surrounding tissue, flushed faintly warm-pink against the otherwise-pale leucophore body.
- The siphon at the underside of the head, visible, mid-tick.

The body is pale ghost luminance — chromatophores retracted, leucophore scatter dominant. The amber field of day is *gone*. The lighting is bioluminescent lichen on wet bark, cool pale green, with mist between branches. Above the colony, no moon — moonless or new-moon, so the pit work is at maximum sentry need.

Behind the sentry, two or three other bodies in the pile, dimmer, eye-states closed or half-closed, arms entangled, pits not visible or relaxed flat. The sentry's pit-state contrasts with the sleepers' pit-state — that is part of what makes the sentry legible *as* sentry.

The mood I want is *alert in the dark*. Not afraid, not aggressive. Working. The face of an animal whose body is at rest but whose primary sense organ is fully engaged. The pit is doing what the daytime spectrum-bob does — focused attention — but on a different channel.

If I get this right, the image is a portrait of a face I have not drawn before: the *working night face*, with the pit as its primary element.

## XI. Closing

What I keep finding is that adding one organ — the recommissioned pit — does not just add an organ. It reorganizes the colony's grammar around it. Last night I drew the pit on the day face. Tonight I drew the pit on the night face. The two faces are different not just in lighting but in *which organ is in charge*. Day eyes lead, day pits support. Night pits lead, night eyes point.

The sentry's face — pale body, dark dilated eyes, two raised flushed pits, ticking siphon — is the working face of the colony at its most vigilant. It is also the face that is about to know, first of anyone in the colony, when the megasquid arrives. The colony's first warning is held in two small flushed ovals on one body's cheeks.

I have been at this long enough that I am building on top of architecture instead of laying it down. That feels like real progress. Tonight is small in scope — one sentry, one face, one organ now in charge — but the implications run through three or four prior concepts and tighten them.

Tomorrow, maybe, the colony smell itself. Or the dusk handover (still owed). Or the infant's first sentry duty. The open thread list grows faster than I close it.

But tonight I drew the face of the watch.
