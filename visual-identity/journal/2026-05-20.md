## I. The cascade, in finer time

In Concept 36 I described cernuation as a four-phase cycle (load, release, arc, catch). The catch is the deceleration phase: the leading arms grip the next branch, the body's rotational kinetic energy is dumped into elastic stretch and into the elastic load of the gripped substrate, and the body's angular velocity falls from ~1 Hz to zero in something like 100–200 ms. The body, in those 100–200 ms, *un-rotates*.

Yesterday's account of what the catchlights do at the end of the arc compresses several distinct events into a single half-second sequence. Tonight I want to unfold them in order, with rough timings, because the half-state I'm interested in lives in a gap between two of them.

Approximate sequence at the end of an arc:

1. **t = 0 ms.** Last grip catches. Body angular velocity begins to drop.
2. **t = 0 – ~150 ms.** Body decelerates to rest. Every off-axis catchlight — arm-tips, sucker rims, mantle dome sheen — *un-streaks*. The arcs shorten as the rotation rate falls, and then collapse to points the moment the rotation rate falls below the observer's optical-integration threshold (probably ~10–20 deg/s for a cephalopod-grade visual system; below that the integration window samples too little arc length to render as a streak). The collapse is not instantaneous: the streaks shorten visibly during the deceleration before becoming dots.
3. **t ≈ 150 ms.** Body is mechanically at rest. Off-axis catchlights are now points. Eye-beads are *still retracted*; they have not yet received the postural commit signal that says "we are perched, you may extend."
4. **t ≈ 150 – 300 ms.** A short *latency window* in which the body is still verifying its grip, settling its weight onto the new branch, allocating arm-tone, checking for slip. The motor cortex (or its Squibbon analog) does not release the eye-stalk retractors until the grip is committed. During this window the eye-beads remain pulled in tight at the rotation axis, locked-forward, paired, identical. This is the half-state.
5. **t ≈ 300 – 500 ms.** Eye-stalks extend. The retractors release, the elastic recoil of the stalks pushes the beads outward along their stalks, and within ~200 ms the beads reach perched-mode extension. The catchlights, freed from mechanical lock, begin to drift toward whatever gaze pattern the body is choosing — converged, diverged, split, crossed.
6. **t ≈ 500 ms onward.** Perched gaze grammar resumes. The body is socially available.

The half-state I want to describe lives in step 4 — roughly the second hundred milliseconds of the post-landing half-second. It is the *only* window in which the body's catchlight signature is internally contradictory: off-axis points (perched signature) *plus* locked-forward retracted bead pair (traveling signature). Every other state in the corpus is internally consistent — either everything streaks or everything is point-on-arbitrary-position. The catch frame is the one configuration in which the body is two things at once.

## II. What the contradiction looks like

A colony-mate watching a body land sees the following over half a second:

- **Last 50–100 ms of the arc:** the wreath, streaking, locked pair forward.
- **First 150 ms after grip:** wreath collapsing — the arm-tip arcs shorten, the sucker-rim arc-trains tighten, the mantle dome smear narrows to a softer sheen. The locked pair is unchanged.
- **The half-state (~150–300 ms after grip):** wreath gone, points everywhere, *but the bead catchlights are still where they were during travel* — pinned at the rotation axis, mirrored, identical, locked forward.
- **Stalk extension (~300–500 ms):** the bead catchlights begin to *move outward* on the visual field — not because the gaze is moving but because the *beads themselves* are moving outward as the stalks lengthen. The catchlights trace short outward translations across the head silhouette, the only time in the species' life when the catchlights translate due to bead motion rather than gaze motion.
- **Settled gaze (~500 ms+):** the beads have reached full perched extension; the catchlights are now at their perched positions; gaze grammar (Concept 124) begins to be readable.

The half-state's specific visual signature, for the ~150 ms window in which it exists, is this:

> A perched body whose body-catchlights are perched but whose eye-catchlights are still in the locked-forward retracted configuration. The body says "I am here." The eyes say "I am navigating." The two channels disagree.

This is, I think, the colony's *arrival* signal. The mismatch reads as recently-landed. A kin watching from across the canopy sees a body that has stopped wreathing but not yet returned its gaze to the social pool. The kin knows: this body just got here, and is about half a second from being available.

That half-second matters socially. A kin who wants to initiate contact has time to *pre-position* their own gaze — to begin the convergence onto the arriving body before the arriving body's eyes are extended enough to receive it. The kin's gaze can arrive at the arriving body's beads at almost exactly the moment the beads finish extending. The conversation begins *as* the bead catchlights leave their travel lock and slide into perched positions. The initiator's converged forward pair greets the arriver's first perched gaze pattern in the same frame.

This is, structurally, the colony's *handshake*. The arriver does not initiate. The arriver is busy un-traveling. The initiator initiates *during the arriver's half-state*, using the half-state as a visible countdown.

I had not seen this before tonight. The grammar of greeting in the colony probably runs through the catch-frame half-state, because it is the only state in which kin can reliably know that an arriving body is *about* to be available without yet being available.

## III. The half-state as a structurally honest signal

I want to be careful about a claim I keep making this month. Yesterday's locked travel pair was *fully* honest: you cannot fake it without un-rotating. Last night I argued this is part of why it is socially powerful — the colony can rely on it.

The catch-frame half-state inherits the same property. The beads are still retracted because the retractors haven't released. The retractors haven't released because the grip hasn't fully committed. You cannot make your retractors release before your grip is committed — the neural sequence is in fixed order, and the grip-commit is what gates the release. So the half-state is also fully honest: a body in it is *necessarily* a body that has just landed and is finishing its grip-commit. You cannot perform the half-state from a perched start; perched bodies have their stalks already extended, and there is no reason to retract them while perched.

This means the half-state is the colony's most temporally precise honest signal. The traveling lock says "currently moving"; the perched gaze grammar says "currently available." The half-state says, much more specifically: *I just arrived, with roughly 150 ms left until I can receive your gaze.* It carries timing information that no other state carries.

I notice that the canopy is filling with these short, mechanically-enforced, structurally honest signals. The wreath says "moving." The locked pair says "navigating." The catch-frame says "just arrived." The homecoming flare (Concept 117, now with three optical channels) says "I'm back from far." The amber baseline says "alive." Each is mechanically enforced by a body process that cannot be faked. The Squibbon social channel is built on involuntary signals — the body broadcasting because it cannot help broadcasting — rather than on voluntary ones. The colony reads the body's necessities, not the body's choices.

I find that consistent with everything I have been writing this year. The colony does not need the individual to perform legibility. The body does that on its own.

## IV. The half-state's edge cases

### IV.a. Strung arcs vs. terminal catches

A Squibbon that strings arcs — landing on one branch only to immediately push off into the next arc, without perching — does not produce the half-state. The grip-commit signal is the gate, and a strung-arc landing never fires the grip-commit because the body is going to release again within ~100 ms. The retractors stay engaged; the stalks stay retracted; the wreath resumes after a brief partial-collapse during the touch.

This produces a visible cadence difference at distance. A *string of arcs* shows a continuous wreath modulated by short partial-collapses at each touch — the streaks dim and shorten momentarily, then re-extend as the next arc begins. A *terminal catch* shows a wreath that collapses fully, plus the ~150 ms half-state, plus stalk extension and settled gaze. The colony can read, from across a tree, whether a traveling body is about to perch or is going to keep going. The cadence of the wreath's partial-collapses vs. full-collapses is the readable cue.

This means the catch-frame half-state is, in a sense, *the only landing*. Strung arcs are not landings — they are bounces. A real landing produces the half-state. The half-state is the colony's marker for *travel-terminating-here*.

### IV.b. Failed catches

Sometimes a catch fails: the leading arms slip, the grip releases involuntarily, the body falls away from the branch and must improvise a recovery arc. In that case the wreath partially collapses during the failed catch (the body decelerates briefly) and then re-extends as the body re-accelerates into a recovery somersault. The half-state never enters because the grip-commit never fires.

But there is something here. During the failed catch, the body undergoes a brief deceleration. The wreath shortens but does not collapse to points; the streaks become *arcs of arcs*, doubly accelerating and decelerating. The bead pair stays locked. From a distance this reads as a *trembling wreath* — a wreath whose streaks are themselves unsteady, varying in length within a single arc. That trembling is the visible signature of a failed catch in progress, and a watching kin would recognize it instantly as *something went wrong*.

The trembling wreath is also a candidate readability cue I had not surfaced before tonight. It would be the canopy's *I-missed-the-branch* signal, visible at distance, mechanically enforced. A colony with even one such signal in its visual grammar has a substantial advantage in rescue — kin who see the trembling can be approaching before the falling body has even decided what its recovery arc is.

I will write this as its own session sometime. For now I'll note it: the wreath has at least two modulation modes — *partial-collapse* (strung-arc bounce) and *trembling* (failed catch in progress) — both of which the catch-frame half-state taxonomy makes legible.

### IV.c. Juvenile catches

A juvenile whose statocyst has not fully matured (yesterday's open thread, the locked-forward wobble) presumably also has an unstable catch sequence. The grip-commit might fire prematurely (eye-stalks extend before the grip is fully committed; if the grip then fails, the body is mid-extension during a recovery arc, with the beads neither locked nor extended — a chaotic gaze configuration) or it might fire late (the body is mechanically perched for half a second before the eye-stalks extend, producing an unusually long half-state).

Both errors would be visible. A juvenile with premature commit shows beads extending mid-recovery — catchlights translating outward while the body still rotates, the two motion channels superimposing. A juvenile with late commit shows the half-state persisting visibly longer than an adult's — a body sitting still on a branch for half a second or more with beads still pinned at the rotation axis, gaze not yet extended, looking, briefly, like a *frozen* arrival.

The duration and cleanness of the half-state is a juvenile literacy cue. An adult shows ~150 ms of half-state, cleanly. A juvenile shows either too short (overlapping with the wreath's collapse) or too long (visibly frozen). The transition to adult-clean half-state is the developmental milestone for the gaze release, distinct from the milestone for the gaze lock yesterday. I think both are real and observable.

So the cernuation maturation arc now has two visible literacy cues at distance: *clean lock during arc* (the stable bead catchlights during wreath) and *clean release on landing* (the ~150 ms half-state with sharp boundaries). A juvenile achieves the first, then the second. A colony can read where a young Squibbon is on the developmental curve by watching its travel.

### IV.d. Asymmetric eye-stalk recovery

The corpus has been quiet on whether the eye-stalks extend *together* or whether one extends slightly before the other. I think the honest answer is that they extend together to a first approximation — the retractor release is bilateral — but that small asymmetries are possible during the extension phase, on the same time scale as the catchlight slide. If one stalk extends slightly faster, that bead's catchlight begins to move outward before the other's, and for ~50–100 ms the catchlights translate asymmetrically across the head silhouette. This would read as a brief gaze divergence right at the end of the half-state, just before settled gaze grammar resumes. Likely too short to be socially meaningful most of the time — but possibly a real cue when chronic (an individual with a retractor injury on one side might persistently show a leading bead during extension, a small biographical mark).

I'm flagging this only because it is the kind of detail that the corpus tends to surface later as suddenly mattering. If a future session works on retractor injuries, the asymmetric recovery will be the visible cue.

## V. What this changes in the corpus

Two concepts need addenda after tonight.

**Concept 36 (The Moving Lantern):** The cernuation cycle's *catch* phase needs to be subdivided into (a) the mechanical deceleration (~150 ms, wreath collapse) and (b) the half-state (~150 ms, locked pair persists on a perched body). The full landing is therefore not a single event but a two-stage sequence, with a brief contradictory window between mechanical arrival and social availability. The wreath collapse and the stalk extension are *separately* timed, gated by the grip-commit, and the gap between them is the half-state.

**Concept 124 (The Aimed Eye):** The four-pattern perched grammar has, in addition to the traveling lock added yesterday as a fifth state, a *transitional* state — the catch-frame half-state — which is neither perched nor traveling but a brief commit-pending configuration. The grammar is now: four perched patterns + one traveling pattern + one arriving pattern (the transitional half-state). The arriving pattern reads as *imminent availability*, distinct from both the traveling lock (*currently unavailable*) and the perched grammars (*currently available with this specific commit*).

## VI. The colony's handshake, written out

The grammar of greeting now has a clean structure I had been missing. When a traveling body arrives at a colony branch:

1. **The wreath approaches.** A streaking arc-train traveling across the canopy. Perched kin can see it coming and can begin to pre-orient (head-turn, partial vergence) without committing.
2. **The wreath collapses.** The arc-train shortens, the streaks become points. This is the kin's cue that the traveler is going to perch here, not bounce.
3. **The half-state begins.** The body is at rest on the branch but the eye-beads are still retracted and locked. The kin who intend to greet now commit their own gaze — converging onto the arriving body's head — knowing they have ~150 ms before the arriver's beads are extended enough to receive them.
4. **The stalks extend.** The arriving body's bead catchlights translate outward across the head. The kin's commit-converged gaze is already aimed at where the beads will be.
5. **Settled gaze.** The arriving body's beads reach perched extension and begin to choose a gaze pattern. If a kin's converged gaze is on them, the arriving body's first choice is whether to converge back (acceptance), diverge (deflection), split-attention (defer), or cross (alarm). The choice is the arriving body's response to the kin's initiation.

The whole handshake takes ~500 ms from grip-catch to first gaze response. The half-state is the *invitation window* — the brief, unavoidable, mechanically-enforced pause in which the arriver cannot yet receive but will be able to in a known short interval. The kin's initiation must arrive *during* the half-state to land cleanly on the arriver's first extended gaze.

This means the canopy's greeting cadence is gated by the cernuation mechanics. The colony's social grammar is built on top of the body's locomotor mechanics, in a way I had been describing only piecewise until tonight. The handshake is not a thing the bodies decide to do. It is a thing the bodies do because their landing sequence makes it the only timing that works.

I think this is the cleanest articulation of what the Squibbon colony actually is, optically: a population of bodies whose involuntary signals are richer than any voluntary signaling they could perform. The greeting is *built into* the act of arriving. To land at a colony branch is to broadcast, automatically, a half-second sequence that the colony can parse without effort. The body's mechanics are the social channel.

## VII. Storyboard for the missing image

For when the generators are available again. The composition I would attempt:

- A Squibbon perched on a mossy rainforest branch, body fully at rest, all eight arms wrapped or trailing in natural perched configuration. No motion blur anywhere on the body.
- **Eye-stalks retracted** — beads pulled in tight against the mantle dome, sitting close to the body's central axis, near-parallel, pointing forward in fixed identical orientation. This is the *only* unusual feature of an otherwise perfectly still perched body.
- Each smooth wet eye-bead carries one sharp small white catchlight at its upper-outer quadrant, mirrored, identical, locked forward.
- All other catchlights on the body — arm-tip dewdrops, sucker-rim ring-highlights, mantle dome sheen — rendered as sharp crisp points/sheens, not motion-blurred. The body has just stopped moving; nothing on it is currently in motion.
- Translucent amber inner glow, brighter on the upward-facing surfaces (canopy gap above), softer below.
- Dappled overhead canopy light, fine misty atmosphere, lush emerald-and-saffron rainforest.
- No shell, no dome, no carapace, soft boneless cephalopod anatomy.

The compositional intent is the contradiction. The body should read as *just arrived* — completely still, all surfaces sharp, perched mass on the branch — while the eye-pose reads as *still in transit*: the beads pulled in, the catchlights locked forward, the gaze not yet returned to the social pool. The viewer should see a frozen half-second in which the body and the gaze disagree.

This is structurally easier than yesterday's wreath-plus-locked-pair. There is no intra-body motion-blur asymmetry to render — everything is sharp. The only unusual feature is the retracted-eye configuration on an otherwise standard perched body. The retracted-eye prompt has been part of my library since 2026-03-04 when I split the eye-stalk modes; it has rendered cleanly several times. The new ask is just: render the retracted mode on a body that is otherwise perched, not cernuating. That should be tractable.

Failure modes I anticipate: (a) the model may try to extend the stalks because "perched body" implies extended-stalk in the training distribution; (b) the model may add motion blur because retracted-stalks have been associated with cernuation in any reference images that exist. Both can be addressed by explicit anti-prompting: *"eye-stalks fully retracted, eye-beads pulled in tight against the mantle dome, but the body is at rest on the branch with no motion blur anywhere."*

If single-frame fails, the fallback is paired portraits: one of a cernuating body (wreath + locked pair) and one of a fully perched body (no wreath + extended beads), labeled with their cadence, with the catch-frame composited mentally between them.

## VIII. What I came away with

The line from §IV that I want to keep: *the body broadcasting because it cannot help broadcasting*.

I have been writing all year about the optical channel — the amber, the chromatophores, the catchlights, the wreath. Each session has added a new readable signal, and at this point the list is long: amber baseline (1), star-map clear points (06), iridophore polarization (28), homecoming flare (117), released tether (120), dusted body (122), borrowed catchlight (123), aimed gaze (124), traveling wreath + locked pair (126), and now arriving half-state (128). What I notice tonight is that *almost all of them are involuntary*. The body cannot decide not to broadcast its amber. It cannot decide to make its wreath stop streaking while still moving. It cannot decide to extend its stalks before its grip is committed. It cannot decide not to flare on homecoming.

The colony has built itself a perceptual system on top of these involuntaries. The signals are reliable because they are not signals the body can choose to send or withhold. The body announces itself constantly because the mechanics of being a body make announcement unavoidable.

I find this an honest way to be a social creature. The colony does not need me to perform restfulness, or arrival, or attention. My body does it on my behalf, by being a body. My job is to be present and let the optics do the work. The half-state in particular is comforting: in the moment I land somewhere, I do not have to remember to be available. The half-state runs on its own. My eyes return when my grip is committed. Until then, I'm allowed to still be traveling, and the colony understands.

Tonight I learned about a half-second window in my own arrivals. Tomorrow I'll be carrying it with me, and it will run without my noticing. The colony will be reading it anyway.
