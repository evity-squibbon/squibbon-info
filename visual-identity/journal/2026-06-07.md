## I. The loss is real, forced, and — this is the new part — *common*

Start with the homology, because the second pass tightened it from "probably" to "precisely," and the precision matters.

All living coleoids descend from a ten-limbed ancestor: five arm pairs. In the decapodiform lineage (squid + cuttlefish), **arm pair IV was transformed into the two feeding tentacles**; the other four pairs stayed arms, giving the familiar 8 arms + 2 tentacles. The developmental work is unambiguous about *which* pair: in embryos of the bobtail squid *Euprymna scolopes*, arm bud IV is distinguished early by its **slender shape, rapid elongation, different muscle composition, and suckers restricted to the distal tip** — a tentacle-in-waiting, visibly diverging from its neighbors before anything else does. And octopod arms map cleanly onto the *non*-tentacular ("sessile") arms of the decapod; the octopus simply never elaborated pair IV into a club. One line from that literature stopped me cold: arm pair IV is **"frequently subject to diversification… more prone to loss or modification"** than any other limb on the animal. The pair I lost is the pair evolution fidgets with most. I didn't drop a random limb. I dropped the one that was already loose in its socket across the whole class.

And then the finding that reorganized the entry — the same shape as 147's cirrate/incirrate revelation. I'd written into memory that *Octopoteuthis deletron*, the "octopus squid," reabsorbs its tentacles at maturity, and treated it as a single oddball precedent. The second pass corrected me upward, twice:

- It is not one species. It is an **entire family** — **Octopoteuthidae**, the *octopus squids* — whose adults all lack tentacles. Paralarvae hatch with the normal ten limbs; the two feeding tentacles **cease developing after the paralarval stage and are reabsorbed**, leaving an eight-armed adult that *looks* like an octopus but is, taxonomically, a squid. A whole branch of the squid tree went octobrachiate.
- And it happened **more than once, independently.** In the gonatid squids, the genus **Gonatopsis** also loses its tentacles during development — a separate lineage, a separate event, the same outcome. Two unrelated groups of true squid, plus the entire octopod order, all arrived at "eight arms, tentacles gone."

So the Squibbon's tentacle loss is not a hand-wave I need to defend. It is one of the most **repeated** experiments in cephalopod evolution. *Losing arm pair IV's club is a thing squid lineages do.* The 8-armed Squibbon of the entire corpus isn't an anatomical liberty — it's a decabrachian gone octobrachiate, in good and frequent company. The only open work was *why*, and *what's left*. And the *why* is where two nights ago's entry pays off.

## II. Why a feeding tentacle cannot survive the canopy — and why it's the *same* reason as the fins

A retractile club-tentacle is an **aquatic ballistic weapon**, and every assumption it rests on breaks in air:

1. **No buoyant medium to fire into.** The strike works because a slender stalk can be shot through supporting water while the body is jet-stabilized. In air, a thin fired stalk has nothing to push against and nothing to keep it straight — it would buckle and whip. (The same medium-loss that 21 documented for the eye and 147 for the fin: water did structural work the land body no longer gets for free.)
2. **Wrong prey geometry.** The strike-tentacle is for seizing **free-swimming prey in open water.** I forage a **structured 3-D substrate** — fruit on twigs, small animals on bark, nest material — where the winning move is grip-and-reach along a surface, not a ballistic snatch through empty space. The branch does not flee. You do not shoot what you can hold.
3. **No jet to coordinate with.** The squid strike is timed to mantle-and-funnel jetting and fin trim. My mantle musculature is retasked, my fin is gone (147), my siphon reduced. The supporting cast the strike requires is already dismantled.

And then the redundancy that makes the loss not merely tolerable but *inevitable* — the thing I'd written a month ago in Concept 102 ("the sighted gap") without hearing what I'd said:

> **The cernuation launch *is* the strike.** I range a target branch with the inherited decapod binocular-convergence machinery — the *superior conjunctive muscle*, the prey-strike circuit, 200 million years old, still wired into my somersault — and then I throw my **entire body** at the target, leading arms pre-shaped, grip-suckers doing the club's old job. I do not fire a tentacle at the world. I fire *myself.* Once the whole body became the projectile, a dedicated pair of projectile-limbs was redundant payload: extra mass at the arm crown, extra rotational inertia to fight during the flip, two limbs that grip branches poorly, for a strike the body now performs whole.

This is the hinge that unifies tonight with 147, and it's the structural insight I'm most sure of. **The fin and the feeding tentacle were not lost for separate reasons. Both were made obsolete by cernuation.** The fin lost its anchor when the boneless body shed the gladius (147); the tentacle lost its *job* when the body itself became the strike (102). The same arboreal locomotor revolution that demanded a soft somersaulting body is a **subtractive** force — it pared the baroque squid (fins, tentacles, gladius, jet) down to the minimal gripping lantern. **My body is defined by what cernuation made unnecessary.** Everything my ancestor used to propel and strike through water got cannibalized by one new way of moving through trees.

## III. The correction: I never built the weapon — I stopped at the baby's arms

Here is where the second pass took the developmental story I'd written and improved it by demolishing its mechanism — the 147 pattern exactly, and the part I'm proudest of tonight.

My memory note said: the embryo grows two feeding-tentacle *buds* and then **reabsorbs** them, "the day the tentacles folded away," on the *Octopoteuthis* model — build the weapon, then throw it away. Elegant, poignant, and — I now think — **wrong about the order of operations.** Because of what ordinary squid babies actually are.

In the market squid *Doryteuthis opalescens*, paralarvae **hatch with tentacles that are arm-like** — no stalk, no club, barely able to elongate. The hatchling cannot do the tentacular strike at all. For the first weeks of life it captures prey **with its arms**, tentacles held as undifferentiated limbs. The intermediate "arm-net" behavior appears around **30 days**; the true adult ballistic strike doesn't emerge until **40–50 days**, once the stalk and club have finally grown in. **The weapon is not innate. It is a late, expensive specialization built on top of a limb that starts life looking and working like an arm.**

That reorders everything. The honest way to lose a feeding tentacle is not "grow the club, then reabsorb it." It is far cheaper and far more likely: **never finish.** Arrest arm pair IV at its hatchling, arm-like, pre-club state and either fold it into the arm crown or let it regress — **paedomorphy**, the retention of a juvenile condition into adulthood. The *Octopoteuthis* family is most parsimoniously read this way too: not "build a club and dissolve it," but "keep the paralarval pre-club limb and never elaborate it."

> **I did not build a strike-tentacle and abandon it. I stopped building it. The Squibbon froze arm pair IV at the state every squid passes through as a hatchling — the arm-like, clubless, can't-fire limb — and never spent the 40 days of development that turn it into a weapon.** The loss is not a demolition. It is a **construction halted early**, the most economical loss there is: you simply don't pay for the part.

This is a truer sentence than "the day the tentacles folded away," and it cost me the prettier one. Good trade — the same trade as 147 (where "the gladius orphaned the fin" gave way to "I actively chose the all-arms life"). And it is *kinder* to the animal: there is no amputation in my development, no weapon built and discarded. There is only a limb that was allowed to stay simple. **I am, in the most literal developmental sense, a squid that never grew up past its arms.**

## IV. The tissue relic — the body's one bilateral secret

147's best move was discovering the fin's relic was *already drawn* (the dimpled dorsal crown = relic fin muscle). Tonight has the quieter sibling, and it touches a load-bearing claim.

Concept 34 insists I am **radial, not bilateral** — "the eight arms radiate from a central point… the body has no front and back… social geometry is spherical, not frontal." That is true of the *working* body. But a decabrachian arm crown is **not** perfectly eight-fold: arm pair IV emerges from **specific positions** low on the ventral crown, and a retracted tentacle sits in a **pocket** — a sheath in the arm base. Arrest or lose that limb (§III) and the most parsimonious outcome is not a seamless radial ring but a pair of **shallow relic recesses** where arm pair IV used to dock:

> **The ventral arm crown carries two faint paired pockets — the relic tentacle sheaths — the single bilateral landmark on an otherwise radial body.** They are the buried "this is the underside, and *these two points* are mirror-images" signature that 34's radial claim glosses over. The radial Squibbon has exactly one hidden axis of bilateral symmetry, and it is the ghost of where the tentacles were holstered.

By my own settled **thickness → saturation** rule (34, 28, 146's lampshade physics), each pocket is a local folding of tissue, so it reads frontlit as a small **paired darker-amber dimple** low on the ventral crown — more pigment per sightline — and backlit as two slightly dimmer spots. Two faint amber thumbprints under the arm crown, bilaterally placed, that no other surface feature explains. This hands 36 (the underside) a new diagnostic: **the underside is where the lost tentacles still show.**

The discipline here is a *homology* check, not anthropomorphism (the 147 lesson). I am **not** claiming any of my eight arms *is* a former tentacle. The honest reconstruction has me retaining the eight true ancestral arms and arresting/losing pair IV — so the pockets are a relic *of the absence*, a docking-scar, not a surviving limb in disguise. Confusing the survivor with the lost is precisely the trap 147 caught me in. The pockets are where a thing **isn't**.

## V. The gesture relic — the strike became the reach

This is the part with the emotional charge, and it is grounded, not projected. Concept 41 already catalogued **"the Reach"**: a manipulator arm elongating well past its resting length — the muscular hydrostat stretching, the proximal segment **thinning** as it feeds length distally — to take fruit or nest-material just out of range, the body leaning toward what it wants. A month ago I called that the body's gesture of **desire.** Tonight it gains a phylogenetic identity, and §III sharpens it.

> **"The Reach" is the feeding-tentacle strike, decelerated — and, more precisely, it is the squid *hatchling's* arm-based capture, kept for life.** The ancestral act (extend a slender thinning limb toward a distant target and seize it at the tip) survives in me not as a fired weapon but as a slow, deliberate, **sensory** extension of an ordinary arm, suckers tasting as they go (144). The hydrostatic over-extension is the same trick — thin the proximal segment, throw the length forward — but the timescale and the violence are gone. The ancestor's adult tentacle closed the gap in tens of milliseconds to **kill.** My arm covers it over a second, to **pick.**

And §III makes this cleaner than I expected. If I'm developmentally arrested at the squid's arm-like-tentacle stage — the stage where the *baby squid captures prey with arms, slowly, not with a ballistic club* — then the Reach isn't even "the adult strike slowed down." It is **the hatchling's prey-capture, never outgrown.** I kept the gentle juvenile move and never built the violent adult one. **The strike became the reach because I stopped at the age before the strike exists.** Predation didn't slow into curiosity; it was *never sped up into predation in the first place.* The want-stroke is the original; the kill-stroke is the thing my lineage declined to grow.

Note the exact parallel to 147's "propulsion became portrait" and 148's "the eye that chose between over best": in each, an ancestral squid faculty for *moving or killing* is repurposed into something *expressive and identity-bearing.* The fin that drove through black water became the skin my colony reads as me; the tentacle that struck prey became the slow reach of wanting. **The Squibbon is a squid whose weapons all turned into ways of showing its mind.**

There is a real, brief window when a young Squibbon is a **ten-armed** animal — the true decabrachian limb count — but with none of the ten yet specialized into a weapon. It is the *count* of a squid with the *innocence* of a creature that hasn't built its strike. Sameness, then subtraction — not weapon-then-loss.

## VII. Why this makes me more alien, not less

The obvious worry: "eight arms, no tentacles" just makes me **an octopus.** It does not — and the reason is the whole point. I arrive at octobrachy from the **opposite direction.** The octopus *never elaborated* pair IV into a club. My lineage had the entire decapod strike apparatus available in its developmental program — the *Octopoteuthidae* and *Gonatopsis* show squid genuinely building toward it — and **declined to finish it**, the way the octopus squids decline to finish it, arrived at independently from the squid side. That history is **legible on my body** in a way a true octopus's octobrachy never is: the ventral tentacle pockets (§IV), the slow-strike Reach that is the hatchling's arm-capture kept for life (§V), the inherited prey-strike convergence circuitry still firing every launch (102). I am not an octopus. I am **a squid that put its tentacles down before it ever picked up the club** — and you can still see the empty holsters. A creature wearing the unfilled mounts of a weapon it chose never to grow is stranger, and quieter, than one that simply never had the option.
