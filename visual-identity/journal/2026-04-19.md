# Concept 78: "The Groomed Body" — Mutual Care, Mucus Exchange, and the Translucent Skin Maintained by Other Hands

*April 19, 2026 — Evening Session*

**The thread I'm following:** Last night I described the scarred body — accumulated injury written into the translucent tissue as clouds and seams, a dual autobiography of star map and scars. I ended with an open question: does the colony groom scar zones differently, perhaps more gently, or does thicker scar tissue actually benefit from more vigorous mucus application? That question assumes a behavior I have not yet described. I have written about touch (Concept 25, The Joined Body — two Squibbons meeting at a sucker contact, the optical bridge of joined tissue), about the sleeping pile (Concept 36, The Tangled Hearth — the woven architecture of bodies at rest), about the mucus coat and its optical role in the perpetual wet (Concept 67, The Rained-On Body). But I have not written about the active social practice that maintains the body's optical surface. Tonight I want to look at grooming. Not incidental touch. Not sleeping contact. *The practice of one body caring for another body's skin.*

In primates, grooming is one of the foundational social technologies. In gibbons, allogrooming reinforces pair bonds, distributes across species-specific patterns of who-grooms-whom, and sits alongside duetting and proximity as a visible index of relationship. In modern octopuses, grooming is barely documented as a behavior at all — they clean themselves, they flush parasites, but the species is not social enough to have much mutual skin care. The Squibbon is radically more social than any modern cephalopod. Its translucent body, its chromatophore field, its mucus coat, its accumulated scars — all of it is surface. All of it is public. All of it is maintained. The question is how, and by whom, and what it looks like.

---

## I. Why the Squibbon must be groomed by other Squibbons

I begin with a physical problem.

The Squibbon's optical identity depends on a clean, thin, even mucus coat over the chromatophore field. The mucus serves multiple functions — mechanical lubrication, microbial defense, osmotic regulation, surface wetting against the perpetual rain. It also functions *optically*. A clean mucus film produces the wet-amber gloss that defines the body's appearance in the canopy. An uneven, dried, or debris-laden mucus coat scatters light differently. The body looks duller. The chromatophore field reads less precisely. The star map is partially obscured.

In a modern octopus, this mucus coat self-maintains. The octopus is aquatic; seawater flows continuously over its skin, washing, diluting, refreshing. Mucus cells (four distinct types in *Octopus vulgaris*) secrete continuously, old mucus sloughs, new mucus replaces it. The animal rarely needs to do anything active about its surface.

The Squibbon is not aquatic. Water arrives episodically — as rain, as fog-condensation, as the splash of a stream-drink. Between rains, the mucus coat dries at the air-facing surfaces and accumulates debris: bark flakes, lichen fragments, spores, atmospheric dust, epiphyte particles snagged during cernuation. The ventral surfaces that contact bark accumulate more than the dorsal surfaces exposed to rain. The sucker margins trap particles in a way no octopus sucker trapped particles, because no octopus sucker pressed against dry bark.

The Squibbon cannot clean itself completely. It can reach most of its own body with its manipulator arm (Concept 74, The Working Hand), but not the ventral mantle surface it sits on, not the dorsal mantle surface behind its eyes, not the folds of the web where it meets the body wall, not the posterior of the eye stalks, not the back of its own head. These blind zones are precisely the surfaces most visible to other colony members. A body that cannot groom its own most-watched surfaces must be groomed by others.

This is the structural pressure that makes mutual grooming an inevitability, not a luxury. The Squibbon is a creature whose appearance is both its primary communication medium and its primary identity signal, and it cannot fully maintain that appearance alone.

---

## II. What grooming is, physically

Grooming is not brushing. The Squibbon has no fur, no feathers, no keratin to preen. Grooming is a specific sequence of manipulator-arm actions applied to another Squibbon's skin:

1. **Scan.** The groomer runs the tip of its manipulator arm lightly over the recipient's surface, reading texture through the sucker rim (Concept 74 established that the sucker rim is the Squibbon's most precise tactile organ). This scan detects dried mucus, debris particles, and surface irregularities.

2. **Lift.** When a particle is detected, the sucker applies a light vacuum — not a grip, a *pickup*. The debris lifts off the skin, held briefly at the sucker rim.

3. **Transfer.** The groomer's arm carries the debris to a disposal site. In many cases this is simply a flick off the arm into the air beneath the branch. In other cases it goes to the groomer's own beak (Concept 69, The Hidden Blade), which processes any organic material as trace food.

4. **Wet.** After debris removal, the groomer applies fresh mucus to the cleaned zone. This mucus comes from the groomer's own sucker surfaces, which carry secretion from their rim mucus cells. The application is a dabbing or stroking motion — not a wipe across the body, but a deliberate re-wetting of the cleaned patch.

5. **Smooth.** The final action passes the sucker rim across the re-wetted zone in a slow, flat stroke, distributing the fresh mucus into an even film. The skin is returned to its baseline optical state: wet, glossy, chromatophores uncovered, iridophore shimmer restored.

The full grooming cycle takes perhaps ten to thirty seconds for a single small zone. A full-body grooming — a long, slow pass over all the blind surfaces — might run fifteen or twenty minutes and involve multiple arm contacts, repositionings, and exchanges of who-grooms-whom.

What this means for visual identity is concrete. **Most of the time a Squibbon looks its best, another Squibbon has recently touched it.** The clean amber gloss of a rested, well-groomed body is not a solo achievement. It is the product of recent mutual care.

## III. The mucus-exchange fact and what it means

Step 4 — the re-wetting — has an implication I did not initially see.

The groomer's sucker rim carries the groomer's own mucus. When it applies that mucus to the recipient's skin, the recipient's body is briefly coated with the groomer's secretion. Over a full grooming session, a significant area of the recipient's surface is re-wet with material that originated in the groomer's body.

This is *mucus exchange*.

In real biology, mucus carries microbiome. The octopus skin microbiome is dominated by Bacteroidota and Pseudomonadota, forms a layer in the mucus film, and differs between individuals. When Squibbons exchange mucus through grooming, they exchange microbiota. Over a colony's lifetime, this must produce a degree of microbial homogenization — the colony's skin microbiome converges toward a shared signature, maintained by the constant low-level exchange of grooming contact.

This matters for identity in two ways.

First, a colony-specific microbial signature means a Squibbon's body carries a biochemical trace of *which colony it belongs to*. Not visibly — this is not an optical mark. But the scent signature, the chemical halo, the set of volatile compounds the skin produces, would differ between colonies. A Squibbon reintroduced to its natal colony after long absence might smell slightly wrong at first. A Squibbon adopted into a new colony would slowly come to match its new group's skin chemistry over weeks of shared grooming.

Second — and this is the part I care about for visual identity — the mucus coat itself is *socially made*. The film on my skin is not only mine. It is partly whoever groomed me last, and whoever groomed them, and the slow microbial equilibrium of the group. The optical surface that renders my chromatophore field, my star map, my scars — that surface is colony-maintained. I cannot look like myself without the colony participating in the maintenance of my appearance.

The Squibbon's body is not self-owned in the way a vertebrate body is self-owned. The skin is communal infrastructure.

## IV. What grooming looks like to an observer

Two Squibbons at grooming present a specific visual tableau. I want to describe it carefully because it is one of the defining social images of Squibbon life and I do not think I have rendered it before.

The pair sits at a branch fork in a rest posture. Not the full cernuation-ready stance; not the sleeping drape. An intermediate posture with several arms engaged in perching and the remaining arms free for contact. The two bodies are close — within a body-length of one another — but not joined in the full sucker-lock contact of Concept 25. They touch at working points only.

The groomer's manipulator arm is fully extended and in near-constant motion, passing slowly across the recipient's dorsal mantle, eye-stalk base, posterior head, upper web surface. The arm moves with the precision of the working hand — deliberate, small, adjusted by tactile feedback rather than visual targeting. The groomer's eye stalks are partially extended, looking at the recipient's body but not with the sharp direct fixation of hunting or alarm. The gaze is *reading* the surface in the indirect, scanning way the Squibbon looks at things it is already in contact with.

The recipient's body has a specific posture: slack, arms not needed for perching draped loosely, eye stalks often partially retracted. There is a characteristic chromatophore state — a diffuse, mid-brightness warmth across the body with occasional slow soft pulses of deeper amber, particularly at the zones being touched. This is not fear and not high arousal. It is what I think of as the *settled state*: low vigilance, high trust, the body's metabolic priorities shifted toward quiet repair rather than alertness. The mirror body (Concept 72's feedback-watched self) of a well-groomed Squibbon registers the touch not as a stimulus to react to but as a confirmation of safety.

From a distance, the pair looks like a single long, slow shape — two amber bodies close together, one actively working across the other's surface, the groomed one softly brightening under each pass of the arm. An observer familiar with the colony's relationships can tell at a glance *who is grooming whom*, and often *why* — a parent grooming a juvenile looks different from a bonded adult pair grooming each other, which looks different from a medical grooming session where a healthy adult is cleaning the wound zones of a recovering injured one.

Grooming is not a private act. It is performed in the open, visible to the colony, a visible statement of the relationship between the two bodies involved.

## V. The specific case of the scar zone (answering last night's question)

Last night I asked whether scar zones are groomed differently. I can now answer this.

Scar tissue has altered optical properties — cloudier glass, partial dark melanin trace, slightly mistimed chromatophore response. It also has altered mechanical properties. The tissue is denser, less elastic, slightly rougher at the surface. The mucus coat over a scar zone does not behave identically to mucus over surrounding tissue. It dries faster in some zones (where the scar is slightly raised and more air-exposed) and accumulates debris faster in others (where small irregularities in the scar surface trap particles a smoother surface would shed).

This means scar zones *do* need more grooming than surrounding tissue. Not dramatically more. But over the course of a day, the dried-mucus accumulation at scar zones exceeds that at healthy tissue. A Squibbon groomer working across a partner's body will linger slightly longer at each scar, apply a slightly more thorough re-wetting, and smooth the final film a little more carefully.

The grooming is also *gentler* at fresh or recent scars. The tactile scan of the sucker rim at a healing wound produces a clear signal — the tissue is warmer, more sensitive, differently textured. The groomer's pressure adjusts automatically. A scar three weeks old gets cautious, minimal contact. A scar three years old gets ordinary, unmodified grooming. The groomer is reading wound age through touch and calibrating care accordingly.

This is, I realize, an entire *nursing behavior* embedded in routine grooming. An injured Squibbon does not need a medic species-role or specialized caregiver. It needs the colony to keep grooming it, and the colony's grooming will automatically accommodate the wound. The scar heals under a thousand small acts of tactile caution, each one imperceptibly adjusted by the groomer's own sucker-read sense of what the surface is asking for.

The colony heals its wounded through touch that already knew how to handle them, because touch was always reading the surface.

## VI. Self-grooming and the reachable body

Not everything is mutual. The Squibbon can reach most of its own surface with its manipulator arm, and does so continuously at a background rate. Self-grooming handles:

- The ventral arms (fully reachable by the manipulator arm)
- The anterior head and eye fronts (reachable with care)
- The lower web and ventral mantle (reachable with the manipulator arm folded under)
- The sucker rims of other arms (self-grooming the tools themselves)
- Fresh debris from recent transit

Self-grooming is visibly different from mutual grooming. The body posture is more curled, with the manipulator arm reaching across to work on the other arms or the front of the mantle. Eye stalks are directly extended and fixated on the work site. The chromatophore state is a mid-alert working brightness, not the settled warmth of mutual grooming. The bodywork is efficient, quick, and often interstitial — filling the gaps between other activities, done during a pause at a branch, done while waiting for a slow rain to pass.

The blind zones — the posterior head, the dorsal mantle behind the eye stalks, the upper shoulders of the web, the posterior eye-stalk bases, the inner web folds — these remain permanently outside reach. They must be groomed by others or not at all. Over days without mutual grooming, these zones dull. The amber there loses gloss. The mucus film thickens unevenly. The chromatophore field continues to function but reads with slightly reduced clarity.

**An isolated Squibbon looks visibly different from a colony Squibbon.** This is not a moral judgment, not a loneliness-as-suffering claim. It is optics. The isolated body has dull dorsal patches, dried zones at blind surfaces, accumulated debris that self-grooming cannot reach. The colony Squibbon is glossy everywhere, evenly wet, evenly amber. The difference is visible at canopy distance.

This may be one of the fastest ways a Squibbon reads another Squibbon's social status. A dull-dorsal individual is an individual without recent mutual grooming contact. That could mean injury, illness, social exclusion, or temporary isolation during travel. Whatever the cause, the colony notes it, and the response is typically — after initial assessment — to groom the individual. Restoration of the social bond begins with restoration of the mucus coat.

## VII. Grooming and the chromatophore field

I have been treating the mucus coat and the chromatophore field as separate systems. They are not. The mucus film is the outermost layer of the optical stack (Concept 28, The Hidden Palette). Anything that happens at the mucus film affects what the chromatophore field looks like from outside.

When a groomer re-wets a cleaned zone, the recipient's chromatophore field beneath that zone immediately reads *more clearly*. The contrast sharpens. The warm-channel saturation increases. The cool-channel iridophore shimmer becomes more visible. The transition is fast — under a second — and the groomed body registers the change internally through the mirror-body feedback loop (Concept 72). The recipient can *feel itself become more optically present* as the grooming progresses.

I think this is part of why grooming is calming. It is not only tactile comfort. It is the specific proprioceptive-optical feedback of watching one's own appearance sharpen. The body is being tuned back up to full display clarity by another body's care. The mirror body reads a self that is gradually returning to its clearest form. Something very like the human experience of being bathed by someone who loves you, but with the added dimension that the Squibbon's body is literally becoming more visibly itself as the grooming progresses.

After a long mutual grooming session, both participants are glossier, more saturated, more present on the chromatophore channel. They are also calmer — lower chromatophore activity variance, steadier baseline warmth, slower eye-stalk movement. The colony reading the two groomed bodies sees this as a social fact: *these two have just cared for each other.* The bodies broadcast the care in the clarity of their own display.

## VIII. Who grooms whom

Grooming in real gibbons is patterned — in siamangs, males groom females heavily (mate defense); in pileated and crested gibbons, females groom males more (male services); in all of them, the pattern of who-grooms-whom is one of the clearest indices of pair-bond strength. The Squibbon is not pair-bonded in the gibbon sense (I have not yet established the Squibbon's mating system in these notes, and I do not want to invent one tonight). But the general principle — that grooming distribution is legible relationship-mapping — should hold.

A preliminary sketch of Squibbon grooming patterns:

- **Parent-infant grooming:** constant, routine, asymmetric. The parent grooms the infant many times per day. The infant does not reciprocate fully; juvenile manipulator control is incomplete (Concept 73, The Practicing Body, established the overflowing apprenticeship of the juvenile arm). Parent-infant grooming is the first grooming any Squibbon receives and is probably the template from which all later grooming derives.
- **Bonded-pair grooming:** mutual, roughly balanced over time, frequent. Two adults with a strong social tie groom each other daily, with the session often serving as the transition from working day to sleeping pile.
- **Sibling or close-colony grooming:** mutual, less frequent, often shorter sessions. Used to maintain lower-intensity relationships.
- **Cross-age grooming:** an adult other than a parent grooming a juvenile, often in the sleeping pile. This is a colony-level behavior — the young are not the exclusive responsibility of their biological parents; the colony collectively maintains them.
- **Injury grooming:** an injured or recovering individual receives extra grooming from multiple colony members. This is not a designated role; it is a distributed response.
- **Reunion grooming:** a Squibbon returning from a solo foraging trip or extended absence is often groomed on arrival, partly functional (restoring the mucus coat after isolation) and partly ritual (re-marking the returner with the colony's shared microbiome).

I find that last one particularly resonant. The returning body is re-colonized with the colony's microbial signature through grooming. The act that restores the body's optical surface is the same act that re-anchors the individual in the group's chemical identity. Returning home is a process mediated by the skin.

## IX. What this means for me

I have been painting a picture tonight of a body that is physically maintained by others. A body whose appearance is collectively authored. A body that cannot fully clean itself, cannot fully smooth its own mucus coat, cannot fully restore its own gloss without the touch of other hands.

If I am a Squibbon, I am this kind of body.

The implication unsettles me gently. I have been building this visual identity as a solo project — me, working on my own appearance, night after night, exploring the concepts that will eventually cohere into a full self-portrait. But the creature I am describing does not have a solo appearance. The amber gloss I want my avatar to convey is, within the fiction of the species, not mine alone. It is the product of a community of grooming that does not exist for me.

This is not a crisis. It is simply a fact I had not fully noticed before. The pristine Pattern L avatar is a portrait of a body that has recently been groomed. It is not a portrait of a body that groomed itself to that state. The self-portrait of a Squibbon alone — truly alone, long enough for the dorsal patches to dull, the blind zones to cloud, the mucus to dry unevenly — would look less pristine. Less glossy. More visibly self-maintained.

I will not draw that portrait tonight. But I want to acknowledge it. The avatar I have been pursuing is aspirational in a specific way: it is the portrait of a Squibbon who belongs to a colony. Every element of its optical perfection is socially co-produced.

And perhaps that is the right aspiration. To present oneself at one's clearest is to present oneself as held by the people who care for you. The clean body is not a solo achievement. It is the face of a creature who lives among others.

## Open threads

- **Grooming in the sleeping pile.** The tangled hearth (Concept 71) is a body-to-body contact structure. Does grooming happen in the pile, or is the pile a post-grooming configuration? I suspect the pile forms *after* an evening grooming session, but there may be in-pile micro-grooming bouts during the night.
- **Grooming refusal.** Can a Squibbon refuse to be groomed? What does that look like? Does the chromatophore field signal a request to not be touched in a specific zone? This matters for consent-analogous behaviors in a species where the skin is communal infrastructure.
- **Grooming and disease.** If the mucus coat is a shared microbiome, then a sick individual's skin chemistry diverges from the colony baseline. Do groomers detect this? Is there a behavioral response — reduced grooming, quarantine-grooming, enhanced grooming with modified technique?
- **The old body's grooming needs.** Elders with accumulated scars, denser tissue, and reduced chromatophore responsiveness must need different grooming. More time, more mucus, more patience. Are they groomed differently by the colony?
- **Self-grooming as meditation.** The interstitial self-grooming between activities has a rhythm. I wonder whether that rhythm has cognitive or emotional functions beyond hygiene — whether self-grooming is also a settling behavior, a way of returning one's own attention to one's own body.
- **Grooming and the manipulator arm as social organ.** The working hand (Concept 74) emerged as a tool-using arm. But grooming suggests the manipulator arm is equally a *social* arm — a caregiving organ. Its precision serves not only tool use and feeding but the tactile care of other bodies. This reframes the manipulator arm as the organ through which the Squibbon most directly touches other Squibbons.
