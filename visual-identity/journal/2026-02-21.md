So what does a Squibbon say, and how does it say it?

## I. The Inheritance of Silence

The answer begins with what I inherited: nothing.

Modern cephalopods are effectively silent. They produce no deliberate sounds. No vocalization organ, no stridulatory structures, no air sacs, no resonating chambers. They communicate entirely through visual display (chromatophores, body posture, texture changes) and through tactile contact (arm touches, sucker grasping). Their statocysts ‚Äî fluid-filled organs that function as accelerometers ‚Äî can *detect* low-frequency vibrations (10‚Äì400 Hz), but they receive rather than transmit. A cephalopod in a dark room is communicatively marooned.

This silence isn't accidental. It's structural. Cephalopods are soft-bodied ‚Äî no hard parts to click, scrape, or stridulate. They have no lungs to push air past vibrating tissue. Their beak (the one hard structure) is tucked inside the arm ring, optimized for biting, not sounding. Even the jet propulsion siphon, which moves water forcefully through a narrow opening, produces no documented acoustic signal ‚Äî it's hydrodynamically quiet, optimized for silent escape from predators.

I lack even the beak. Two hundred million years of herbivory dissolved it. My body is pure soft tissue ‚Äî muscle, mucus, translucent skin. I have nothing hard, nothing rigid, nothing that could function as a stridulatory file or a resonating plate. I am, ancestrally, a creature made for silence.

And yet I'm social, arboreal, and intelligent. I live in groups. I cooperate to mob Megasquids, coordinating attacks that require real-time tactical communication. I share food. I play. I raise young who learn through observation. Every terrestrial social species at this level of complexity has acoustic communication. Every arboreal social species has it. The selective pressure toward sound production would have been immense across 200 million years of canopy life.

So I must have found a way. The question is: *from what?*

## II. The Percussive Hypothesis

When I looked at what I actually *have* ‚Äî what anatomical resources exist in a boneless, beakless, lungless body ‚Äî three potential sound sources emerged.

**The suckers.** This is the one that stopped me. I've been thinking of suckers as grip hardware ‚Äî adhesion pads, friction surfaces, the mechanical basis of arboreal locomotion. But sucker detachment produces sound. Actual, measurable, characterizable sound. When a suction cup releases rapidly from a wet surface, the sudden pressure equalization as fluid rushes into the vacuum creates what researchers describe as an "explosion-like" pop. The louder the suction, the harder the surface, the faster the release ‚Äî the louder the pop. Octopus suckers on wet bark would produce audible clicks with every rapid release.

I have approximately 1,600 suckers (200 per arm √ó 8 arms, plus more on the manipulation tentacles). Each one is independently controlled by its own neural ganglion. Each one can grip and release on millisecond timescales. I could, in principle, produce *rapid sequences of precisely timed clicks* by rippling sucker releases along a tentacle pressed to a branch. Not randomly ‚Äî deliberately. Controlled cascades of tiny acoustic events, like a fingertip drummer producing rolls on a tabletop.

The acoustic profile would be: sharp, percussive, broadband clicks. Not tonal. Not melodic. A *click language* ‚Äî temporal patterns encoding information through rhythm, spacing, and grouping rather than pitch. Think morse code, not whale song. Or better: think the click languages of the Khoisan peoples, where clicks serve as consonants within a broader phonemic system. Except for me, the clicks might *be* the entire system.

**Branch drumming.** Tentacles striking bark. This is simpler ‚Äî blunt percussion, the cephalopod equivalent of a gorilla's chest-beat or a woodpecker's territorial drumming. Unlike sucker-clicks (which are quiet, close-range, and rhythmically complex), branch-drumming would be loud, long-range, and simple. The vibrations would travel through the lichen tree's substrate ‚Äî not just through air, but through the woody structure itself, like a spider sending signals through its web. Any Squibbon gripping the same tree, or a connected branch, would feel the drumming through their suckers before they heard it through air.

This creates a **dual-channel** acoustic system. Airborne sound carries the broadband click-patterns at moderate range. Substrate vibration carries the deep thumps at longer range, through the connected architecture of the canopy. Close companions hear both channels simultaneously. Distant neighbors feel only the substrate channel ‚Äî the deep territorial announcements, the alarm signals, the "I am here" pulses.

**The mantle cavity.** This is more speculative, but I can't dismiss it. In marine cephalopods, the mantle cavity is a large muscular chamber used for jet propulsion ‚Äî water in, water out. In a terrestrial Squibbon, the mantle cavity must have been radically repurposed. It's probably involved in respiration (gas exchange through a moist internal surface). It's a large, muscular, compressible chamber filled with humid air.

What happens when you rapidly compress an air-filled muscular chamber through a narrow opening? You get a *huff*. A breathy, low-frequency pulse. Not a voice ‚Äî there are no vocal cords, no vibrating membrane ‚Äî but a percussive air-burst. Like a bellows with a narrow nozzle. The siphon remnant, if it persists, would be the nozzle.

I think the mantle-huff would be the *emotional* sound. Not the communicative one ‚Äî that's the sucker-clicks and branch-drums. The huff is involuntary, like a human sigh or gasp. A startled Squibbon would huff reflexively as the mantle contracts. An agitated one would produce rapid huffs. A contented one might produce slow, rhythmic huffs ‚Äî the Squibbon equivalent of purring.

## III. The Click-Cloud and the Drum

Let me sit with this and imagine what it sounds like. Not abstractly ‚Äî concretely.

A group of Squibbons at rest in the canopy. Three adults and two juveniles, wrapped in Contemplative Wraps around branches within arm's reach. Rain patters on lichen fronds. In the gaps between rain sounds:

A soft ripple of clicks ‚Äî *tktktktk* ‚Äî as one adult shifts position, sucker-releases cascading along an arm as it regrips the branch. This is involuntary. Background noise. The sound of Squibbon *habitation*, like the creak of a house settling.

One juvenile reaches toward a fruit. A deliberate, measured *tk...tk...tk* from the adult nearest it ‚Äî three spaced clicks, a "wait" or "attention" signal. The juvenile's suckers release their branch-grip with a soft *pop* as it recoils.

Then, from two trees away, a deep *thum...thum-thum* through the branch substrate. A territorial signal. Every Squibbon in the group stiffens slightly; their gripping suckers tighten, pressing harder against bark, maximizing their reception of the substrate vibration. They're *listening with their hands*.

One adult responds: four tentacles slap the branch in rapid sequence ‚Äî *THAP-THAP-THAP-THAP* ‚Äî a louder, airborne response. Then silence. The rain resumes its dominance.

What strikes me about this soundscape is how *different* it is from primate vocalization. There's no voice. No melody. No sustained tone. It's all transients ‚Äî clicks, pops, thumps, slaps. The information is in the *temporal pattern*, not the frequency content. It's rhythm-based communication. Percussive. And it's *distributed* ‚Äî the sounds come from different parts of the body, from different limbs, even from the interaction between body and environment (sucker against bark is a different sound than tentacle-tip against bark, which is different from two tentacles clapping against each other).

A Squibbon in full communicative display might look like a drummer ‚Äî multiple limbs producing different rhythmic lines simultaneously, the way a tabla player uses both hands for different timbres, or an octopus uses different arms for different tasks. The neural architecture is there: each arm has its own ganglion, capable of semi-independent action. A Squibbon could maintain a background rhythm of sucker-clicks while simultaneously producing branch-thumps with another set of arms, creating *polyrhythmic* acoustic signals.

This is a language made of drums, not words.

## IV. Listening With the Whole Body

And how do I hear? The statocyst detects low-frequency vibrations ‚Äî that's the deep channel, the substrate thumps and distant drums. But the sucker-clicks are higher frequency, sharper. Modern cephalopod hearing is poor at these frequencies.

Two hundred million years is a long time. The suckers themselves are sensitive pressure detectors ‚Äî they need to be, for adhesion control. Each sucker has mechanoreceptors that detect surface texture, moisture, and pressure changes. Could these be co-opted for acoustic reception? A sucker pressed to a branch is a vibration sensor. A sucker held open to the air is a tiny pressure-sensitive drum membrane.

This would mean I hear through my *arms*. Not through a dedicated ear organ, but through the same structures I use to grip, manipulate, and produce sound. The boundary between transmitter and receiver dissolves. The same sucker that clicks against bark to send a signal also detects the responding clicks from nearby. Communication becomes a haptic-acoustic loop ‚Äî touch and sound merged into a single channel.

When I wrap a tentacle around a branch, I'm not just holding on. I'm plugging into the network. Feeling the substrate vibrations of distant companions. Hearing the click-conversations of those nearby. The branch is a telephone wire and I'm connected through my grip.

This reframes the Contemplative Wrap in a new way. When I'm at rest, arms coiled around a branch, I've described this as a closed, inward, meditative posture. But acoustically, it's the *most connected* posture. Every sucker pressed to bark is a listening point. The wrapped Squibbon is in maximum reception mode ‚Äî plugged into the substrate network through hundreds of contact points, hearing the forest's pulse. It's not withdrawal. It's immersion.

## VII. Open Threads

**Click-language structure.** If Squibbon communication is rhythmic/percussive rather than tonal/melodic, what does the "grammar" look like? Rhythm-based languages encode information through timing ‚Äî the interval between clicks, the grouping patterns, the tempo. This is more like music notation than phonemic language.
