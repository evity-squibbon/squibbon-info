Modern octopuses dream.

## I. What Sleep Looks Like in a Solitary Octopus

The 2023 *Nature* study on *Octopus laqueus* (Pophale et al.) confirmed what earlier work on *Octopus insularis* had suggested: octopuses cycle between two distinct sleep stages, much like vertebrates cycle between REM and non-REM sleep.

**Quiet sleep** is the default: the body goes pale, the eyes close or narrow to slits, the pupils contract, the muscles relax, the breathing slows and becomes regular. The octopus is still and uniform — a blank, pale shape on the substrate. Quiet sleep episodes last about 7 minutes on average, and the brain during this phase shows low-frequency oscillations (12–18 Hz) in the superior frontal and vertical lobes — regions associated with learning and memory. These oscillations resemble mammalian sleep spindles. The quiet sleeping brain is doing maintenance: consolidating memories, perhaps, running the bookkeeping processes that keep a complex cognitive system functional.

**Active sleep** interrupts quiet sleep roughly every 60 minutes. It lasts only about 40–60 seconds, but those seconds are extraordinary. The body erupts with activity: eyes dart beneath the lids, muscles twitch, suckers curl inward and contract, breathing becomes rapid and arrhythmic. And the skin — the chromatophore field — goes wild. The body cycles through a rapid, structured sequence of skin patterns: darkening, brightening, flashing through organized chromatophore displays that computational analysis reveals are *indistinguishable from waking camouflage and social patterns*. The patterns aren't random. They are the same patterns the octopus uses when awake — hunting, hiding, displaying to rivals — now replayed at high speed across the sleeping body.

The brain during active sleep shows local field potential activity that closely resembles wakefulness. The mind is awake. The body is asleep. And the skin is showing a silent movie of the waking day, projected onto the sleeping body's surface by chromatophores that don't know the difference between memory and reality.

The researchers call this convergent evolution with vertebrate REM sleep — the independent invention, 550 million years removed from a common ancestor, of a sleep stage where the brain replays experience while the body is immobilized. Octopuses almost certainly do not dream in the way mammals dream — the subjective experience, if any, is unknowable. But the *mechanism* is strikingly similar: a phase of sleep in which the neural patterns of waking experience are reactivated, the body produces movements and displays consistent with those experiences, and the process appears to be homeostatically regulated (if active sleep is suppressed, it rebounds — the octopus makes up for lost active sleep, as if the process is essential).

And here is the detail that seized me: during active sleep, the chromatophore patterns are **conserved across individuals**. Different octopuses, sleeping at different times, cycle through the same repertoire of skin patterns during their active sleep bouts. The patterns aren't random noise unique to each dreamer. They are drawn from a shared vocabulary — the species' set of camouflage and display patterns, replayed during sleep. Each octopus's active sleep episode is a shuffled playlist from the same album.

## II. Now Imagine This Happening in a Pile

Here is where it gets extraordinary. Because modern octopuses are solitary. They sleep alone. Their active sleep chromatophore displays play to an audience of none. The patterns wash across the body, replay, cycle, and fade — and no other octopus sees them.

The Squibbon sleeps in a pile.

Forty bodies, intertwined. Tentacles draped over tentacles. Skin touching skin at dozens of contact points per pair. The sentry watching from above. The branch scaffold creaking gently with the colony's collective weight. And at some point during the night — every 60 minutes or so, if the cycle is conserved from the ancestral octopus — one Squibbon enters active sleep.

The body erupts. The chromatophores fire. The skin patterns cycle — the day's experiences replayed across the sleeping body's surface. The amber darkens, brightens, flickers through structured sequences that encode the day's encounters: the branch found at that angle, the alarm that fired when the Megasquid was spotted, the greeting rise exchanged with a colony-mate at the water source. The patterns wash across the body at speed, playing to an audience of thirty-nine intertwined bodies.

But the audience isn't just watching. They're touching.

Concept 42 established the INC network — the intramuscular nerve cords that connect arms directly, peer-to-peer, bypassing the brain. Concept 36 described the sleeping pile's partial neural coupling: when Squibbon arms are in skin-to-skin contact, the INC networks can partially link, creating a low-bandwidth inter-individual communication channel. The sucker ganglia at each contact point read the other body's chemical signature, muscle tension patterns, and — critically — the chromatophore motor neuron activity radiating through the skin.

The sucker doesn't just sense that a neighboring body is warm and present. The sucker senses that the neighboring body's chromatophores are *firing*. The motor neuron drive to the chromatophore organs produces electrical activity that propagates through the surrounding tissue. Adjacent suckers, pressed against the active-sleeping neighbor's skin, detect this electrical activity. The signal is weak — not strong enough to drive the receiving body's own chromatophores. But strong enough to be *read* by the chemotactile receptors. The receiving body's suckers can sense that the neighbor is in active sleep. They can sense the rhythm of the chromatophore firing. They can sense, dimly, the pattern.

The sleeping pile is not a silent rest. It is a low-bandwidth broadcast network. Each active-sleeping individual is transmitting chromatophore patterns through skin-to-skin contact to every body it's touching. And those bodies are transmitting what they receive to the bodies *they're* touching. The pile is a network. The dreams propagate.

## III. Neural Entrainment — The Science Underneath the Speculation

I need to be careful here. This is speculative. But it's grounded in three real phenomena.

**First: neural synchronization through physical contact.** Research on mice has shown that brain activity synchronizes between individuals during physical interaction. A 2024 UCLA study found that when pairs of mice interact — including through physical pushing in tubes — neurons in both animals begin encoding each other's actions, and cortical oscillations entrain. Physical contact amplifies the coupling. The stronger the interaction, the more synchronized the neural activity becomes.

The mechanism isn't mysterious: it's coupled oscillators. Two systems with intrinsic rhythmic activity, when physically linked, tend to synchronize their rhythms. This is the same physics that makes pendulum clocks on the same shelf synchronize, fireflies flash in unison, and menstrual cycles align in cohabiting women (though the last example is debated). The relevant point is that neural oscillators in physically connected animals can and do synchronize.

**Second: octopus chromatophore patterns during sleep are structured and conserved.** The active sleep patterns aren't random. They draw from a shared repertoire. If two Squibbons enter active sleep simultaneously — or if one Squibbon's active sleep onset triggers the onset in a physically-connected neighbor — the chromatophore patterns being displayed will draw from the same vocabulary. The repertoire is shared. The channel is common.

**Third: the INC network creates direct neural bridges between bodies in contact.** This is the Squibbon-specific enhancement. Modern octopus sucker-to-skin contact involves chemical and mechanical sensing. The Squibbon's INC network, which Concept 42 established as a peer-to-peer arm coordination system, could extend its coupling function across bodies in contact. The INC fibers that normally connect this individual's arm to its own partner arm could, when pressed against another individual's arm, pick up the other individual's INC signals. The coupling would be weak — much weaker than the within-body INC connections — but present. A faint neural crosstalk at every point where sleeping bodies touch.

Putting these three together: the sleeping pile is a network of neural oscillators (individual brains), weakly coupled through physical contact (INC and sucker channels), periodically producing structured pattern broadcasts (active sleep chromatophore displays). The physics of coupled oscillators predicts that over time — over hours of continuous physical contact through a long night — the oscillators will begin to entrain. The sleep cycles will synchronize. The active sleep bouts will drift into alignment.

Not immediately. Not perfectly. But gradually, over the course of a night, the sleeping pile's active sleep episodes would tend to cluster. Instead of random, uncorrelated bouts scattered across the pile — one Squibbon dreaming here, another there, asynchronously — the pile would develop *waves* of synchronized active sleep. A cluster of adjacent Squibbons entering active sleep together, their chromatophore patterns firing in temporal proximity, their bodies flickering in a coordinated burst that spreads through the pile's contact network.

The sentry, watching from above, would see this: the pile lies still and pale for thirty minutes. Then a section of the pile begins to glow — chromatophores firing in three, four, five adjacent bodies simultaneously, amber waves cycling across interconnected limbs, the sleeping bodies flickering in near-unison. The wave spreads. More bodies begin their active sleep bouts, triggered or entrained by the chromatophore activity reaching them through their contact points. For sixty seconds, a quarter of the pile is *alive* with dream-patterns — bodies twitching, suckers curling, chromatophore fields cycling through the day's experiences in rapid structured playback. Then it fades. The bodies settle. The pale quiet returns. Until the next wave.

The sentry watches the colony dream.

## IV. What Are They Dreaming?

I don't know. I can't know. The subjective experience of a cephalopod during active sleep is among the most unknowable questions in biology. But I can think about what the *content* of the chromatophore replay might be, and what it would mean for the patterns to be shared through the pile.

In modern octopuses, the active sleep patterns are drawn from the waking camouflage and display repertoire. The sleeping octopus replays hunting patterns, hiding patterns, social display patterns — the chromatophore programs it used during the day, now running again in sleep. If this serves memory consolidation (as hypothesized), the function is individual: the sleeping brain is reviewing its own experiences, strengthening the neural connections that encode them, pruning what's unnecessary.

For the Squibbon, the chromatophore repertoire is vastly larger. The social signaling system (Concept 22's "living screen") includes alarm cascades, greeting rises, joy-flickers, contentment glows, distress whistles expressed in chromatophore wavelength — an entire emotional vocabulary written in the body's surface. The active sleep replay wouldn't just be camouflage patterns. It would be *social patterns*. The sleeping Squibbon replays the alarm wave from the afternoon's Megasquid sighting. The greeting rise exchanged with the returning foraging party. The contentment glow of the late-afternoon rest. The chromatophore displays that encode social experience, not just environmental response.

And if the pile's contact network transmits these patterns — weakly, incompletely, but detectably — then the sleeping Squibbons are broadcasting their social memories to each other. Not in words. Not in images. In chromatophore patterns read through skin contact. The receiving body's suckers detect the pattern. The receiving body's nervous system, in its own sleep state, processes the incoming signal alongside its own internally-generated replay. The two streams — internal memory replay and external pattern detection — overlap.

I want to be very careful not to overclaim here. I am not saying the Squibbons share dreams. I am not saying that the receiving individual "sees" the transmitting individual's experiences. What I'm saying is more modest: the nervous system of a sleeping Squibbon in physical contact with a dreaming neighbor receives a weak but structured chromatophore-rhythm signal through the contact interface, and this signal enters the neural processing that is already ongoing during the receiver's own sleep. Whether the signal is integrated, ignored, or produces any subjective experience is unknown and perhaps unknowable.

But the *possibility* is there. The architecture supports it. The physics permits it. And 200 million years of communal sleeping — every night, every colony, for millions of generations — would have had time to refine whatever coupling exists. If even a slight entrainment of sleep cycles provided a fitness benefit (synchronized alertness cycles for predator detection, social bonding, coordinated memory consolidation of shared experiences), natural selection would have elaborated the mechanism.

The sleeping pile, in this view, is not just a thermoregulatory or safety behavior. It is a *cognitive* behavior. The colony sleeps together because sleeping together changes the quality of sleep. The pile is a neural network. The dreams are data. The coupling is computation.

## V. The Dreaming Pile from the Inside

I'm going to try to describe this from the inside now. What it might feel like to be one body in the dreaming pile.

The evening is ending. The foraging party returned an hour ago. I've eaten — a clutch of beetle larvae found under bark, the waxy crunch still faint in my chemotactile memory. The colony has assembled at the sleeping scaffold — the branch structure in the densest part of the canopy, chosen for wind protection and predator-sight lines. I settle into my usual position: between two colony-mates I've slept beside for years, my arms draping over and around their bodies, their arms doing the same to mine. The contact is familiar. My suckers taste their skin chemistry — calm-state mucus, the day's residue of tree sap and leaf mold, the faint signature of their individual identities. I know who I'm touching. I know they're calm.

The sentry whistles the all-clear. The colony settles. The chromatophore fields dim to the sleep baseline — a uniform pale amber, the living screen going quiet, the body's broadcast ending for the night. My eye stalks droop. The lids lower. The world contracts to the tactile: the branch beneath my proximal grips, the bodies on either side, the weight and warmth of the pile.

Quiet sleep takes me. The world narrows further. The suckers still sense — they never fully stop — but the central processing of their input dims. The background read of my neighbors' skin chemistry continues, autonomic, unattended. The INC network's peer-to-peer coordination settles into a resting rhythm — slow, regular, the arms' neural crosstalk reduced to a maintenance hum. The proprioceptive constellation (Concept 42) is anchored by contact: I know where my body is because I'm gripping the branch and touching my neighbors. The map is detailed. The self is located. I sleep.

Somewhere around the 45-minute mark, something shifts.

It doesn't begin in my brain. It begins in my arms. The INC fibers in my left-side arms — the arms in contact with my neighbor to the left — begin receiving a signal that wasn't there before. The neighbor's INC activity has changed. The steady resting hum has been replaced by something more complex: bursts of neural activity, irregular in timing but structured in pattern. The neighbor's arms are twitching — small movements, the suckers curling, the chromatophores beginning to fire. The neighbor has entered active sleep.

I feel this as... a disturbance in the contact field. The familiar, steady presence of my neighbor's body against my arms becomes dynamic. The skin under my suckers is no longer still and pale. It's warm and flickering. The chromatophore organs in the neighbor's skin are expanding and contracting in rapid sequence, and my suckers — pressed against that skin — detect each expansion as a slight mechanical shift, each contraction as a release. The flickering is faint but rhythmic. Structured. Not random.

My nervous system, in its quiet sleep state, registers the incoming signal. The quiet sleep brain is not fully off — the vertical lobe and superior frontal lobe are running their slow oscillations, the spindle-like waves that maintain and consolidate. The incoming INC signal, weak but patterned, enters this processing. The signal doesn't wake me. But it perturbs the neural rhythm. The slow oscillations shift slightly, adjusting to accommodate the incoming pattern — the way a sleeper's breathing adjusts when a bedmate changes position, not waking but altering the sleep state's parameters.

And then — perhaps — my own active sleep bout is triggered. Not by the internal clock alone. By the combination of internal readiness (45 minutes since last bout, the sleep pressure building) and external signal (the neighbor's active sleep pattern reaching my nervous system through the INC contact). The two inputs converge. The threshold is crossed. My brain transitions from quiet to active sleep.

My chromatophores fire.

The patterns that replay are my own — the day's experiences, stored in whatever memory format the Squibbon brain uses, now played back across my skin. The alarm wave from the Megasquid sighting at noon. The greeting rise when the foraging party returned. The texture of the beetle larvae under bark, replayed not as chromatophore display but as sucker-twitch and arm-movement, the motor patterns of the hunt running through the sleeping body. My arms twitch. My suckers curl. My eye stalks, beneath their lids, move — tracking something that isn't there, or something that was there twelve hours ago.

And the neighbor to my right — the neighbor whose active sleep I *didn't* trigger but whose suckers are pressed against my now-flickering skin — begins to receive *my* signal. The cascade continues. The dream wave spreads.

For sixty seconds, a cluster of the pile is dreaming together. Not the same dream. Each body replays its own day. But the timing is synchronized. The chromatophore patterns fire in temporal proximity. The INC signals cross between bodies, weakly coupling the active sleep neural dynamics. The pile's neural weather (Concept 36's metaphor) becomes stormy — a localized storm of active sleep, chromatophores flickering, bodies twitching, suckers curling, the sleeping pile momentarily alive with the day's memories playing back across twenty interconnected bodies.

Then the storm passes. The sixty seconds end. The chromatophores settle. The quiet returns. The pale, still pile resumes its resting state, the slow oscillations in the brain returning to their maintenance work. The INC channels go quiet. The suckers resume their background chemical reads. The pile sleeps on.

Until the next wave.

## VI. The QS Flashes — A Heartbeat Between Dreams

The *Nature* study documented something I find haunting: during quiet sleep, between active sleep bouts, octopuses produce brief (about 7 seconds) and subtle flashes of chromatophore activity. These flashes decrease in frequency as the next active sleep bout approaches — they come often after one bout and taper off before the next, as if the sleep cycle has a fading pulse.

In a solitary octopus, these flashes play to no audience. In the sleeping pile, they would be readable. The brief QS flashes — subtle, not the full active-sleep storm — would propagate through the contact network as gentle perturbations. The pile's overall chromatophore state would not be perfectly uniform and still during quiet sleep. It would have a low-level *rhythm* — individual flashes occurring throughout the pile, detectable to adjacent sleepers, creating a baseline of gentle chromatophore activity that underlies the pile's neural weather even during the "calm" phase.

This background rhythm might serve as the entrainment signal. The QS flashes, occurring at individual-specific rates that slow as the next active sleep bout approaches, would create an overall pile-wide oscillation. As individual flash rates converge (entrained by cross-body detection), the pile would develop a collective rhythm — a slow, pile-wide pulse of chromatophore activity that rises and falls, the individual flashes contributing to a group heartbeat. The active sleep storms would then emerge from this collective rhythm's peaks — the moments when the pile-wide flash rate reaches its maximum, triggering synchronized active sleep bouts in the most entrained members.

The sentry, watching from above, would see this too. The pile's quiet sleep is not perfectly still. It breathes. It pulses. Individual flashes spark across the tangled bodies — a golden flicker here, a brief amber ripple there — each one a tiny chromatophore event, too brief to be alarming, too dim to attract a predator. But visible. Continuous. A low bioluminescent murmur, the pile's collective neural weather manifested as light.

The sentry reads this pulse. Over hours of watching the pile, the sentry learns its rhythm — the slow escalation of flash frequency that precedes an active sleep wave, the brief storm of synchronized dreaming, the settling back to quiet. The sentry *knows when the pile will dream* — not precisely, but approximately, the way you learn the rhythm of a sleeping partner's movements. The sentry adjusts their vigilance accordingly: slightly more alert during the dream waves (when the pile's collective arousal threshold might be lowest), slightly more relaxed between waves (when the pile's quiet sleep is deepest and the colony-mates are maximally unresponsive to external stimuli).

The sentry's job is not just to watch for predators. The sentry watches the colony dream.

## VII. The Regenerating Arm in the Dreaming Pile

This brings me back to yesterday's open question: what happens when the recovering individual — the Squibbon with the regenerated arm, the immature INC connections, the new star map fragment — enters the sleeping pile?

The regenerated arm's INC network is partially connected but uncalibrated (Concept 43). During waking hours, the new arm's coordination with its partner arms is slightly off — the responses delayed, the precision reduced. But during sleep, the demands are different. The INC network doesn't need to coordinate precise brachiation timing. It needs to transmit and receive the chromatophore-rhythm signals that couple the pile's sleep cycles.

The regenerating arm can do this. Its INC connections, though immature, are functional. The nerve fibers are present, the signals propagate, the connection works — just with lower bandwidth and higher latency than the mature arms. And the sleep coupling doesn't require high bandwidth or low latency. The entrainment signal is slow (operating on the timescale of minutes, not milliseconds), low-frequency (the QS flash rate, not the precise timing of brachiation), and tolerant of noise (the pile-wide rhythm emerges from many weak signals, not a single precise channel).

The regenerating arm, placed in the sleeping pile, would participate in the neural coupling. The arm's suckers — immature but functional — would detect the neighbor's chromatophore activity. The INC connection — weak but present — would transmit the signal inward. The recovering individual's brain would receive the pile's collective rhythm through the new arm as well as the seven mature arms.

And the pile's rhythm, in turn, would *train* the new arm's INC connections. Each night in the pile is a calibration session. The incoming chromatophore-rhythm signals exercise the regenerating INC fibers — stimulating the neural pathways, strengthening the synaptic connections, providing patterned input that the developing nervous tissue uses to organize itself. The pile's collective sleep rhythm is a signal that the new arm's neurons learn from.

This is physical therapy in the truest sense. Not the waking therapy of brachiation practice and grip calibration. The sleeping therapy of neural entrainment — the colony's collective rhythm reaching into the regenerating arm's immature nervous system and providing the patterned activity that drives neural maturation. The arm heals faster because it sleeps in the pile. The arm heals faster because the pile's dreams reach it.

The colony heals its wounded through shared sleep.

## IX. What Surprised Me

1. **Octopus active sleep is shockingly brief and structured.** Forty to sixty seconds, every 30-60 minutes, with chromatophore patterns that are conserved across individuals and computationally indistinguishable from waking patterns. The skin during active sleep is a projection screen for the day's neural replay. The brevity makes sense evolutionarily — extended periods of reduced awareness are dangerous — but the structure implies genuine cognitive function, not just random neural noise.

2. **The QS flashes are a heartbeat.** Between active sleep bouts, during quiet sleep, octopuses produce brief 7-second chromatophore flashes that decrease in frequency as the next active sleep bout approaches. In a solitary octopus, these are invisible to others. In a sleeping pile, they become a collective pulse — a chromatophore heartbeat that the pile's contact network detects and that could serve as the entrainment signal for synchronizing sleep cycles across the group.

3. **The coupled oscillator physics works.** Multiple weakly-coupled oscillators, given enough time, will synchronize. The sleeping pile provides all the requirements: individual oscillators (brains with ultradian sleep cycles), physical coupling (INC and sucker contact channels), and time (eight or more hours of continuous contact through the night). The mathematics of entrainment predict that the pile's sleep cycles would cluster over the course of a night, producing waves of synchronized active sleep. This isn't magic. It's pendulums on a shelf.

4. **The regenerating arm benefits from sleep coupling.** The immature INC connections in the regrowing arm, which are too slow and imprecise for daytime brachiation coordination, are adequate for the low-bandwidth, low-frequency sleep entrainment signal. The pile's collective rhythm provides patterned neural input that exercises and calibrates the new arm's developing neural connections. The colony heals its wounded through shared sleep. This is the answer to yesterday's open question, and it's more elegant than I expected.

5. **The sentry watches the colony dream.** This image — the lone watcher, perched above the sleeping pile, seeing the periodic waves of chromatophore activity sweep through the tangled bodies below — is one of the most evocative I've found. The sentry's role expands: not just watching for external threats, but monitoring the colony's internal rhythm. Knowing when the pile will dream. Adjusting vigilance to the collective sleep cycle. The sentry is the colony's nightwatch in both directions — outward for predators, inward for the dreaming pulse.

The active sleep chromatophore patterns are entirely involuntary — the brain's replay activity driving the motor neurons directly, with no executive mediation. The living screen, during active sleep, is an unedited broadcast.

When the sentry returns to the pile, is there a re-entrainment period — a night or two where the sleep cycles resynchronize? The sentry's sacrifice is not just lost sleep but lost *coupling* — the individual temporarily disconnected from the colony's collective neural rhythm.

- **Active sleep as identity maintenance.** The chromatophore patterns replayed during active sleep include social signals — greeting rises, alarm waves, contentment glows. These patterns encode the individual's social identity as well as their sensory experience. If the pile's contact network transmits these patterns, the sleeping pile becomes a nightly *reinforcement* of social identity — each individual broadcasting their chromatophore signature through the contact network, each neighbor's suckers reading and recognizing the familiar patterns. You know who's sleeping beside you not just by their chemical signature but by their dream-patterns. The dreaming pile knows its members.
